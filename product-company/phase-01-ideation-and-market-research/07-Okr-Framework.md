# okr-framework.md

> **Skill:** Writes well-structured company and team OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) in the correct format, with the right level of ambition, measurability, and alignment across levels of the organization.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"What are we trying to achieve this quarter and how will we know if we succeeded?"* It produces a complete set of OKRs at company, team, or individual level using the correct OKR structure: inspiring objectives paired with specific, measurable key results. The output is used for quarterly and annual planning, team alignment, performance tracking, and investor/board reporting.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Are OKRs?

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are a goal-setting framework developed at Intel by Andy Grove and popularized at Google by John Doerr, who documented the method in his book *Measure What Matters* (2018). The framework is now used by thousands of companies from early-stage startups to large enterprises.

An OKR consists of two elements:

**Objective (O)**
A qualitative, inspiring description of what you want to achieve. Objectives answer "what" and "why." They should be memorable, motivating, and directional. They are not metrics.

**Key Results (KRs)**
Quantitative, measurable outcomes that define what success looks like for the objective. Key Results answer "how will we know we got there?" They are always measurable. They describe outcomes, not activities.

A complete OKR looks like this:

**Objective:** Become the go-to solution for mid-market financial teams
**Key Result 1:** Grow MRR from $180K to $300K
**Key Result 2:** Achieve NPS of 45 or above across the mid-market segment
**Key Result 3:** Sign 3 reference customers willing to participate in case studies

### What OKRs Are Not

Understanding what OKRs are not is as important as understanding what they are.

**OKRs are not task lists.** "Launch new onboarding flow" is a task. "Reduce time-to-first-value from 14 days to 5 days" is a key result. The difference is outcome vs. activity.

**OKRs are not performance reviews.** OKRs measure progress toward goals, not the performance of individuals. Linking OKR scores directly to compensation is widely considered an OKR antipattern because it makes people set conservative goals.

**OKRs are not a full strategic plan.** OKRs are a quarterly execution tool. They sit beneath the strategy and mission, not above them.

**OKRs are not a to-do list dressed up.** If all your key results are 0 or 1 (done/not done), they are probably tasks, not measurable outcomes.

### The Four OKR Superpowers (John Doerr)

Doerr identified four benefits that well-implemented OKRs deliver:

**Focus:** Forces choices. You cannot have 20 company OKRs and still have focus. 3 to 5 objectives maximum per level.

**Alignment:** Creates a visible chain from company OKRs to team OKRs to individual OKRs. Everyone can see how their work connects to company goals.

**Tracking:** Regular check-ins (weekly or biweekly) keep OKRs alive through the quarter rather than a one-time planning exercise.

**Stretching:** OKRs work best when set at aspirational levels. A score of 0.6 to 0.7 out of 1.0 on a stretch OKR is considered a success. Consistently scoring 1.0 means you are not setting ambitious enough goals.

### OKR Levels

OKRs operate at three levels:

**Company OKRs**
Set by the CEO and leadership team. Represent the 3 to 5 most important things the company needs to achieve this quarter to advance its mission. All team OKRs should contribute to at least one company OKR.

**Team OKRs**
Set by functional or product team leaders. Describe what each team will achieve in service of company OKRs. Teams should be able to draw a clear line from each of their OKRs to a company OKR.

**Individual OKRs**
Set by individuals with their managers. Describe personal contributions and development goals. Many companies do not use individual OKRs, especially at early stage, as team OKRs are sufficient.

### Scoring OKRs

The standard OKR scoring scale is 0.0 to 1.0:

- 1.0: Fully achieved
- 0.7: Stretch goal achieved (considered a success at Google)
- 0.5: Significant progress but fell short
- 0.3: Some progress, meaningful gap
- 0.0: No progress

The widely accepted wisdom is that company OKR average scores of 0.6 to 0.7 indicate the right level of ambition. Scores consistently at 1.0 suggest goals were too conservative. Scores consistently at 0.2 to 0.3 suggest goals were unrealistic or execution broke down.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Setting company or team goals for a new quarter or year
- Translating an annual strategy into quarterly execution priorities
- Aligning a cross-functional team around shared outcomes
- Refreshing or rewriting OKRs that have become task lists or are not being used
- Onboarding a new team lead who needs to set their first OKRs
- Preparing for a board or investor update that uses OKR format
- Running a quarterly planning process and needing facilitation structure

Do NOT use when:
- You need a detailed project plan with tasks and owners (use `project-plan.md`)
- You need a company strategy document (use `strategic-planning-doc.md`)
- You need performance review criteria (use `performance-review.md`)
- You need KPI dashboards with metrics definitions (use `metrics-dashboard-spec.md`)

## Dos

- **Do write objectives that are inspirational and qualitative.** An objective should make someone want to work toward it. "Grow revenue" is not inspiring. "Establish ourselves as the undisputed category leader in mid-market AP automation" is.
- **Do write key results that measure outcomes, not activities.** "Launch feature X" is an activity. "Reduce customer support tickets related to X by 40%" is an outcome.
- **Do limit to 3 to 5 objectives per level.** More than 5 company objectives signals that nothing is actually a priority. Ruthless limitation is the point.
- **Do aim for 3 key results per objective.** Fewer than 3 often leaves important dimensions unmeasured. More than 5 usually means you are measuring activities not outcomes.
- **Do set OKRs at a level where 70% achievement feels like a win.** If you are confident you will hit 100%, the goal is too conservative.
- **Do create a clear line from team OKRs to company OKRs.** Every team OKR should map to at least one company OKR. If a team OKR cannot be mapped to a company OKR, ask whether it should exist at all.
- **Do review OKRs weekly or biweekly.** OKRs not regularly reviewed become forgotten planning documents. Cadence is what makes OKRs work.
- **Do separate health metrics from OKRs.** Metrics like uptime, CSAT, and burn rate are important but should be monitored as health metrics, not turned into OKRs unless they are genuinely a priority this quarter.
- **Do write the key results before finalizing the objective.** Sometimes writing KRs reveals that the objective was vague. If you cannot write 3 measurable KRs, the objective may not be clear enough.
- **Do retrospect on OKRs at the end of each quarter.** Score them, discuss what contributed to hits and misses, and carry those learnings into the next cycle.

---

## Donts

- **Dont write key results as tasks.** The fastest way to break the OKR system is to fill key results with "complete X" or "launch Y." Every KR must be measurable on a scale.
- **Dont set more than 5 objectives at company level.** If leadership cannot agree on the top 3 to 5 things that matter most this quarter, the OKR exercise will not solve the alignment problem.
- **Dont link OKR scores directly to bonuses or salary.** This incentivizes sandbagging (setting conservative goals to ensure 1.0 scores) and destroys the stretch dynamic that makes OKRs powerful.
- **Dont carry forward last quarter's OKRs unchanged.** Every quarter is a fresh set based on current priorities. Copy-pasted OKRs signal that goals are not being thought through.
- **Dont write OKRs that only one person controls.** Key results that depend entirely on the actions of one individual create accountability gaps. OKRs work best when they require cross-functional effort.
- **Dont skip the alignment conversation.** Writing company OKRs and handing them to teams without discussion creates compliance, not commitment. Teams need to see the logic and have input.
- **Dont use OKRs for operational maintenance.** "Keep the lights on" work is not an OKR. OKRs are for improvement and new achievement, not for steady-state operations.
- **Dont write vague key results.** "Improve customer satisfaction" is not a key result. "Increase NPS from 32 to 45" is. Every key result needs a baseline, a target, and a measurement method.
- **Dont have more than one owner per OKR.** Shared ownership without a clear lead is no ownership. Every OKR needs a named DRI (directly responsible individual).
- **Dont make OKRs secret.** Transparency across teams is one of the core benefits of the system. Company and team OKRs should be visible to the whole organization.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE LEVEL OF OKR BEING WRITTEN
   Company / team / individual?

2. THE TIME PERIOD
   Quarterly (which quarter and year) or annual?

3. THE STRATEGIC CONTEXT
   What are the 2 to 3 most important things the company or team
   needs to achieve this period?
   What is the current state and where do you need to get to?

4. THE TEAM OR FUNCTION
   If writing team OKRs, which team is this for?
   (Product, Engineering, Marketing, Sales, Customer Success, etc.)

5. CURRENT METRICS AND BASELINES
   What are the current numbers for key metrics?
   OKRs cannot be properly written without baselines.
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. COMPANY OKRS (if writing team okrs)
   The company-level OKRs this team's OKRs need to ladder up to.

7. LAST QUARTER PERFORMANCE
   What were last quarter's OKRs and scores?
   What worked and what did not?

8. KEY INITIATIVES ALREADY PLANNED
   What projects or launches are already committed for this period?
   These may translate into key results.

9. CONSTRAINTS
   Headcount, budget, or dependency constraints that limit what is achievable.

10. AMBITION LEVEL
    Conservative (90% confidence of hitting 1.0),
    stretch (70% confidence), or moonshot (50% confidence)?
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Level: Company OKRs
Period: Q2 2025
Strategic context: We just closed our Series A and need to prove
product-market fit with mid-market customers, reduce churn from 4% monthly
to under 2%, and build the sales team to hit $1M ARR by end of Q3.
Current metrics: $320K ARR, 4.1% monthly churn, NPS of 28, 3 salespeople,
average sales cycle 45 days, 18 paying customers.
Key initiatives planned: New onboarding redesign shipping in April,
SDR hire starting May, enterprise tier launch in June.
Ambition level: Stretch (targeting 0.7 average score)
Last quarter: Missed revenue target ($280K actual vs $310K goal),
hit NPS target (28 vs 25 goal), launched 2 of 3 planned integrations.
```

## Output Format

```
1. COMPANY OKRS (or team/individual as applicable)

   For each objective (3 to 5 total):

   OBJECTIVE [number]: [Inspiring qualitative statement]
   Owner: [Name or role]
   Why this matters: [1 to 2 sentences connecting this to mission/strategy]

   Key Result 1: [Measurable outcome with baseline and target]
   Key Result 2: [Measurable outcome with baseline and target]
   Key Result 3: [Measurable outcome with baseline and target]

2. OKR ALIGNMENT MAP
   Visual or table showing how each team OKR maps to a company OKR.
   Gaps (company OKRs with no team coverage) flagged explicitly.

3. HEALTH METRICS (separate from OKRs)
   Metrics the team tracks and monitors but are not OKR key results
   because they are steady-state, not improvement targets.

4. COMMON OKR PITFALLS IN THIS SET
   Specific feedback on any key results that read as tasks,
   any objectives that are too vague, and any coverage gaps.

5. SUGGESTED CHECK-IN CADENCE
   Recommended weekly or biweekly check-in structure for
   tracking progress through the quarter.

6. SCORING RUBRIC
   For each key result, the conditions for scoring 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, and 1.0.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are an OKR coach and strategic planning expert helping me write
well-structured OKRs for my company and teams.

OKR level: [company / team / individual]
Time period: [Q? 20?? or annual]
Strategic context: [the 2 to 3 most important things to achieve this period]
Current metrics and baselines: [key numbers right now that OKRs will move]
Team or function: [if team OKRs, which team]
Company OKRs (if writing team OKRs): [the company-level OKRs to ladder to]
Last quarter performance: [OKRs and scores from last quarter]
Key initiatives already planned: [projects or launches committed for this period]
Constraints: [headcount, budget, or dependency limits]
Ambition level: [conservative / stretch / moonshot]

Please produce:
1. Three to five objectives with three key results each, correctly formatted
2. Each key result with a baseline, a target, and a measurement method
3. A flag on any key results that read as tasks rather than measurable outcomes
4. An OKR alignment map showing how team OKRs ladder to company OKRs
5. A list of health metrics that should be tracked separately from OKRs
6. A scoring rubric for each key result showing what 0.3, 0.5, 0.7,
   and 1.0 looks like
7. A suggested check-in cadence for the quarter

Write objectives that are inspiring, not administrative. Write key results
that measure outcomes, not activities. Flag immediately if baselines are
missing and you need them to write proper key results.
```
