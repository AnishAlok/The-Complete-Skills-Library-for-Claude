# trend-signal-analysis.md

> **Skill:** Detects weak signals of emerging change and structures horizon scanning reports to help leaders anticipate market shifts before they become obvious.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"What is changing in the world that we should be paying attention to right now, before everyone else does?"* It produces a structured trend and signal analysis that separates noise from genuine early indicators of change across technology, behavior, regulation, and market structure. The output is used to inform product strategy, investment theses, roadmap bets, and competitive positioning before trends become mainstream.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is Trend Signal Analysis?

Trend signal analysis is the practice of systematically scanning for early indicators of change that could materially affect your business, market, or customers. It is distinct from reading industry news, which mostly reports on things that have already happened. Signal analysis is about identifying what is just beginning to emerge.

The most strategically valuable signals are weak ones. By the time a trend is obvious, it is already priced into competitor roadmaps, investor theses, and customer expectations. The teams that act on weak signals early build durable advantages.

### Weak Signals vs. Strong Signals

**Weak signals** are early, ambiguous, fragmented indicators of change. They often come from the edges of a market, from unusual customer behavior, from academic research not yet commercialized, or from adjacent industries experiencing a shift before yours does.

Characteristics of weak signals:
- Appear in small, unexpected places
- Are easy to dismiss as noise or anomaly
- Are contradicted by the mainstream view
- Require interpretation and pattern recognition to surface

**Strong signals** are clear, widely recognized indicators of an established trend.

Characteristics of strong signals:
- Covered extensively in mainstream business press
- Already incorporated into analyst forecasts
- Discussed openly by incumbent competitors
- Visible in customer RFPs and procurement criteria

The strategic window for acting on strong signals is nearly closed by the time they become strong. Weak signals are where the opportunity lives.

### Horizon Scanning

Horizon scanning is the structured practice of looking across three time horizons for signals of change:

**Horizon 1: 0 to 2 years**
Changes that are already underway and will materially affect you in the near term. Strong signals. Require operational response now.

**Horizon 2: 2 to 5 years**
Trends that are emerging but not yet mainstream. Mix of weak and strengthening signals. Require strategic positioning and investment decisions today.

**Horizon 3: 5 to 10 years**
Distant, speculative, but structurally important shifts. Weak signals and futures scenarios. Require exploratory thinking and option-keeping rather than commitment.

### Signal Categories

A complete horizon scan covers signals across six categories:

**Technology signals**
New capabilities becoming technically feasible or economically viable. Examples: a new foundation model capability, a drop in sensor costs, browser API changes.

**Behavioral signals**
Shifts in how customers, employees, or society at large are acting. Examples: rising comfort with AI-generated content, changing privacy expectations, remote-first work normalization.

**Regulatory and policy signals**
Legislative activity, enforcement patterns, government investment priorities. Examples: EU AI Act implementation, data residency requirements, carbon pricing expansion.

**Economic and market structure signals**
Changes in capital flows, pricing dynamics, consolidation or fragmentation patterns. Examples: rising CAC across a category, private equity rollup activity, VC pulling back from a sector.

**Social and demographic signals**
Generational shifts, workforce changes, values evolution. Examples: Gen Z entering the workforce as buyers, urbanization patterns, mental health becoming a mainstream workplace topic.

**Adjacent industry signals**
What is happening in industries 2 to 3 steps removed from yours that tends to reach your industry next. Examples: a workflow automation pattern that spread from finance to HR is now approaching legal.

### The STEEP Framework

A common structuring tool for horizon scanning. STEEP stands for:
- **S**ocial
- **T**echnological
- **E**conomic
- **E**nvironmental
- **P**olitical/Regulatory

Running a STEEP scan ensures you do not anchor only on the signal categories most familiar to your team.

### Signal Scoring

Not all signals deserve equal attention. Score each signal you identify across three dimensions:

**Impact potential (1 to 5)**
If this signal becomes a full trend, how much would it change your market, customer behavior, or competitive landscape?

**Time to impact (1 to 5)**
How quickly could this signal materially affect your business? Faster = higher urgency score.

**Signal strength (1 to 5)**
How much evidence exists for this signal right now? Weak and fragmented = low score but potentially high value. Strong and confirmed = high score but lower strategic window.

Signals with high impact, moderate to fast time-to-impact, and currently low signal strength are your highest-value targets. These are the ones competitors have not yet acted on.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Running annual or quarterly strategic planning and needing to surface what is changing externally
- Preparing a board presentation on market dynamics and future risks
- Developing a multi-year product roadmap and needing to make bets on where the market is going
- Writing an investment thesis that needs to articulate why now is the right timing
- Evaluating whether to enter a new market or category
- Briefing a leadership team or new executive on emerging forces shaping the business
- Responding to a board or investor question about external threats and opportunities
- Building a research or insights practice that regularly informs strategy

Do NOT use when:
- You need a current-state competitor analysis (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)
- You need customer-level insight on present-day needs (use `customer-discovery-interview.md`)
- You need to size a specific known market (use `market-opportunity-analysis.md`)

## Dos

- **Do cast a wide net first, then filter.** Start by collecting signals from diverse and unexpected sources before applying strategic relevance filters. Premature filtering kills weak signal detection.
- **Do look at the edges of your market.** The most interesting signals come from early adopters, fringe customers, academic researchers, and adjacent industries, not from your core customer base.
- **Do separate observation from interpretation.** First describe what you are seeing (the signal), then separately analyze what it might mean (the implication). Conflating them leads to confirmation bias.
- **Do track signal velocity.** A signal that was mentioned in 3 places six months ago and is now in 30 places is accelerating. Velocity matters as much as current volume.
- **Do use diverse source types.** Combine academic preprints, patent filings, niche trade publications, startup funding data, job posting trends, and conference agendas. Do not rely only on mainstream tech press.
- **Do invite dissent.** The value of weak signal analysis depends on surfacing uncomfortable or contrarian signals, not just confirming what the team already believes.
- **Do assign owners to signals.** Each signal someone commits to monitoring should have a named owner who tracks it over time.
- **Do connect signals to strategic implications.** A list of interesting observations is not a strategy input. Every signal should end with "and therefore we should consider..."
- **Do revisit signals quarterly.** Signal strength changes. A weak signal from six months ago may now be strong. A signal that seemed important may have fizzled.
- **Do use scenarios to stress-test signals.** For your highest-impact signals, build a short scenario: what does the world look like in 3 years if this signal becomes a dominant trend?

---

## Donts

- **Dont only scan sources you already read.** Familiar sources produce familiar signals. Weak signal detection requires going outside your information comfort zone.
- **Dont dismiss signals because they seem small or unlikely.** That is literally what makes them weak signals. The point is to take them seriously before they are obvious.
- **Dont build a list of trends without strategic implication.** A "top 10 trends" list with no "so what" is intellectual entertainment, not strategy input.
- **Dont confuse hype with signal.** A topic being widely discussed on LinkedIn or at conferences is a strong signal of conversation volume, not necessarily of real-world change. Separate media hype from actual adoption or behavioral change.
- **Dont anchor on Horizon 1 only.** Most teams naturally focus on near-term changes because they feel more actionable. Horizon 3 thinking is where competitive separation is built.
- **Dont make this a once-a-year exercise.** The signal environment changes continuously. Build a lightweight ongoing scanning habit with a formal quarterly synthesis.
- **Dont assign this only to a research team.** The best signal detectors are the people closest to customers, developers, and technical edges. Frontline teams should be contributing signals, not just receiving the output.
- **Dont present signals without confidence levels.** Every signal should be tagged with its current strength (weak / emerging / established) so readers know how much weight to place on it.
- **Dont skip adjacent industries.** Many of the most important signals for your business will appear first in adjacent sectors. Financial services technology trends often precede HR technology trends. Healthcare digitization patterns often precede education digitization.
- **Dont let the exercise become purely defensive.** Horizon scanning should surface offensive opportunities as much as threats.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. YOUR INDUSTRY AND MARKET
   What market or category are you operating in?
   Who are your customers and what problem are you solving?

2. STRATEGIC QUESTIONS YOU ARE TRYING TO ANSWER
   What decisions is this analysis meant to inform?
   Examples: "Should we invest in an AI-native product line?"
   "Is our core market at risk of disruption in the next 3 years?"
   "Where should we place our biggest roadmap bet for 2027?"

3. TIME HORIZON FOCUS
   Are you most interested in Horizon 1 (0 to 2 years),
   Horizon 2 (2 to 5 years), Horizon 3 (5 to 10 years), or all three?

4. SIGNAL CATEGORIES TO PRIORITIZE
   Which of the six categories matter most for this analysis:
   technology, behavioral, regulatory, economic/market structure,
   social/demographic, adjacent industry?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
5. SIGNALS YOU HAVE ALREADY NOTICED
   Any early observations or patterns you have already spotted
   that you want included and analyzed.

6. STRATEGIC BETS ALREADY ON THE TABLE
   Decisions or investments already being considered that
   this analysis should inform or stress-test.

7. ADJACENT INDUSTRIES TO SCAN
   Specific industries you believe are ahead of yours in
   relevant ways worth scanning.

8. SOURCES YOU TRUST
   Specific publications, researchers, or communities whose
   signal quality you value.

9. AUDIENCE FOR THE OUTPUT
   Is this for a board presentation, internal strategy team,
   investor memo, or product planning session?
   This shapes depth, format, and tone.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Industry: B2B HR tech, specifically employee performance management SaaS
Strategic question: Where should we place our biggest product bet for 2026 to 2028?
Time horizon: Primarily Horizon 2 (2 to 5 years) with some Horizon 3
Signal categories: Technology (AI/ML), behavioral (how managers and employees are changing),
regulatory (labor law, pay transparency), adjacent industry (productivity tools, edtech)
Signals already noticed: Managers increasingly asking for AI coaching suggestions,
Gen Z employees pushing back on traditional annual review cycles
Strategic bets on table: Build AI coaching layer vs. double down on integrations vs. expand to frontline workers
Audience: CPO and executive team strategy offsite
```

## Output Format

```
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
   The 3 to 5 most important signals and their combined strategic implication
   in plain language. Written for a time-constrained executive.

2. SCANNING METHODOLOGY
   Which horizons were scanned, which signal categories were covered,
   and what source types were used. Establishes credibility and scope.

3. SIGNAL INVENTORY
   All signals identified, organized by category and horizon.
   For each signal:
   - Signal description (what is being observed)
   - Evidence (where this signal was detected and how strong)
   - Signal strength rating: Weak / Emerging / Established
   - Impact potential score (1 to 5)
   - Time to impact score (1 to 5)
   - Strategic implication (what this means for your business)

4. PRIORITY SIGNALS (top 5 to 8)
   The highest-scoring signals given full narrative treatment:
   - What is happening
   - Evidence base
   - Why it matters specifically to your business
   - Scenario: what does this look like if it becomes a dominant trend
   - Recommended response: monitor / prepare / act now

5. HORIZON MAP
   Visual or structured summary placing priority signals on a
   Horizon 1 / 2 / 3 timeline.

6. STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS
   Synthesized view of what the signal set means for:
   - Product roadmap and bets
   - Competitive positioning
   - GTM and customer targeting
   - Investment and resourcing priorities

7. WATCHING LIST
   Lower-priority signals worth monitoring but not acting on yet.
   Each with a trigger condition: "Escalate this signal if..."

8. SOURCES LOG
   All sources used with date of access.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a strategic foresight analyst helping me build a trend and weak signal analysis
for my business.

My industry and market: [describe your market and customers]
Strategic questions this analysis should answer: [what decisions is this informing]
Time horizon focus: [Horizon 1 / 2 / 3 or all three]
Signal categories to prioritize: [technology / behavioral / regulatory / economic /
social / adjacent industry]
Signals I have already noticed: [any early observations you have spotted]
Strategic bets already on the table: [decisions being considered that this should inform]
Adjacent industries to scan: [industries ahead of yours in relevant ways]
Audience: [board / CPO / product team / investor memo]

Please produce:
1. A signal inventory covering all six STEEP categories across my chosen horizons
2. Top 5 to 8 priority signals with full narrative treatment, evidence base,
   scenario if they become dominant, and recommended response
3. A horizon map showing where each priority signal sits in time
4. Strategic implications for product, positioning, and investment
5. A watching list of lower-priority signals with escalation triggers
6. All sources cited with access dates

Prioritize signals that are currently weak or emerging over those already mainstream.
Flag any signals that contradict the prevailing view in my industry.
Be specific about what evidence supports each signal. Do not include signals
that are just widely discussed opinions with no behavioral or data evidence behind them.
```
