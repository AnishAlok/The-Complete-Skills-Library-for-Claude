# strategic-planning-doc.md

> **Skill:** Produces a structured annual or quarterly strategic planning document that translates vision and market context into clear priorities, bets, resource allocation, and success metrics for leadership alignment.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"What are we doing this year, why, and how will we know if it worked?"* It produces a complete strategic planning document that captures market context, strategic priorities, resource allocation decisions, key bets, risks, and success metrics. Used for annual planning cycles, board presentations, executive team alignment, and quarterly strategy reviews.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is a Strategic Planning Document?

A strategic planning document is the written output of a strategic planning process. It translates a company's vision and mission into concrete priorities, resource decisions, and measurable outcomes for a defined time period, typically one year with quarterly milestones.

It is different from an OKR document (which defines specific measurable goals) and different from a roadmap (which defines what will be built). A strategic plan is the narrative and logic layer that explains why you are making the choices you are making, what you are choosing not to do, and how you will allocate resources in service of the priorities you have identified.

A well-written strategic plan answers these questions:
- Where does the market stand and where is it going?
- What are our strengths, weaknesses, and the critical assumptions behind our strategy?
- What are the 3 to 5 strategic priorities for this period?
- What are we deliberately choosing not to do?
- How are we allocating headcount, budget, and leadership attention?
- What are the key bets we are making and what has to be true for them to work?
- What does success look like at 12 months and what are the interim checkpoints?

### The Strategic Planning Cycle

Strategic planning works best as an annual cycle with quarterly reviews:

**Q4 (Planning):** Annual strategy development. Review last year, assess market, set priorities for next year, allocate resources.

**Q1 (Launch):** Communicate the plan, cascade into team OKRs and roadmaps, begin execution.

**Q2 (Review):** Mid-year review. Are we on track? Has anything changed in the market that requires a pivot?

**Q3 (Adjust):** Refine execution based on Q2 learnings. Begin thinking about next year's planning.

**Q4 (Retrospect and Plan):** Close out the year, score performance, and start the next cycle.

### Strategy vs. Tactics

One of the most common failures of strategic planning documents is the slide into tactical detail. A strategic plan should answer "what" and "why" at a high level. The "how" belongs in project plans, product roadmaps, and OKRs.

**Strategic level:** "We will focus our go-to-market exclusively on mid-market manufacturing companies in North America in 2025 and not attempt to move upmarket or expand geographically."

**Tactical level:** "We will hire two enterprise AEs, run a targeted LinkedIn campaign, and attend three industry conferences."

Both are necessary. Only the first belongs in a strategic planning document.

### The Role of Trade-offs

The most important signal of a genuine strategy is what it says no to. A document that lists every possible initiative as a priority is not a strategy. It is a wishlist.

Every strong strategic plan explicitly names:
- The customer segments you are prioritizing and the ones you are explicitly deprioritizing
- The product bets you are making and the ones you are deferring
- The markets you are entering and the ones you are not yet entering
- The capabilities you are building and the ones you are outsourcing or skipping

When a team cannot name what they are choosing not to do, they do not have a strategy. They have a list.

### The Three Strategy Questions (Roger Martin)

Roger Martin's "Playing to Win" framework identifies five cascading questions every strategy must answer:

1. What is our winning aspiration? (mission/vision)
2. Where will we play? (markets, customers, geographies, categories)
3. How will we win? (differentiation, capabilities, competitive advantage)
4. What capabilities must be in place? (what we must be excellent at)
5. What management systems are required? (structure, processes, measures)

A strategic planning document that cannot answer all five clearly has gaps that will manifest as execution failures.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Running an annual planning process and needing to produce the strategy document
- Preparing a board presentation on the company's strategic direction for the coming year
- Aligning a leadership team that is pulling in different directions on priorities
- Onboarding a new executive who needs to understand and internalize the strategy
- Reviewing strategy at mid-year to determine if adjustments are needed
- Preparing for a fundraising conversation where investors will ask about strategic direction
- Documenting the rationale for a major resource allocation decision

Do NOT use when:
- You need specific measurable goals for the quarter (use `okr-framework.md`)
- You need a product roadmap (use `product-roadmap.md`)
- You need a go-to-market plan (use `gtm-strategy.md`)
- You need an investor pitch deck (use `pitch-deck.md`)

## Dos

- **Do anchor the strategy in an honest market assessment.** Strategy built on optimistic assumptions about the market or your current position fails fast. Start with a clear-eyed view of where you are.
- **Do name the 3 to 5 priorities explicitly and force-rank them.** If everything is a priority, nothing is. The document must make choices visible.
- **Do state what you are choosing not to do.** Explicit non-priorities are as strategically important as priorities.
- **Do include the key assumptions behind each strategic bet.** What has to be true for this strategy to work? Naming assumptions makes them testable.
- **Do separate the 12-month plan from the 3-year direction.** The annual plan should be specific and committal. The 3-year view can be more directional.
- **Do include resource allocation.** A strategy without headcount and budget allocation is a wish. Show where leadership attention, people, and money are going.
- **Do define success metrics for the year.** By December, what will we have achieved? These are the yardsticks for the annual retrospective.
- **Do involve the leadership team in building it.** A strategy written by the CEO alone and handed to a team creates compliance, not commitment. Co-creation creates ownership.
- **Do write it so that a new employee reading it six months later understands the logic.** If the document relies on verbal context to make sense, rewrite it.
- **Do review it quarterly.** A strategy reviewed only once at the end of the year is not being used as a management tool.

---

## Donts

- **Dont fill the document with last year's slides with updated dates.** Strategy should be freshly reasoned each cycle based on current market reality.
- **Dont present strategy as a list of initiatives.** A list of things you are doing is not a strategy. The logic connecting them to a competitive outcome is the strategy.
- **Dont skip the external context section.** Strategy made without a view of market dynamics, competitive shifts, and macro environment is inside-out thinking.
- **Dont use aspirational language without commitment.** "We will explore opportunities in X" is not a strategic priority. It is a hedge. Either commit or explicitly deprioritize.
- **Dont write strategy at a level of specificity that makes it a project plan.** Strategic planning documents should be 8 to 15 pages, not 40.
- **Dont ignore resource constraints.** An ambitious strategy that does not account for headcount, budget, or capability gaps is fantasy planning.
- **Dont let the process take longer than 6 to 8 weeks.** Annual planning cycles that run for six months consume the year they are supposed to plan.
- **Dont create the document and then file it.** The strategic plan should be referenced in every leadership team meeting, every OKR cycle, and every major resource decision.
- **Dont make it too polished too early.** The first draft should surface disagreements and gaps. Premature polish hides the real tensions that strategy needs to resolve.
- **Dont forget to document the decisions that were considered and rejected.** Future team members and leaders need to understand why certain paths were not taken.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. COMPANY STAGE AND CONTEXT
   How old is the company? What is current ARR/revenue?
   How many employees? What is the funding situation?

2. LAST YEAR PERFORMANCE
   What were the key goals last year and how did you perform against them?
   What worked and what did not?

3. MARKET CONTEXT
   What is happening in your market and competitive environment
   that is relevant to planning?

4. CURRENT STRENGTHS AND GAPS
   What does the company do well today?
   Where are the critical capability or performance gaps?

5. THE PLANNING PERIOD
   Annual (which year) or quarterly (which quarter)?
   Is this a fresh plan or a refresh?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. LEADERSHIP TEAM INPUTS
   What are the 2 to 3 most important things each function leader
   believes should be a priority this year?

7. CUSTOMER FEEDBACK THEMES
   What are customers consistently asking for or complaining about?

8. INVESTOR OR BOARD DIRECTION
   Any explicit guidance or expectations from investors or the board
   that must be reflected in the strategy.

9. COMPETITIVE DEVELOPMENTS
   Any significant moves by competitors in the past 6 to 12 months
   that need to inform the strategic response.

10. RESOURCE CONSTRAINTS
    Headcount plan, budget envelope, or capability constraints
    that bound what is realistically achievable.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Company: B2B SaaS, project management for AEC (architecture, engineering, construction) firms
Stage: Series A, $2.4M ARR, 28 employees, 18 months runway
Last year: Hit ARR goal ($2.4M vs $2M target), missed NPS goal (31 vs 40),
launched mobile app 3 months late, lost 2 large enterprise deals to Procore
Market context: Procore expanding into mid-market, two new well-funded entrants (Buildots, Joist),
AI-based site monitoring emerging as a new category
Strengths: Deep integrations with BIM tools, high retention in mid-market (94% NRR), strong CS team
Gaps: No mobile-first experience, weak brand awareness, no AI capabilities, thin sales team
Board direction: Prove path to $10M ARR before next raise, do not expand to new verticals yet
Planning period: Annual 2025
Resource constraints: Can hire 6 people in 2025, $800K marketing budget
```

## Output Format

```
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (1 page)
   The three most important strategic choices for the year and why.
   What success looks like at year end.
   Written for a board member or new executive to read in 3 minutes.

2. MARKET AND COMPETITIVE CONTEXT (1 to 2 pages)
   Where the market stands today and where it is heading.
   Key competitive developments and their implications.
   The macro tailwinds and headwinds affecting the plan.

3. CURRENT STATE ASSESSMENT (1 page)
   Honest view of strengths and weaknesses.
   Where the company is performing well and where the gaps are.
   The key lessons from last year's performance.

4. STRATEGIC PRIORITIES (2 to 3 pages)
   The 3 to 5 priorities for the planning period, force-ranked.
   For each priority:
   - What it is and why it matters
   - What success looks like
   - The key bets or assumptions behind it
   - The approximate resource allocation (people and budget)

5. WHAT WE ARE NOT DOING (half page)
   Explicit list of things that were considered and deliberately deprioritized.
   With brief rationale for each deferral.

6. RESOURCE ALLOCATION SUMMARY (1 page)
   How headcount growth, budget, and leadership attention
   are distributed across the strategic priorities.

7. KEY RISKS AND MITIGATIONS (1 page)
   The 4 to 6 biggest risks to the plan.
   For each: the risk, the trigger condition, and the mitigation or contingency.

8. SUCCESS METRICS AND CHECKPOINTS (half page)
   The 5 to 7 metrics that will determine if the year was successful.
   With Q1, Q2, Q3 checkpoint targets to enable mid-year course correction.

9. OPEN QUESTIONS AND DECISIONS PENDING (half page)
   Strategic questions not yet resolved that will need to be answered
   in Q1 for the plan to be fully executable.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a strategic planning facilitator and business strategist helping me
produce a complete annual strategic planning document for my company.

Company stage and context: [stage, ARR, headcount, funding]
Last year performance: [goals, actuals, key hits and misses]
Market context: [what is happening in the market and competitive environment]
Current strengths: [what we do well]
Current gaps: [where we fall short]
Board or investor direction: [any explicit guidance or constraints]
Planning period: [annual 20?? or Q? 20??]
Resource constraints: [headcount budget, cost budget, capability limits]
Leadership inputs: [what each function believes should be a priority]
Customer feedback themes: [what customers are saying]

Please produce a complete strategic planning document with:
1. A one-page executive summary of the three most important strategic choices
2. Market and competitive context with key implications
3. An honest current state assessment
4. Three to five force-ranked strategic priorities with resource allocation
5. An explicit list of what we are not doing this year and why
6. A resource allocation summary across priorities
7. A risk analysis with mitigations
8. Success metrics with Q1, Q2, Q3 checkpoint targets
9. Open questions that must be resolved in Q1

Be direct. If the strategic priorities conflict with the resource constraints,
flag the gap. If the plan requires assumptions that seem fragile, name them.
If what we are planning not to do seems like a mistake, challenge it.
A good strategic plan makes hard choices visible, not comfortable ones invisible.
```
