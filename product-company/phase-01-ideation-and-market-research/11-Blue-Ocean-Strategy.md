# blue-ocean-strategy.md

> **Skill:** Applies the Blue Ocean Strategy framework to identify uncontested market space, reconstruct market boundaries, and create a strategy canvas that maps a value innovation path away from direct competition.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"How do we stop competing in an overcrowded market and create a space where competition is irrelevant?"* It applies W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne's Blue Ocean Strategy framework to produce a strategy canvas, a four actions framework analysis (eliminate, reduce, raise, create), and a value innovation roadmap. Used for product strategy, market positioning, new venture design, and strategic planning when a market is commoditizing or overcrowded.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is Blue Ocean Strategy?

Blue Ocean Strategy is a framework developed by W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne of INSEAD, introduced in their 2005 book *Blue Ocean Strategy* and updated in *Blue Ocean Shift* (2017). The framework argues that lasting competitive success comes not from fighting over existing demand in overcrowded markets (red oceans) but from creating new market space (blue oceans) that makes competition irrelevant.

**Red Oceans** are existing industries with defined boundaries and accepted competitive rules. Companies fight for a larger share of existing demand. As more players crowd in, profits and growth decline. The ocean turns red from the fighting.

**Blue Oceans** are new, uncontested market spaces created by companies that redefine industry boundaries. Demand is created rather than fought over. Growth is highly profitable because no one else is in the same space yet.

The core insight is that value and cost are not a trade-off. Most companies accept that you either deliver more value at higher cost or deliver acceptable value at lower cost. Blue Ocean thinking challenges this. Value innovation, the simultaneous pursuit of differentiation and low cost, is what creates a blue ocean.

### The Strategy Canvas

The Strategy Canvas is the central diagnostic and action tool of Blue Ocean Strategy. It serves two purposes:

1. It captures the current state of play in a known market space, showing where competitors invest, what factors they compete on, and what buyers receive.
2. It reveals how a blue ocean strategy is different by showing a value curve that diverges sharply from the industry's standard profile.

The horizontal axis lists the factors the industry competes on and invests in. The vertical axis shows the offering level that buyers receive across each factor (from low to high). Each competitor or strategic group has a value curve that maps their offering level across all factors.

A blue ocean strategy produces a value curve that:
- Diverges from the industry's standard profile
- Has focus (not high on every factor, but high on the ones that matter)
- Has a compelling tagline that captures the new offering in a memorable way

### The Four Actions Framework (ERRC Grid)

The Four Actions Framework is used to reconstruct buyer value to create a new value curve. It asks four questions:

**Eliminate**
Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?
These are factors that have long been competed on but deliver no real value to buyers, or whose value has been eroded by changing buyer expectations.

**Reduce**
Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
These are areas where products have been over-engineered or over-delivered relative to what buyers actually value. Reducing these frees up cost.

**Raise**
Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
These are the dimensions where the industry has underserved buyers, forcing compromises buyers should not have to make.

**Create**
Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?
These are entirely new sources of value, often discovered by looking outside the industry at what buyers do when they cannot find the value they need within the current industry.

The ERRC Grid produces a new value curve by showing what to remove, reduce, raise, and create. It also reveals the cost structure impact: eliminating and reducing frees up cost; raising and creating invests cost where it matters most to new buyers.

### Six Paths for Reconstructing Market Boundaries

Kim and Mauborgne identified six analytical paths for discovering blue ocean opportunities:

**Path 1: Look across substitute industries**
What industries do buyers consider as alternatives to your industry? What factors make buyers switch between them? Creating across the boundary unlocks new value.

**Path 2: Look across strategic groups within the industry**
Most industries have groups competing at different price/value points. Why do buyers trade up or down between groups? The answer reveals where new value can be created.

**Path 3: Look across the chain of buyers**
Who is the buyer, the user, and the influencer? Industries converge on influencing one group. Shifting to a different buyer group can reveal an entirely new value perspective.

**Path 4: Look across complementary products and services**
What happens before, during, and after a buyer uses your product? Value is often destroyed by the total context of use. Solving for the total experience, not just the product, creates new space.

**Path 5: Look across functional and emotional appeal**
Some industries compete on function. Others compete on emotion. Shifting from one to the other often reveals untapped demand. A functional industry made emotional, or an emotional industry made functional, can create new space.

**Path 6: Look across time**
What trends are shaping your industry? Most companies adapt to trends reactively. Examining how trends will change value delivery, and designing for that future now, can create blue ocean space ahead of the market.

### Three Tiers of Non-Customers

A key tool for discovering new demand is analyzing non-customers, the people not currently buying from your industry.

**First-tier non-customers:** Soon-to-be non-customers. People who use your industry's products minimally and are looking for a better alternative. They are on the edge of the market.

**Second-tier non-customers:** Refusing non-customers. People who consciously chose not to use your industry's offerings. They have seen the options and found them unacceptable.

**Third-tier non-customers:** Unexplored non-customers. People who have never been targeted by your industry because they were assumed to be in a completely different market.

The commonalities in why non-customers do not buy reveal the dimensions of a blue ocean: the factors the industry ignores that would unlock massive new demand if addressed.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Your market is increasingly commoditized and you are competing primarily on price
- You are losing differentiation as competitors copy your features
- Growth has slowed because the total addressable market feels saturated
- You are building a new product and want to design it to create new demand rather than steal share
- You are preparing a fundraising narrative that explains why you are not just another competitor in an established category
- You are running a strategic planning session and need a framework for identifying breakthrough moves
- You want to evaluate whether a proposed new product is genuinely differentiated or just another option in an existing red ocean

Do NOT use when:
- You need a near-term competitive response to an immediate threat (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)
- You need to size an existing market (use `market-opportunity-analysis.md`)
- You need a product feature prioritization tool (use `feature-prioritization.md`)
- The market is genuinely new and there is no existing industry to reconstruct from

## Dos

- **Do start with a rigorous as-is strategy canvas before designing the to-be canvas.** You cannot identify where to diverge until you honestly map where the industry currently clusters.
- **Do look for factors to eliminate before adding new ones.** Most companies only raise and create. The power of Blue Ocean is that eliminating and reducing funds the raising and creating, making value innovation economically viable.
- **Do seek input from non-customers.** The most powerful blue ocean insights come from people who do not currently buy in your category, not from your existing customers who have adapted to the current product.
- **Do test the value curve against three criteria:** focus (not high on everything), divergence (clearly different from competitors), and compelling tagline (captures the new offering in a sentence a non-customer understands).
- **Do map the full buyer experience, not just the product.** Blue ocean opportunities often live in the experience around the product: purchase, delivery, use, complementary services, and disposal.
- **Do connect the ERRC grid to cost structure.** Eliminating and reducing should demonstrably free up cost that funds raising and creating. If the cost math does not work, the strategy canvas is aspirational, not executable.
- **Do examine at least two of the six paths before settling on a direction.** Different paths surface different blue ocean opportunities. The first one you look at is rarely the best one.
- **Do think about how the blue ocean can be protected.** Blue oceans do not stay blue forever. Identify the execution and timing factors that create a window before competition follows.

---

## Donts

- **Dont confuse differentiation with blue ocean strategy.** Adding a feature no competitor has is differentiation. Blue ocean strategy reconstructs the value curve across multiple factors simultaneously.
- **Dont assume blue ocean means completely new technology.** Many of the most powerful blue ocean moves use existing technology differently. Cirque du Soleil did not invent a new art form. It combined existing elements in a new way for a new audience.
- **Dont skip the eliminate and reduce quadrants of the ERRC grid.** A strategy canvas that only raises and creates is just a more expensive version of the same product. Elimination and reduction are what make value innovation economically viable.
- **Dont build the strategy canvas using only internal perspectives.** The most important canvas inputs come from actual buyers, near-buyers, and non-buyers, not from the product or marketing team's assumptions about what matters.
- **Dont ignore the cost side.** Blue ocean strategy produces value innovation: differentiation AND cost reduction simultaneously. A strategy that only adds value but increases costs is not a blue ocean strategy. It is a premium positioning strategy.
- **Dont confuse a niche product with a blue ocean.** Serving a small underserved segment is niche strategy. Blue ocean strategy creates new demand from non-customers at scale.
- **Dont treat it as a one-time exercise.** Blue oceans eventually turn red as imitators arrive. The strategic discipline is to continuously look for the next blue ocean while defending the current one.
- **Dont try to apply the framework to a product that has not yet validated basic demand.** Blue Ocean Strategy works best for products that are past initial validation and need to find a growth path that avoids commoditization.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. YOUR CURRENT PRODUCT OR MARKET POSITION
   What do you offer today and who do you serve?
   What market category do you currently compete in?

2. THE EXISTING COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE
   Who are the main competitors and what do they compete on?
   What factors does the industry treat as standard?

3. YOUR CURRENT VALUE CURVE
   How do you currently compare to competitors on key factors?
   Where are you higher, lower, or similar?

4. WHO IS NOT BUYING IN YOUR CATEGORY AND WHY
   Who are the first, second, and third-tier non-customers?
   Why do they not buy, and what would they need to?

5. THE STRATEGIC GOAL
   Are you trying to create a new product in blue ocean space,
   or shift an existing product away from a red ocean?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. FACTORS THE INDUSTRY CURRENTLY COMPETES ON
   A complete list of what every player in your category
   invests in and competes on, even if they seem obvious.

7. COMPLEMENTARY INDUSTRIES TO LOOK ACROSS
   What do buyers use before, during, or after your product?
   What adjacent industries serve the same need differently?

8. CUSTOMER AND NON-CUSTOMER INTERVIEWS
   Any qualitative data on why current customers chose you
   and why non-customers have not engaged with the category.

9. COST STRUCTURE AWARENESS
   Which factors are most expensive to deliver?
   This helps evaluate what is feasible to eliminate or reduce.

10. TIME HORIZON
    Are you designing a blue ocean for the next 3 years or 10 years?
    Longer horizons allow for more path 6 (trends) exploration.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Current product: Project management SaaS for marketing agencies
Current market: Competing with Asana, Monday.com, ClickUp, Teamwork for agency use
Current value curve: We are roughly equal to competitors on task management,
collaboration, and integrations. We are slightly better on agency-specific templates.
We lose on brand recognition and mobile experience.
Non-customers: Freelance creative directors who manage projects in email and Notion
because PM tools feel too corporate and interrupt creative flow. Independent consultants
who tried PM tools and found them too complex for solo work.
Competitive factors the industry competes on: Task management depth, workflow automation,
integrations, reporting dashboards, pricing tiers, mobile apps, templates, collaboration
Strategic goal: Shift existing product away from the red ocean into a position
that makes the Asana/Monday comparison irrelevant
```

## Output Format

```
1. RED OCEAN DIAGNOSIS
   The current industry factors being competed on.
   How the main competitors cluster on these factors.
   Evidence that the market is a red ocean: margin compression,
   feature parity, price competition, commoditization signals.

2. AS-IS STRATEGY CANVAS
   Table or description mapping competitors and your current product
   across all industry factors, with relative offering levels.
   Identification of where the industry clusters (everyone high or low
   on the same factors).

3. NON-CUSTOMER ANALYSIS
   First-tier non-customers: who they are and why they are on the edge
   Second-tier non-customers: who refuses and why
   Third-tier non-customers: who has never been targeted and why
   Commonalities across all three tiers: the shared pain or unmet need
   that a blue ocean strategy could address

4. SIX PATHS ANALYSIS
   Evaluation of at least 3 of the 6 paths most relevant to your market:
   - Which path was explored
   - What it revealed about potential blue ocean space
   - The specific opportunity it suggests

5. ERRC GRID (Four Actions Framework)
   ELIMINATE: Factors to remove entirely (with rationale and cost implication)
   REDUCE: Factors to drop below industry standard (with rationale and cost implication)
   RAISE: Factors to lift above industry standard (with rationale and value implication)
   CREATE: New factors never offered in the industry (with rationale and value implication)

6. TO-BE STRATEGY CANVAS
   The new value curve resulting from the ERRC grid.
   Annotated to show how it diverges from the current industry standard.
   Tested against three criteria: focus, divergence, compelling tagline.

7. BLUE OCEAN TAGLINE
   A one-sentence description of the new offering that a non-customer
   would immediately understand and find compelling.

8. VALUE INNOVATION LOGIC
   How the ERRC grid simultaneously creates more value for buyers
   while reducing or containing cost. The economic logic of the blue ocean.

9. EXECUTION CONSIDERATIONS
   The main execution risks in moving to the new value curve.
   The window of opportunity before imitators follow.
   The capabilities that need to be built or acquired.

10. STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS
    What this means for product roadmap, positioning, messaging,
    and go-to-market strategy.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a strategic innovation analyst applying the Blue Ocean Strategy framework
to help me identify and design uncontested market space.

My current product and market: [what you offer and who you currently serve]
Current competitive landscape: [main competitors and what they compete on]
My current value curve: [how I compare to competitors on key factors]
Non-customers: [who is not buying in this category and why]
Complementary industries: [what buyers use before, during, or after my product]
Current cost structure: [which factors are most expensive to deliver]
Strategic goal: [create new product in blue ocean space / shift existing product
away from red ocean]
Time horizon: [3 years / 5 years / 10 years]

Please produce:
1. A red ocean diagnosis of my current market
2. An as-is strategy canvas mapping competitors and my product across all
   industry factors
3. A non-customer analysis across all three tiers with common pain themes
4. An analysis of at least three of the six paths for reconstructing market boundaries
5. A complete ERRC grid with rationale and cost/value implications for each action
6. A to-be strategy canvas showing the new value curve
7. A blue ocean tagline that a non-customer would immediately understand
8. The value innovation logic showing how differentiation and cost reduction
   work simultaneously
9. Execution considerations and the competitive window
10. Strategic implications for product, positioning, and go-to-market

Be direct. If my current position is deep in a red ocean with no clear path
to differentiation, say so. If one of the six paths is significantly more
promising than others for my specific market, explain why.
Challenge me if my proposed raises and creates do not actually address
non-customer pain or unlock new demand.
```
