# porter-five-forces.md

> **Skill:** Applies Porter's Five Forces framework to analyze the structural attractiveness and competitive intensity of an industry, identifying where power sits, how it shifts, and what it means for your strategic position.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"Is this industry structurally attractive, and where does competitive power actually sit?"* It applies Michael Porter's Five Forces framework to produce a rigorous analysis of competitive intensity across five dimensions: competitive rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, bargaining power of buyers, and bargaining power of suppliers. The output is used to evaluate market attractiveness, inform positioning strategy, identify where to build defensibility, and explain competitive dynamics to investors or boards.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is Porter's Five Forces?

Porter's Five Forces is a framework developed by Michael Porter of Harvard Business School, introduced in his 1979 Harvard Business Review article "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy" and later expanded in his book *Competitive Strategy* (1980).

The framework analyzes the competitive structure of an industry by examining five forces that determine the intensity of competition and the long-run profitability potential of any industry. The core insight is that competition for profit goes beyond direct rivals. Customers, suppliers, potential entrants, and substitute products all compete with established players for a share of the industry's profit pool.

Understanding these five forces tells you:
- How attractive an industry is to enter or remain in
- Where structural advantages exist that can be built into a defensible position
- How the industry's profit will be distributed among players
- Which competitive threats to prioritize and how to mitigate them

### The Five Forces Explained

**Force 1: Competitive Rivalry**
The intensity of competition among existing players in the industry.

High rivalry intensity is driven by:
- Many competitors of similar size and capability
- Slow industry growth (players fight for share rather than riding growth)
- Undifferentiated products or services (price becomes the primary battle)
- High fixed costs (players must fill capacity aggressively)
- High exit barriers (struggling companies stay in rather than exit)
- Low switching costs for customers

Low rivalry intensity exists when:
- Few competitors and clear market leaders
- Fast market growth (everyone grows without taking share)
- Differentiated products with loyal customer bases
- Low fixed costs and flexible capacity
- High switching costs keeping customers loyal

**Force 2: Threat of New Entrants**
The risk that new competitors will enter the market and erode existing players' profitability.

High threat of entry when:
- Low capital requirements to start
- No significant economies of scale advantage for incumbents
- Easy access to distribution channels
- No proprietary technology, IP, or data advantages
- Low customer switching costs (easy to move to a new entrant)
- Minimal regulatory barriers

Low threat of entry when:
- High capital requirements (e.g., semiconductor fabs)
- Significant scale economies that incumbents possess
- Strong brand loyalty and high switching costs
- Proprietary technology, patents, or exclusive data
- Network effects that make incumbents more valuable as they grow
- Heavy regulation requiring licenses, approvals, or compliance investment

**Force 3: Threat of Substitutes**
The risk that customers will switch to a fundamentally different product or service that satisfies the same need.

Note: Substitutes are different from direct competitors. A substitute fulfills the same customer need through a different mechanism. Email is a substitute for physical mail. Zoom is a substitute for business travel.

High threat of substitutes when:
- Alternative approaches to the same customer job are readily available
- The price-to-performance ratio of substitutes is improving rapidly
- Customers have low cost or effort to switch to the substitute
- The substitute is backed by a large, well-resourced company

Low threat of substitutes when:
- No viable alternative approach exists for the customer job
- The substitute is significantly inferior in performance or convenience
- High switching costs or integration depth makes switching impractical
- The industry has strong brand identity tied to the specific category

**Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers**
The ability of customers to drive prices down, demand higher quality, or play competitors against each other.

High buyer power when:
- Buyers are few in number or purchase in large volumes
- The product is undifferentiated and buyers can switch easily
- Buyers have full pricing transparency across competitors
- Buyers could credibly develop the capability themselves (backward integration)
- The product represents a significant portion of buyers' total cost

Low buyer power when:
- Many fragmented buyers, none individually significant
- Highly differentiated product with high switching costs
- Buyers have limited visibility into alternative pricing
- The product is a small percentage of buyers' total cost
- Buyers depend on the product for their own core operations

**Force 5: Bargaining Power of Suppliers**
The ability of suppliers (inputs, labor, technology, capital) to raise prices, reduce quality, or constrain supply.

High supplier power when:
- Few suppliers relative to many buyers
- The supplier's input is critical and has no substitute
- High cost for the buyer to switch to an alternative supplier
- Suppliers could credibly enter the industry directly (forward integration)
- Suppliers have strong brands or proprietary technology

Low supplier power when:
- Many competing suppliers for the same input
- Low switching costs between suppliers
- The input is a commodity with standardized pricing
- The buyer represents a large portion of the supplier's revenue

### The Sixth Force: Complements and Ecosystem

Andrew Grove of Intel and others extended Porter's model with a sixth force: complementors, companies whose products increase the value of yours (or yours increases the value of theirs). This is particularly relevant in platform and ecosystem businesses.

Examples: App developers are complements to iOS. Systems integrators are complements to Salesforce. The strength of your complement ecosystem affects both your attractiveness to buyers and your competitive position against rivals.

### How the Forces Interact

No force exists in isolation. They interact:
- High supplier power and high buyer power simultaneously crushes the middle of a value chain (classic "stuck in the middle" problem)
- High rivalry combined with low switching costs accelerates commoditization
- Network effects (a barrier to entry) simultaneously reduce rivalry by creating winner-take-most dynamics
- Platform businesses can shift competitive dynamics dramatically by changing which forces apply

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Evaluating whether to enter a new industry or market category
- Assessing the long-run profitability potential of your current market
- Preparing an investor memo or board presentation on market dynamics
- Identifying where to build structural defensibility in your business model
- Evaluating an acquisition target's industry attractiveness
- Understanding why margins in an industry are structurally high or low
- Designing a business model that works with the industry structure rather than against it

Do NOT use when:
- You need a competitor-by-competitor analysis (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)
- You need to size the market opportunity (use `market-opportunity-analysis.md`)
- You need an internal capabilities assessment (use `swot-analysis.md`)
- You are analyzing a single company rather than an industry structure

## Dos

- **Do analyze the industry, not just your company's position.** Five Forces is about industry structure. Your company's position within that structure is a separate question.
- **Do rate the intensity of each force on a clear scale.** Low, medium, or high intensity for each force. Qualitative narrative without a rating leaves the strategic implication ambiguous.
- **Do explain the direction of change for each force.** Is buyer power increasing or decreasing? Is the threat of entry rising or falling? Trend matters as much as current state.
- **Do identify the 2 to 3 most strategically significant forces for your specific industry.** Not all five forces are equally important in every industry. In software, threat of substitutes and bargaining power of buyers often dominate. In manufacturing, supplier power and rivalry often dominate.
- **Do connect each force to specific strategic implications.** Every force analysis should end with "and therefore our strategy should..."
- **Do update the analysis when industry conditions change significantly.** A Five Forces analysis from three years ago may not reflect the current competitive reality, especially in technology markets.
- **Do use data and evidence to support your force ratings.** "Buyer power is high" is an assertion. "Buyer power is high because customers have 12 viable alternatives, zero switching costs, and purchase in volumes of $50K+ which makes them individually significant" is analysis.
- **Do consider the impact of technology on traditional force ratings.** Digital businesses often experience different force dynamics than physical businesses (e.g., near-zero marginal cost distribution changes supplier dynamics, digital marketplaces aggregate buyer power differently).

---

## Donts

- **Dont treat Five Forces as a checklist to complete.** It is an analytical lens. Each force requires genuine thinking about the specific dynamics in your industry, not generic observations.
- **Dont conflate your competitors with the competitive forces.** Competitive rivalry is one of five forces. Your specific competitors are examples within that force, not the full picture.
- **Dont analyze only the current state.** Industry structure evolves. Technology, regulation, and market development all shift force dynamics. Your analysis must address trends, not just snapshot.
- **Dont ignore the implications.** A Five Forces analysis that describes the forces but draws no conclusions is incomplete. The output of the analysis is strategic direction, not the analysis itself.
- **Dont apply it rigidly to every industry.** Porter designed the framework primarily for manufacturing and traditional industries. Applying it to platform businesses, network-effect businesses, or two-sided markets requires adaptation and additional frameworks.
- **Dont treat all five forces as equally important.** In most industries, two or three forces dominate. Identify which ones and focus the most analysis there.
- **Dont skip the complements/ecosystem dimension for technology businesses.** In software and platform markets, the sixth force of complementors can be as strategically important as any of the original five.
- **Dont use it to justify a predetermined conclusion.** The value of Five Forces is that it surfaces uncomfortable structural realities. If your analysis concludes everything is favorable, you are probably missing something.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE INDUSTRY OR MARKET TO ANALYZE
   Be specific about the industry definition.
   Too broad (e.g., "software") or too narrow (e.g., "our specific product")
   produces a useless analysis. The right level is the competitive space
   where buyers are choosing between alternatives.

2. YOUR COMPANY'S POSITION IN THE INDUSTRY
   Are you an incumbent, a new entrant, or evaluating entry?
   This affects how you interpret the force implications.

3. KEY PLAYERS OVERVIEW
   Who are the main competitors, key suppliers, and major customer segments?
   This grounds the analysis in the specific industry structure.

4. THE PURPOSE OF THE ANALYSIS
   Evaluating market entry? Building investor narrative?
   Strategic planning? Acquisition evaluation?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
5. KNOWN STRUCTURAL FEATURES
   Anything you already know about the industry's structure:
   concentration levels, switching costs, capital requirements,
   regulatory environment, margin profiles.

6. RECENT INDUSTRY DEVELOPMENTS
   Significant changes in the past 12 to 24 months:
   new entrants, technology shifts, regulatory changes,
   consolidation activity, major funding rounds.

7. TECHNOLOGY DYNAMICS
   Is technology increasing or decreasing barriers to entry?
   Is it shifting power toward or away from buyers or suppliers?

8. GEOGRAPHIC SCOPE
   Is this a global, regional, or local industry analysis?
   Force dynamics often vary significantly by geography.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Industry: B2B HR payroll and compliance software for US small businesses (1 to 50 employees)
My position: New entrant building a niche product for restaurant operators specifically
Key players: Gusto (dominant), QuickBooks Payroll, ADP Run, Paychex Flex, Square Payroll,
Manual/accountant-managed payroll
Key suppliers: Payment processing rails (Stripe, Dwolla), banking relationships,
compliance data providers (Bloomberg Tax), cloud infrastructure (AWS)
Key customers: Small business owners, office managers, HR generalists
Purpose: Evaluating market entry and building investor narrative
Recent developments: Gusto raised $200M Series E, Square Payroll improving restaurant compliance,
new tip credit regulations creating compliance complexity, accountant channel becoming important
Margin profile: Gusto reported ~55 to 60% gross margins at scale
```

## Output Format

```
1. INDUSTRY DEFINITION
   Clear statement of what industry is being analyzed and at what level of specificity.
   Why this definition is the right analytical boundary.

2. FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

   For each of the five forces:
   - Force name and brief definition
   - Intensity rating: Low / Medium / High
   - Direction of change: Increasing / Stable / Decreasing
   - Key drivers: The 3 to 5 specific factors determining this force's intensity
   - Evidence: Data, examples, or market observations supporting the rating
   - Strategic implication: What this force means for incumbents and entrants

3. FORCE INTERACTION ANALYSIS
   How the most significant forces interact with each other.
   Any reinforcing or counteracting dynamics between forces.

4. INDUSTRY ATTRACTIVENESS SUMMARY
   Overall rating: Highly Attractive / Moderately Attractive /
   Unattractive / Structurally Challenged
   The 2 to 3 forces that most determine this overall rating.
   Profit pool distribution: where in the value chain does profit accumulate?

5. STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS FOR YOUR POSITION
   Given the force structure, what does this mean for:
   - Whether to enter or remain in this industry
   - Where to build structural defensibility
   - Which forces to work with vs. which to try to neutralize
   - The business model design that best fits this force structure

6. FORCE TREND ANALYSIS
   The 2 to 3 most significant ways the force structure is likely to
   shift in the next 3 to 5 years and why.
   The strategic response those shifts require.

7. COMPLEMENTORS ANALYSIS (for technology/platform businesses)
   Who are the key complements to your business?
   How does complement strength or weakness affect your competitive position?
```

```
You are a strategic analyst applying Porter's Five Forces framework to help me
understand the structural dynamics and attractiveness of an industry.

Industry to analyze: [specific industry and market level]

Key players: [main competitors, suppliers, and customer types]

Technology dynamics: [how is technology affecting industry structure]
Geographic scope: [global / regional / local]

Please produce:
1. A clear industry definition at the right level of analytical specificity
2. Analysis of all five forces with intensity rating (Low/Medium/High),
   direction of change, key drivers, supporting evidence, and strategic implication
3. An analysis of how the dominant forces interact
4. An overall industry attractiveness rating with rationale
5. Strategic implications for my specific position: where to build defensibility,
   which forces to neutralize, and what business model design fits this structure

Do not soften the implications to make entry look more appealing than it is.
```

## Industry Attractiveness Reference

| Force Combination | Typical Outcome |
|---|---|
| All five forces low intensity | Highly attractive: high margins, stable profits, defensible positions |
| High rivalry + low switching costs | Commoditization: price competition erodes margins for all |
| High buyer power + many substitutes | Thin margins: customers have too many options |
| High entry threat + rapid technology change | Winner-take-most dynamics: first movers must build moats fast |
| High supplier power + high buyer power | Value chain squeeze: middlemen struggle to retain margin |
| Network effects present | Self-reinforcing: early leader gains increasing structural advantage |
