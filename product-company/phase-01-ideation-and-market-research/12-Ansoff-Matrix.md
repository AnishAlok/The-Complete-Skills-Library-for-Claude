# ansoff-matrix.md

> **Skill:** Applies the Ansoff Matrix to structure and evaluate growth strategy options across four quadrants: market penetration, market development, product development, and diversification.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"What are our realistic options for growing this business, and how risky is each one?"* It applies Igor Ansoff's growth strategy matrix to evaluate four possible paths: selling more of what you have to who you already sell it to, taking what you have to new customers, building new products for existing customers, or entering entirely new markets with new products. The output is a structured growth strategy analysis with risk assessment and a clear recommendation for which path to prioritize and in what sequence.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is the Ansoff Matrix?

The Ansoff Matrix (also called the Product-Market Expansion Grid) was developed by Igor Ansoff, a Russian-American mathematician and business manager, and published in his 1957 Harvard Business Review article "Strategies for Diversification." It remains one of the most widely used strategic planning tools for evaluating growth options.

The matrix plots two dimensions:
- **Products:** Existing products vs. new products
- **Markets:** Existing markets vs. new markets

This creates four strategic quadrants, each representing a different growth path with a different risk profile.

### The Four Quadrants

**Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (existing product, existing market)**
Grow by selling more of your current product to your current customers and market.

This is the lowest-risk growth strategy because you are working with known products and known customers. You are not asking anyone to take a new kind of risk. You are asking your existing market to buy more, buy more often, or stay longer.

Tactics include:
- Increasing usage frequency among existing customers
- Reducing churn to retain a larger percentage of the customer base
- Converting free or trial users to paid
- Increasing average contract value through upsell or expansion
- Taking market share from competitors through better positioning or pricing
- Referral programs that use existing customers to reach similar prospects

Risk level: Low
When to prioritize: When there is significant untapped demand in your current market, when you have not yet achieved dominant market share, or when you need to improve unit economics before expanding.

**Quadrant 2: Market Development (existing product, new market)**
Grow by taking your current product into new markets: new geographies, new customer segments, new verticals, or new use cases.

Risk level: Medium. The product risk is low (you know it works) but the market risk is higher. New markets have different buying behaviors, distribution channels, competitive dynamics, and customer needs. What worked in one market does not automatically translate.

Tactics include:
- Geographic expansion (new countries or regions)
- Segment expansion (moving from SMB to mid-market or enterprise, or vice versa)
- Vertical expansion (moving from one industry to another)
- New channel development (direct to partner-led, or online to offline)
- New use case positioning (repositioning existing product for a different job-to-be-done)

Risk level: Medium
When to prioritize: When your current market is near saturation, when a clearly adjacent market has expressed demand, or when your product is genuinely applicable to a new segment with minimal modification.

**Quadrant 3: Product Development (new product, existing market)**
Grow by building new products or significant product extensions for your existing customer base.

Risk level: Medium to high. You know the customer (low market risk) but you are building something new (higher product risk). New products require R&D investment, may cannibalize existing revenue, and may not achieve adoption even with an established customer relationship.

Tactics include:
- Building adjacent features that expand the product footprint
- Creating new product tiers or editions for different buyer needs
- Launching complementary products that solve related problems for the same customer
- Platform extension: opening up an API or marketplace for third-party solutions
- Acquiring capabilities through M&A to accelerate product development

Risk level: Medium to high
When to prioritize: When you have high customer retention and trust, when customers are explicitly asking for adjacent solutions, or when a new product can significantly increase LTV without proportionate CAC increase.

**Quadrant 4: Diversification (new product, new market)**
Grow by entering entirely new markets with new products. The highest-risk quadrant.

Two types of diversification:
- **Related diversification:** New products and markets that share some capability, technology, or customer relationship with your existing business. Risk is reduced by the overlap.
- **Unrelated diversification:** New products and markets with no connection to existing operations. Risk is highest. Often seen in large conglomerates with excess capital but less common and rarely recommended for focused growth companies.

Risk level: High (related) to very high (unrelated)
When to prioritize: When existing markets are declining or saturated, when a transformational opportunity is identified with strong evidence, or when the core business generates excess cash that cannot be reinvested at sufficient returns.

### Risk Calibration Across the Matrix

Ansoff's original framing described risk increasing as you move away from the existing product/existing market quadrant. A common representation:

- Market Penetration: 1x risk (baseline)
- Market Development: 2x risk
- Product Development: 2x risk
- Diversification: 4x to 8x risk

Modern practitioners add that the actual risk of each quadrant depends on how different the new market or new product is from the existing base. An international expansion into a very similar market (same language, culture, and buying behavior) is lower risk than moving to an adjacent customer segment with radically different needs.

### Sequencing Growth Strategies

The most important practical output of an Ansoff analysis is not just identifying which quadrants are available but sequencing them correctly. Most companies should:

1. Exhaust market penetration opportunities before expanding
2. Choose between market development and product development based on where evidence of demand is stronger
3. Treat diversification as a last resort or opportunistic play, not a primary growth strategy

The mistake most growth-stage companies make is pursuing quadrants 2, 3, and 4 simultaneously before they have fully captured quadrant 1. This spreads execution capacity too thin and is a leading cause of growth stalls.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Running annual strategic planning and needing to evaluate growth options systematically
- A company has hit a growth plateau in its current market and needs to identify the next source of growth
- Leadership is debating between multiple growth directions and needs a framework to evaluate trade-offs
- Preparing a board or investor presentation on growth strategy
- Evaluating whether to expand internationally, launch a new product, or go deeper in the existing market
- A new CEO or CPO is assessing the strategic options available to the business
- Sizing the growth opportunity of each quadrant to determine which offers the best return on investment

Do NOT use when:
- You need a competitive analysis (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)
- You need a go-to-market plan for a specific initiative (use `gtm-strategy.md`)
- You need a product roadmap (use `product-roadmap.md`)
- You need to evaluate a specific acquisition target (use a dedicated M&A analysis)

## Dos

- **Do assess all four quadrants before choosing one.** The best growth path is often not the most obvious one. Evaluating all four ensures you do not default to a familiar option without considering alternatives.
- **Do estimate the size of the opportunity in each quadrant.** A quadrant that feels exciting but offers limited incremental revenue should be ranked below a less exciting quadrant with larger upside.
- **Do honestly assess the execution capacity required.** Each quadrant has different resource and capability requirements. A quadrant that requires capabilities you do not have is riskier than one that builds on existing strengths.
- **Do look at where evidence of demand already exists.** Inbound requests for a new segment (market development) or existing customer requests for a new feature (product development) are lower-risk starting points than speculative expansion.
- **Do sequence your growth strategy.** Most companies should fully capture quadrant 1 before moving to 2 or 3. Present the sequencing logic, not just the list of options.
- **Do consider the impact on existing business.** New quadrant investments draw resources, leadership attention, and engineering capacity from existing operations. The opportunity cost must be part of the evaluation.
- **Do validate assumptions with data before committing.** Each quadrant's risk can be reduced through small experiments before making large bets.
- **Do use the matrix to say no as much as to say yes.** One of the most valuable outputs of an Ansoff analysis is explicit deprioritization of quadrants that are not right at this stage.

---

## Donts

- **Dont pursue multiple quadrants simultaneously without sufficient scale.** Most companies under $20M ARR do not have the resources to run more than one growth quadrant at a time without diluting execution in all of them.
- **Dont confuse market development with product development.** Adding a Spanish-language version of your product is market development (same product, new geography). Building a new module for your existing customers is product development (new product, same market).
- **Dont underestimate the organizational change required for market development.** Entering a new geography or segment often requires new sales motion, new marketing content, new support capabilities, and sometimes new legal and compliance infrastructure. It is rarely just flipping a switch.
- **Dont treat diversification as a default when the current market is hard.** Diversification does not solve execution problems in the core business. It adds execution complexity on top of them.
- **Dont confuse product extension with new product development.** Adding a feature to your existing product is penetration or product extension. Building a separate product with its own positioning, pricing, and buyer journey is true product development and carries meaningfully higher risk.
- **Dont skip the risk calibration.** A growth plan that lists all four quadrants as equally viable is not strategic. Risk must be explicitly compared across quadrants to produce a prioritized recommendation.
- **Dont use the matrix only for the current year.** The most useful application of Ansoff is a multi-year growth sequencing plan: penetration this year, market development next year, product development the year after.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. CURRENT PRODUCT AND MARKET
   What do you currently sell and to whom?
   What is the current revenue or traction?

2. CURRENT MARKET PENETRATION
   How saturated is your current market?
   What percentage of the addressable market do you currently serve?
   What is the realistic upside remaining in your current market?

3. POTENTIAL NEW MARKETS
   What adjacent geographies, segments, or verticals have been identified
   as potential expansion targets?
   Is there existing evidence of demand in any of these?

4. POTENTIAL NEW PRODUCTS
   What adjacent products or significant extensions are being considered?
   Are existing customers asking for anything specific?

5. RESOURCE AND EXECUTION CONTEXT
   Headcount, budget, engineering capacity.
   What can realistically be pursued given current resources?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. CUSTOMER DATA
   Expansion revenue from existing customers, churn rate, NPS,
   common feature requests. This informs quadrant 1 and 3 potential.

7. INBOUND DEMAND SIGNALS
   Are prospects from new segments or geographies already finding you?
   Is there pull from a market you have not targeted?

8. COMPETITIVE CONTEXT
   Are competitors moving into your current market (forcing faster expansion)
   or opening up adjacent markets you could follow into?

9. MARKET SIZE ESTIMATES BY QUADRANT
   If you have any sizing data for potential new markets or new product opportunities,
   include it so the analysis can be quantified.

10. TIME HORIZON AND GROWTH TARGETS
    What growth targets are you trying to hit and over what period?
    This determines how aggressive the quadrant sequencing needs to be.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Current product: SaaS compliance automation for US fintech startups (SOC 2, ISO 27001)
Current customers: 85 US-based fintech companies, $1.8M ARR, 40% YoY growth
Current market penetration: Estimate 2 to 3% of addressable US fintech compliance market
Potential new markets: UK fintech (PSD2/FCA compliance is similar pain),
healthcare startups (HIPAA compliance has parallel structure),
mid-market companies currently over-served by enterprise tools
Potential new products: Vendor risk management module (customers asking for this),
compliance training content platform, automated evidence collection integrations
Resources: 18 employees, $3.2M runway, can hire 4 people this year
Competitive context: Vanta expanding aggressively into our segment, new entrant Drata
is well-funded and growing fast
Market sizes: US fintech compliance market SAM ~$400M, UK fintech ~$120M,
healthcare startup compliance ~$280M, vendor risk management market ~$600M
Growth target: $5M ARR in 18 months, Series A in 12 months
```

## Output Format

```
1. CURRENT STATE SUMMARY
   Where you are today: product, market, penetration level, growth rate.
   The starting point for the growth strategy evaluation.

2. ANSOFF MATRIX ANALYSIS

   For each of the four quadrants:

   QUADRANT NAME (e.g., Market Penetration)
   Product: existing / new
   Market: existing / new
   Risk level: Low / Medium / Medium-High / High

   Opportunity size: estimated incremental revenue potential
   Evidence of demand: what data or signals support this
   Key tactics available: 3 to 5 specific approaches
   Resources required: headcount, budget, time, capabilities needed
   Key risks: what could go wrong
   Readiness assessment: how prepared are you to execute this today?

3. RISK-RETURN COMPARISON
   Side-by-side comparison of all four quadrants on:
   - Estimated revenue upside
   - Risk level
   - Time to revenue
   - Resources required
   - Confidence in demand evidence

4. RECOMMENDED GROWTH SEQUENCE
   The prioritized sequence across quadrants for this year and next 2 to 3 years.
   Rationale for the sequence: why this order, not another.
   The trigger conditions for moving from one quadrant to the next.

5. PRIMARY QUADRANT DEEP DIVE
   For the recommended primary focus quadrant:
   A detailed go-to-market and execution plan outline.
   Milestones, resources, and success metrics.

6. DEPRIORITIZED QUADRANTS
   Explicit statement of which quadrants are being deprioritized and why.
   Conditions under which they should be reconsidered.

7. RESOURCE ALLOCATION IMPLICATIONS
   How headcount, budget, and leadership attention should be
   distributed to execute the recommended sequence.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a growth strategy advisor applying the Ansoff Matrix to help me
identify, evaluate, and sequence my growth options.

Current product and market: [what you sell and who you sell it to]
Current revenue and growth rate: [ARR/revenue and YoY growth]
Current market penetration: [estimated % of addressable market you currently serve]
Potential new markets: [adjacent geographies, segments, or verticals identified]
Potential new products: [adjacent products or significant extensions being considered]
Resources available: [headcount, budget, engineering capacity]
Growth targets: [revenue goal and time horizon]
Competitive context: [what competitors are doing that affects timing]
Evidence of demand in new quadrants: [any inbound signals or customer requests]
Market size estimates: [any sizing data for potential expansion options]

Please produce:
1. A current state summary as the baseline for growth strategy
2. A full analysis of all four Ansoff quadrants with opportunity size,
   demand evidence, key tactics, resources required, risks, and readiness rating
3. A risk-return comparison across all four quadrants
4. A recommended growth sequence for this year and the next 2 to 3 years,
   with trigger conditions for moving between quadrants
5. A deep-dive execution outline for the primary recommended quadrant
6. An explicit deprioritization of the quadrants not being pursued, with rationale
7. Resource allocation implications across the sequence

Be direct about which quadrants the company is not ready for.
If pursuing more than one quadrant simultaneously would dilute execution
in all of them, say so explicitly.
If the evidence of demand in a quadrant is weak, flag it as speculative
and recommend validation steps before committing resources.
```
