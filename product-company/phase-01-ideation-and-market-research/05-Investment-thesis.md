# investment-thesis.md

> **Skill:** Writes investment memos and board-ready rationale documents that articulate why a market, company, or product bet is worth making, with structured logic, evidence, and risk analysis.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"How do I make a compelling, rigorous written case for this investment or strategic bet?"* It produces an investment thesis document or memo that lays out the market opportunity, the specific bet being made, the logic chain supporting it, the risks and how they are mitigated, and the expected return or outcome. Used for investor fundraising, board investment decisions, internal capital allocation, and strategic partnership rationale.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is an Investment Thesis?

An investment thesis is a structured argument for why a particular investment, whether in a company, a market, a product, or a strategic direction, is worth making. It is not a pitch deck. It is not a financial model. It is the written logic that sits beneath both.

A well-structured investment thesis answers five questions in sequence:
1. Why is this market interesting and why is it interesting right now?
2. What specific bet are we making within that market?
3. Why are we the right entity to make this bet and win?
4. What has to be true for this to work, and what could make it fail?
5. What does success look like and what return does it generate?

Investment theses are used in multiple contexts:
- **VC and growth investors** writing a thesis on a company before writing a check
- **Corporate development teams** building the rationale for an acquisition
- **Boards** evaluating a major capital allocation decision
- **Founders** articulating to investors or their own team why the company exists and why now
- **Product leaders** making the internal case for a major new product investment
- **Strategy teams** documenting the rationale for entering a new market

### The Structure of a Strong Investment Thesis

The strongest investment theses follow a logical narrative with four layers:

**Layer 1: The Market Thesis**
The world is changing in a specific direction. Here is the evidence. Here is why that change creates a significant, durable opportunity. This layer is about the macro context, not the specific company or product.

**Layer 2: The Wedge Thesis**
Within this market opportunity, a specific entry point or customer segment or problem is underserved. Here is why that wedge is the right place to start and how it leads to a larger position over time.

**Layer 3: The Differentiation Thesis**
This company or product is uniquely positioned to win this wedge because of specific advantages: technology, team, distribution, data, network effects, brand, or proprietary access. Here is what makes us different from others attempting the same thing.

**Layer 4: The Return Thesis**
Given the market size, growth rate, competitive dynamics, and our differentiation, here is the expected outcome and return on investment within a defined timeframe.

### The "Why Now" Argument

One of the most important and most frequently underdeveloped sections of any investment thesis is the "why now" argument. A good market opportunity with no timing rationale is a weak thesis. Many good ideas were tried before and failed because the timing was wrong.

"Why now" is driven by one or more of these factors:
- A technology that has just become feasible or affordable (e.g., LLM costs dropping 100x in 18 months)
- A regulatory change that creates or opens a market (e.g., open banking APIs mandated by PSD2)
- A behavioral shift triggered by an external event (e.g., remote work normalizing async collaboration)
- A market structure change (e.g., an incumbent pulling back, a consolidation creating a gap)
- A platform shift creating new distribution (e.g., App Store opening mobile, ChatGPT creating LLM adoption)

### Risk Framework

Every credible investment thesis includes an honest risk analysis. Investors and boards are suspicious of theses that do not acknowledge risk. A structured risk section demonstrates intellectual honesty and shows you have thought through failure modes.

Risks fall into four categories:

**Market risks**
Is the market large enough? Is it growing fast enough? Is the timing right?

**Competitive risks**
Are incumbents more capable of winning this than we think? Are there well-funded competitors we are underestimating? Could a platform giant commoditize this?

**Execution risks**
Can we actually build and ship what we are proposing? Do we have the team? Is the timeline realistic?

**External risks**
Regulatory changes, economic downturns, technology shifts, or macro events that could invalidate the thesis.

For each risk, the thesis should include: the risk, why it is a real concern, and the specific factor that mitigates or monitors it.

## Dos

- **Do lead with the market, not the product.** Investors and boards fund market opportunities. Products are means to an end. Start with the macro thesis before introducing what you are building.
- **Do make the "why now" argument explicit and specific.** Name the precise technical, behavioral, or regulatory change that makes this moment the right time. Vague timing arguments undermine the entire thesis.
- **Do quantify wherever possible.** Market size, growth rate, TAM/SAM/SOM, expected revenue, payback period, IRR or return multiple. Numbers make logic credible.
- **Do be direct about your differentiation.** Claiming you will win because of "strong execution" or a "great team" is not a differentiation thesis. Name the specific defensible advantage.
- **Do include the most credible bear case.** Writing a bear case before an investor or board does shows intellectual honesty and preparedness. Address the strongest version of the counterargument, not a strawman.
- **Do separate facts from assumptions.** Every forward-looking statement is an assumption. Make this explicit. "We assume X because of Y evidence" is far more credible than stating projections as facts.
- **Do use the pyramid principle for structure.** Lead with your conclusion (this is a compelling investment), then provide the supporting arguments, then the evidence. Do not make the reader wait until the end to know your recommendation.
- **Do tailor the depth to the audience.** A board memo needs different depth than a VC memo. An internal capital allocation case needs different framing than an external fundraising document.
- **Do include the path to scale.** Investors and boards want to see not just how you win the first wedge, but how that wedge leads to a large, defensible position over time.
- **Do version and date the document.** Investment theses evolve as you learn. Version control shows rigor and makes it clear which assumptions have been updated.

---

## Donts

- **Dont start with the product features.** Leading with product functionality before establishing market context is the most common investment memo mistake. Context first, product second.
- **Dont ignore competitive risk.** Saying "there is no real competition" is a red flag. Every market has competition. Acknowledging it and explaining why you win anyway is far more credible.
- **Dont use projected revenue numbers without showing the model.** Stating "$50M ARR in Year 3" without showing the underlying assumptions destroys credibility. Show the logic.
- **Dont conflate the vision with the thesis.** The vision is where you want to go. The thesis is why you will get there. Both are needed, but they are different arguments.
- **Dont use jargon without defining it.** "AI-native platform with PLG motion driving NRR expansion" needs unpacking for a board audience. Know your audience and write accordingly.
- **Dont write the bear case as an afterthought.** The risk section should be as carefully constructed as the opportunity section. Boards and investors will find the risks. You should find them first.
- **Dont claim an unfair advantage you cannot defend.** If your differentiation is something competitors can copy in 6 months, it is not a durable advantage. Be honest about the durability of your edge.
- **Dont make the document too long.** A board investment memo should be 4 to 8 pages. A VC investment memo is typically 5 to 10 pages. Longer is not more rigorous. Tighter is more credible.
- **Dont skip the expected return.** Every investment thesis needs to close the loop on return. What does success look like and what does it generate financially?
- **Dont write it alone without pressure-testing.** The best investment theses are challenged internally before they go external. Ask a trusted critic to take the other side.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE INVESTMENT OR BET BEING DESCRIBED
   What exactly is the investment? A company raise? An acquisition?
   A new product line? A market entry? A major resource allocation?

2. THE MARKET CONTEXT
   What market or category is this investment in?
   What is happening in that market that creates the opportunity?

3. THE SPECIFIC OPPORTUNITY
   What is the specific wedge, customer segment, or problem being addressed?
   Why is this the right entry point?

4. YOUR DIFFERENTIATION
   Why are you or this company uniquely positioned to win?
   What specific advantages do you have?

5. THE EXPECTED RETURN OR OUTCOME
   What does success look like in 3 to 5 years?
   What financial or strategic return does this generate?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. MARKET SIZING DATA
   TAM/SAM/SOM figures you have already developed.
   (Or use market-opportunity-analysis.md to develop these first.)

7. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE OVERVIEW
   Who else is pursuing this opportunity and why you win against them.

8. FINANCIAL ASSUMPTIONS
   Revenue model, pricing, growth rate assumptions, unit economics.

9. TEAM OR CAPABILITY CONTEXT
   Why the team or organization making this investment is the right one.

10. AUDIENCE AND PURPOSE
    Who is this memo for: VC, board, internal exec team, corporate development?
    This shapes tone, depth, level of financial detail, and framing.

11. KNOWN RISKS
    Any specific risks you have already identified that must be addressed.

12. COMPARABLE INVESTMENTS OR OUTCOMES
    Analogous companies, deals, or investments that benchmark this opportunity.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Investment: Series A raise for our B2B SaaS company
Market: AP automation for mid-market manufacturing companies
Opportunity: Mid-market manufacturers are underserved by both enterprise ERP modules
(too complex, too expensive) and SMB tools (too simple). We sit in the gap.
Differentiation: Only solution purpose-built for manufacturer-specific AP workflows
(3-way match, BOM reconciliation). 14 months average to first competitor copy based on
technical complexity.
Expected outcome: $15M ARR in 36 months, path to $100M ARR in 60 months at current growth rate
Market sizing: TAM $4.2B, SAM $800M (US mid-market manufacturers), SOM $24M Year 3
Audience: Series A VC partners (Tier 1 firms)
Known risks: Enterprise ERP vendors expanding downmarket, longer sales cycle than modeled
Comparable outcomes: Tipalti Series A at similar metrics, Ramp early traction pattern
```

## Output Format

```
1. TITLE AND SUMMARY
   Document title, date, version.
   3 to 5 sentence executive summary: the investment, the opportunity,
   the differentiation, and the expected return.

2. MARKET THESIS
   Why this market is large, growing, and underserved.
   Key market data: TAM/SAM/SOM, CAGR, market structure.
   Trends and tailwinds creating the opportunity.

3. THE WHY NOW ARGUMENT
   The specific timing rationale: what has changed in technology, behavior,
   regulation, or market structure that makes this the right moment.
   This section must be specific and evidence-based.

4. THE SPECIFIC OPPORTUNITY (THE WEDGE)
   The precise customer segment, problem, or use case being targeted first.
   Why this is the right entry point.
   How this wedge expands into a larger position over time.

5. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE
   Who else is pursuing this opportunity.
   Why existing solutions are inadequate for this specific wedge.
   The specific reasons this investment wins against alternatives.

6. DIFFERENTIATION AND DEFENSIBILITY
   The specific advantages that make this bet winnable and durable.
   How long those advantages are likely to hold.
   What needs to be built or maintained to sustain them.

7. BUSINESS MODEL AND UNIT ECONOMICS
   How the business creates and captures value.
   Key unit economics: LTV, CAC, payback period, gross margin.
   Revenue model and pricing logic.

8. FINANCIAL PROJECTIONS AND RETURN
   Revenue projections with assumptions stated explicitly.
   Expected return: IRR, revenue multiple, strategic value, or milestone outcome.
   The scenario that gets you there and what has to be true.

9. RISK ANALYSIS
   Top 4 to 6 risks across market, competitive, execution, and external categories.
   For each: the risk, why it is real, and the specific mitigation or monitor.
   The bear case: what does failure look like and what triggers it?

10. THE ASK AND USE OF CAPITAL (if fundraising)
    Specific amount being raised, use of capital by category,
    milestones that will be achieved with this capital,
    and what the next raise looks like.

11. APPENDIX
    Supporting data, market research, unit economics detail,
    team backgrounds, customer references or metrics.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a venture investor and strategic analyst helping me write a rigorous
investment thesis memo.

The investment: [describe exactly what is being invested in]
Market context: [the market, what is happening, what the opportunity is]
The specific wedge: [the precise entry point: customer, problem, or use case]
Our differentiation: [specific advantages and why they are durable]
Expected return or outcome: [financial or strategic outcome in 3 to 5 years]
Market sizing: [TAM/SAM/SOM if available, or ask Claude to help build it]
Competitive landscape: [who else is trying to win this and why we beat them]
Unit economics: [LTV, CAC, gross margin, payback period if available]
Known risks: [specific risks you have already identified]
Comparable companies or deals: [analogous investments or outcomes]
Audience: [VC / board / internal exec team / corporate development]
Purpose: [Series A raise / board approval / acquisition rationale / internal capital case]

Please produce a full investment thesis memo with:
1. A 3 to 5 sentence executive summary leading with the conclusion
2. A market thesis section with data and tailwinds
3. A specific "why now" argument grounded in evidence, not general trends
4. A wedge and expansion path analysis
5. A competitive landscape and differentiation section
6. Business model and unit economics overview
7. Financial projections with all assumptions explicitly stated
8. A risk analysis addressing market, competitive, execution, and external risks
9. A bear case: what does failure look like and what triggers it?
10. The ask and use of capital (if fundraising)

Apply the pyramid principle: lead with conclusions, then supporting arguments,
then evidence. Be direct. If the thesis has a weak section, say so and ask for more input.
Separate stated facts from assumptions throughout.
```
