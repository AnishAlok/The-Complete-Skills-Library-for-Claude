# vision-mission-values.md

> **Skill:** Crafts clear, specific, and internally consistent vision statements, mission statements, and company values that align teams, guide decisions, and communicate organizational identity to employees, customers, and investors.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"Who are we, why do we exist, and what do we stand for?"* It produces a vision statement (where we are going), a mission statement (what we do and for whom today), and a set of company values (how we behave in pursuit of both). These three artifacts are the foundation of culture, hiring, strategy, and communication. Done well they are used daily. Done poorly they hang on a wall and get ignored.

---

## Topic Explanation

### The Three Documents and How They Differ

Most companies conflate vision, mission, and values or use the terms interchangeably. They are distinct documents that answer different questions and operate on different time horizons.

**Vision Statement**
Answers: Where are we going?
Time horizon: 5 to 10 years
Nature: Aspirational, future-focused, describes a world that does not yet exist
Stability: Changes rarely, maybe once per decade
Example character: "A world where every small business has access to the same financial tools as a Fortune 500 company."

**Mission Statement**
Answers: What do we do, for whom, and why does it matter today?
Time horizon: Present tense
Nature: Operational, describes what the company does right now to move toward the vision
Stability: Changes when the business model or customer focus changes significantly
Example character: "We build financial software that gives independent retailers real-time cash flow visibility so they can make confident decisions without a CFO."

**Company Values**
Answers: How do we behave in pursuit of the mission and vision?
Time horizon: Enduring, behavioral
Nature: Descriptive of actual behavior, not aspirational platitudes
Stability: Very stable, rarely changes unless there is a fundamental culture shift
Example character: Not "Integrity" but "We say the hard thing in the room, not about the room."

### Why Most Vision and Mission Statements Fail

The vast majority of company vision and mission statements are useless. They share a common set of failure modes:

**Failure mode 1: Generic language**
"To be the world's leading provider of innovative solutions that empower our customers to achieve their goals." This could describe 10,000 companies. It helps no one make a decision.

**Failure mode 2: Internal jargon**
Language that only makes sense to people already inside the company. Customers, recruits, and new employees cannot decode it.

**Failure mode 3: Aspirational values that describe ideals, not behavior**
"Integrity. Excellence. Innovation." Every company claims these. They differentiate nothing and guide no decisions.

**Failure mode 4: Written by committee into blandness**
Vision and mission written by a 15-person working group over six months produce something everyone can live with, which means something no one is excited about.

**Failure mode 5: Disconnected from reality**
A vision statement that does not match what leadership actually prioritizes, or values that are violated publicly by leaders, create cynicism faster than having no values at all.

### What Makes a Great Vision Statement

A great vision statement has three qualities:
1. It describes a specific future state that is meaningfully different from today
2. It is emotionally resonant enough that people want to work toward it
3. It is directional enough to help reject things that do not fit

The best vision statements describe a change in the world, not the company's market position. "We will be the number one platform for X" describes a company ambition. "A world where X is no longer a problem for Y" describes a change that matters to humans.

### What Makes a Great Mission Statement

A great mission statement answers four elements in one or two sentences:
1. What you do (the product or service)
2. For whom (the specific customer)
3. How (the mechanism or approach)
4. Why it matters (the outcome or change you create)

Mission statements that omit the customer become self-referential. Ones that omit the outcome become feature descriptions.

### What Makes Great Company Values

Great values share these qualities:
- **Specific and behavioral.** They describe how people act, not abstract nouns.
- **Distinctive.** They reflect choices your company actually makes that others do not.
- **Actionable.** A new employee can use them to make a decision on their first week.
- **Honest.** They describe what the company actually does, not what it aspires to do.
- **Testable.** You can point to a decision or behavior and say "that is this value in action."

The gold standard for values writing is Netflix's original culture deck, Patagonia's values, and Stripe's operating principles. All three are specific, behavioral, and distinctive.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Founding a new company and needing to establish foundational identity documents
- A company has outgrown its original vision or mission and needs a refresh
- A merger, acquisition, or pivot has changed what the company fundamentally does
- Leadership alignment is breaking down because teams have different mental models of where the company is going
- Recruiting is suffering because candidates cannot understand what the company stands for
- Culture problems are emerging and leadership suspects the values are disconnected from reality
- Preparing for a fundraising round where investors will ask about vision and differentiation
- Onboarding new executives who need to quickly internalize and communicate company identity

Do NOT use when:
- You need a detailed product vision document (use `product-vision-doc.md`)
- You need a positioning statement for marketing (use `positioning-doc.md`)
- You need a culture assessment of existing values (use `culture-assessment.md`)

## Dos

- **Do write the vision in terms of a changed world, not a company achievement.** "The world where X" is more inspiring than "We will be number one in X."
- **Do name a specific customer in the mission.** Vague customer references ("businesses," "people," "enterprises") make the mission useless as a decision-making tool.
- **Do write values as sentences or phrases that describe behavior, not nouns.** "We give feedback directly and receive it openly" is a value. "Transparency" is a word.
- **Do pressure-test every value by asking: "Would a competitor be embarrassed to claim this?"** If not, it is not distinctive enough to be useful.
- **Do involve employees in values development, but have one person write the final draft.** Collective input surfaces authentic behaviors. Collective writing produces mush.
- **Do include examples or "in practice this means..." annotations for each value.** This is what transforms values from abstract to actionable.
- **Do make sure leadership visibly lives the values before publishing them.** Values that leaders violate are worse than having no values.
- **Do keep values to 4 to 7.** Fewer means you have not thought carefully enough. More means you are listing everything good instead of making choices.
- **Do read the vision aloud to someone outside the company.** If they cannot explain it back to you, it is not clear enough.
- **Do review the mission annually.** Vision is stable for a decade. Mission should be checked every year to confirm it still accurately describes what you do.

---

## Donts

- **Dont use the word "innovative" in any of these documents.** It is the single most overused and meaningless word in corporate communications.
- **Dont write values that sound like a list of HR virtues.** Respect. Integrity. Collaboration. These describe minimum bars, not cultural choices.
- **Dont write a vision that is just a big revenue number.** "$1B company by 2030" is a financial goal, not a vision of the future.
- **Dont make the mission so broad it could apply to any company.** If your mission statement would work equally well for a competitor, it is not specific enough.
- **Dont confuse the customer outcome with the company activity.** "We provide software solutions" is an activity. "So that restaurant owners spend their time cooking, not doing accounting" is an outcome.
- **Dont write values that your company regularly violates.** If "transparency" is listed but leadership routinely withholds information, the value destroys trust instead of building it.
- **Dont workshop these documents into oblivion.** Set a deadline. Draft fast. Refine quickly. Iteration in public beats perfection in private.
- **Dont ignore the "tension values."** The most useful values are the ones that encode a deliberate choice between two good things. "We choose depth over breadth" is more useful than "we do great work."
- **Dont write separate documents for internal and external audiences.** Vision, mission, and values should be the same whether facing employees, customers, or investors. Divergence signals inauthenticity.
- **Dont treat this as a one-time branding exercise.** These documents do their real work through daily repetition and leadership behavior, not through publication.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. WHAT THE COMPANY DOES
   Describe the product or service in plain language.
   What problem does it solve? What does it make possible?

2. WHO THE CUSTOMER IS
   Who specifically benefits from what you do?
   Be as specific as possible: industry, size, role, situation.

3. WHY THE COMPANY WAS FOUNDED
   What was the founding insight or frustration?
   What did the founders believe was broken or missing?

4. WHERE YOU WANT TO GO IN 10 YEARS
   If everything goes right, what has changed in the world?
   Not company metrics: what is different for your customers and the broader market?

5. HOW YOUR COMPANY BEHAVES DIFFERENTLY
   What are 3 to 5 things your company does that most companies in your space do not?
   These become the raw material for values.
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. EXISTING VISION/MISSION/VALUES (IF REFRESHING)
   What do you currently have and what is not working about it?

7. CULTURE EXAMPLES
   Stories of moments where your company behaved in a way that
   felt distinctly "us." These are evidence of genuine values.

8. ANTI-VALUES
   What behaviors do you refuse to accept even if they would be profitable?
   What would you never do even if a competitor does it?

9. HOW EMPLOYEES DESCRIBE THE COMPANY
   Verbatim quotes from team members about what makes the company distinctive.
   These are often more honest than leadership's self-description.

10. AUDIENCE AND PURPOSE
    Is this for an internal culture document, an investor deck,
    a website about page, or a recruiting campaign?
    This affects tone and level of external polish.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Company: B2B payroll software for restaurant groups
What we do: Automate tip pooling, shift reporting, and payroll compliance
for restaurant operators with 5 to 50 locations
Customer: Restaurant group CFOs and operators who currently use spreadsheets
and manual processes and are drowning in compliance risk
Founding insight: The founders ran restaurants and spent 6 hours every week
on payroll. They could not find a tool built for hospitality specifically.
10-year world change: Restaurant operators spend their time on hospitality,
not administration. Running a restaurant group should not require a finance team.
How we behave differently: We respond to support tickets in under 2 hours.
We tell customers when our product is wrong for them. We ship monthly.
We hire former restaurant operators onto our product team.
Anti-values: We will not add features just because competitors have them.
We will not hide pricing.
Purpose: Internal culture doc plus investor deck language
```

## Output Format

```
1. VISION STATEMENT
   1 to 2 sentences. Describes a changed world, not a company achievement.
   Written so that any person inside or outside the company understands it
   without context.

2. MISSION STATEMENT
   1 to 3 sentences. Answers: what you do, for whom, how, and why it matters.
   Present tense. Specific enough to exclude things that are not you.

3. COMPANY VALUES (4 to 7 values)
   For each value:
   - Value headline (a sentence or phrase, not a noun)
   - 2 to 3 sentence description of what this looks like in practice
   - One concrete example: "This means we..." or "You will see this when..."

4. INTERNAL CONSISTENCY CHECK
   Does the mission serve the vision?
   Do the values support the behavior required to execute the mission?
   Any tensions or contradictions between the three documents flagged.

5. ALTERNATIVE DRAFTS (optional)
   2 to 3 alternative versions of the vision and mission for comparison.
   Useful when the first draft does not feel right but it is hard to articulate why.

6. USAGE GUIDANCE
   Where and how each document should be used:
   vision (strategy, fundraising, long-horizon decisions),
   mission (day-to-day alignment, hiring, customer communication),
   values (performance reviews, hiring criteria, conflict resolution).
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are an organizational strategist and culture writer helping me craft
a vision statement, mission statement, and company values.

What our company does: [plain language description of product/service]
Who our customer is: [specific customer type, industry, role, situation]
Why we were founded: [the founding insight or frustration]
The world we want to create in 10 years: [what changes for customers and the market]
How we behave differently from others in our space: [3 to 5 specific behaviors]
Things we would never do even if profitable: [anti-values or deliberate trade-offs]
Existing documents (if refreshing): [what you have and what is not working]
Culture stories: [moments that felt distinctly "us"]
Employee descriptions of what makes us different: [verbatim quotes if available]
Purpose: [internal culture doc / investor deck / website / recruiting]

Please produce:
1. A vision statement (1 to 2 sentences, describes a changed world)
2. A mission statement (1 to 3 sentences, specific customer and outcome)
3. Four to seven company values written as behavioral sentences, each with
   a 2 to 3 sentence description and one concrete example
4. An internal consistency check across all three documents
5. Two alternative drafts of the vision and mission for comparison
6. Brief guidance on where and how to use each document

Avoid: the word "innovative," abstract value nouns, generic language
that could apply to any company, and aspirational claims that leadership
does not currently live up to.
Flag immediately if any input is too vague to produce a specific output.
```
