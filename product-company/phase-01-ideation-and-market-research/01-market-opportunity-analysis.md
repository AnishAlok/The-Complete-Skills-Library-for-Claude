# market-opportunity-analysis.md

> **Skill:** Structures TAM/SAM/SOM analysis and market sizing frameworks to evaluate the size, attractiveness, and accessibility of a market opportunity.

---

## ⚡ TLDR

This skill helps you answer one of the most important early questions any founder, PM, or strategist faces: *"Is this market worth pursuing, and how big can we actually get?"* It produces a structured, defensible market sizing document using TAM/SAM/SOM methodology, layered with qualitative signals about market attractiveness and competitive density. The output is board-ready, investor-ready, and strategy-ready.

---

## 🧠 Topic Explanation

### What Is Market Opportunity Analysis?

Market Opportunity Analysis is the process of estimating the size of a potential market, understanding who the addressable customers are, and determining what realistic share of that market a company can capture given its current resources, positioning, and go-to-market approach.

It answers three core questions:
1. **How big is the total universe of demand?** (TAM)
2. **How much of that can we realistically reach?** (SAM)
3. **How much can we win in the near term?** (SOM)

This analysis is foundational to product strategy, fundraising, resource allocation, and prioritization decisions. It is one of the most frequently requested documents by investors, boards, and executive stakeholders.

---

### The Core Framework: TAM / SAM / SOM

#### TAM — Total Addressable Market
The total global revenue opportunity if 100% of all potential buyers purchased your product/service at your price point. This is the theoretical ceiling.

- **Top-down approach:** Start from industry analyst reports (Gartner, IDC, Forrester, Grand View Research) and identify the total market size for the category.
- **Bottom-up approach:** Count all potential customers in the world × average revenue per customer per year.
- **Value theory approach:** Estimate total economic value your solution creates, then model your capture rate.

> Example: If you're building project management software for construction firms, the TAM would be all construction firms globally × their average annual spend on project management tooling.

#### SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market
The portion of TAM you can actually reach given your product's current features, geography, language, sales channels, pricing, and business model. This is the realistic target universe.

- Filter TAM by: geography served, customer segments your product fits, languages supported, regulatory constraints, distribution reach.
- SAM is typically 10–40% of TAM for an early-stage company.

> Example: If you only serve English-speaking construction firms in North America with a SaaS model priced at $500/month, your SAM narrows dramatically from global TAM.

#### SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market
The portion of SAM you can realistically capture in the next 3–5 years given your team size, sales capacity, marketing budget, competitive position, and growth rate. This is your actual near-term target.

- SOM is typically 1–10% of SAM for an early-stage company.
- Should be grounded in unit economics: how many deals can your sales team close per quarter? What's your expected CAC and ramp time?

> Example: With a 5-person sales team, $2M marketing budget, and a 6-month sales cycle, you might realistically close 80 accounts in Year 1, representing $X in ARR.

### Supporting Frameworks

#### Market Attractiveness Scoring (MSPOT)
Beyond size, evaluate market attractiveness across five dimensions:
- **M**arket size & growth rate
- **S**tructural factors (fragmentation, consolidation, regulatory trends)
- **P**rofit potential (margin profile, pricing power)
- **O**pportunity for differentiation (whitespace, underserved segments)
- **T**iming (is the market ready? too early? too late?)

#### Porter's Market Readiness Test
Before investing in a market, assess:
- Buyer sophistication and willingness to pay
- Presence of a clear "pain trigger" (event that makes buyers act)
- Budget ownership clarity (who controls the spend?)
- Switching costs from incumbent solutions

#### Market Growth Rate Signals
- **Declining market (<0% CAGR):** Requires disruption thesis; high risk unless you're taking share from incumbents
- **Slow growth (1–5% CAGR):** Stable but competitive; need strong differentiation
- **Moderate growth (5–15% CAGR):** Sweet spot for most B2B SaaS
- **High growth (15–30% CAGR):** Attractive but attracts competition fast
- **Hypergrowth (>30% CAGR):** Land-grab dynamics; speed matters more than margin

### Common Market Sizing Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Matters | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Using only top-down TAM | Makes you look naive to investors | Always pair with bottom-up validation |
| Assuming % of a huge market | "If we get 1% of a $10B market..." is a red flag | Build from unit economics up |
| Confusing TAM with SAM | Makes opportunity look inflated | Explicitly define your ICP and filter accordingly |
| Ignoring CAGR direction | A shrinking market is a trap | Always include 5-year growth rate |
| No competitive density analysis | Market size means nothing if it's locked up | Include incumbent market share |
| Citing one analyst report | Single-source sizing is fragile | Triangulate 3+ sources |

## 🎯 When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when:

- You are **evaluating whether to build** a new product or enter a new market
- You are **preparing a fundraising pitch** (Seed, Series A, Series B)
- You are **presenting to the board** on market expansion
- You are **prioritizing which customer segment** to go after first
- You are **writing a business case** for a new product investment internally
- You are **responding to an investor's due diligence** request
- You are **stress-testing** whether your current market is large enough to justify your growth targets
- You are **evaluating an acquisition target** and need to understand their market ceiling

Do NOT use this skill when:
- You need a competitor feature-by-feature comparison (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)
- You need customer-level insights (use `customer-discovery-interview.md`)
- You need to define product strategy (use `product-strategy-doc.md`) — complete this skill first as an input

## ✅ Dos

- **Do triangulate.** Use at least 3 independent sources: one industry analyst report, one bottom-up calculation, and one comparable company benchmark.
- **Do show your math.** Every number in your TAM/SAM/SOM should have a visible calculation chain. Never present a number without showing how you got there.
- **Do segment the market.** Break TAM into sub-segments. Different segments grow at different rates, have different willingness to pay, and require different GTM motions.
- **Do use real unit economics in SOM.** Ground your SOM in your sales capacity, CAC, sales cycle, and team size — not just a percentage.
- **Do cite sources with dates.** Market data goes stale. Always include the source, year, and methodology.
- **Do include a growth rate.** A $5B market growing at 25% CAGR is far more attractive than a $10B market growing at 2%.
- **Do define your ICP explicitly.** Your SAM is only as good as your Ideal Customer Profile definition. Be specific about firmographics, geographies, and behaviors.
- **Do acknowledge uncertainty.** Ranges are more credible than false precision. "We estimate SAM at $800M–$1.2B" is more trustworthy than "$1B."
- **Do include a market map.** A visual showing where incumbents play, where whitespace exists, and where you fit dramatically improves communication.
- **Do consider adjacent markets.** Show the expansion path: what's the next market you'd enter after dominating your beachhead?

---

## ❌ Don'ts

- **Don't use "1% of a giant market" logic.** This is the single biggest red flag in any market sizing document. It signals no real bottom-up thinking.
- **Don't ignore market concentration.** If 3 players control 80% of the market, your accessible opportunity is much smaller than the headline TAM.
- **Don't conflate market size with revenue potential.** Your revenue is always a fraction of market size — be explicit about pricing model and capture assumptions.
- **Don't use outdated data.** A 2019 industry report in a 2024 analysis is a credibility killer. Seek data from the last 24 months.
- **Don't skip the "why now" framing.** Market size alone isn't enough. You need to explain why this market is capturable NOW — what tailwind, regulation, technology shift, or behavioral change has opened the window.
- **Don't assume global from day one.** Most companies should define a beachhead geography and show that market is meaningful before expanding the TAM narrative.
- **Don't omit market growth rate.** Size without trajectory is half the story.
- **Don't present SAM without an ICP.** SAM requires a clearly defined customer segment. Presenting SAM without ICP definition is hand-waving.
- **Don't make SOM a round number.** "We'll capture 5% of SAM" with no supporting logic is not credible. Build SOM from the bottom up.
- **Don't forget to show the path from SOM → SAM → TAM over time.** Your expansion roadmap should make the full TAM believable, even if you start narrow.

## 📥 Ideal Inputs — What to Give Claude for Best Output

The more context you provide, the more accurate and specific the output. Here is what to include in your prompt:

### Required Inputs
```
1. PRODUCT/SERVICE DESCRIPTION
   What does your product/service do? Who does it help? What problem does it solve?
   Example: "B2B SaaS platform that automates accounts payable reconciliation for mid-market manufacturers."

2. TARGET CUSTOMER DEFINITION (ICP)
   Who is your ideal customer? Include: industry, company size, geography, role/buyer, behavior.
   Example: "CFOs and Controllers at US-based manufacturing companies with $50M–$500M revenue."

3. PRICING MODEL
   How do you charge? Per seat, usage-based, flat subscription, % of transaction, etc.
   Example: "$2,000/month per company, all-inclusive SaaS."

4. CURRENT STAGE
   Are you pre-product, MVP, post-launch, scaling? This affects how conservative SOM should be.

5. GEOGRAPHIC FOCUS
   Where are you operating or planning to operate in the next 3 years?
```

### Optional but High-Value Inputs
```
6. KNOWN COMPETITORS
   Who are the main players you know about? This helps calibrate market concentration.

7. EXISTING DATA POINTS
   Any market research, analyst quotes, or data points you've already found.

8. USE CASE / PURPOSE
   Is this for a fundraising deck, internal strategy, board presentation, or business case?
   This affects tone, depth, and framing.

9. COMPARABLE COMPANIES
   Companies in adjacent spaces whose public market data can benchmark your sizing.

10. KEY ASSUMPTIONS YOU WANT TO TEST
    Are there specific hypotheses you want the analysis to validate or challenge?
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Product: SaaS platform that automates AP reconciliation for manufacturers
ICP: CFOs/Controllers at US manufacturers, $50M–$500M revenue, currently using Excel or legacy ERP
Pricing: $2,000/month flat SaaS fee
Stage: 12 customers, $288K ARR, pre-Series A
Geography: US only for next 3 years, Europe Year 4+
Competitors: SAP, Oracle NetSuite, Tipalti, BILL.com
Purpose: Series A fundraising deck
I want bottom-up + top-down sizing, with CAGR, and a clear ICP-filtered SAM.
```

## 📤 Output Format

A complete market opportunity analysis produced by this skill should contain:

```
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (3–5 sentences)
   — Market size, growth rate, your opportunity, one-line "why now"

2. MARKET DEFINITION
   — Clear definition of the market category
   — Scope boundaries (what's in/out)
   — ICP definition used as SAM filter

3. TAM ANALYSIS
   — Top-down calculation with sources
   — Bottom-up calculation with unit economics
   — Reconciliation of the two approaches
   — Market growth rate (CAGR) with source

4. SAM ANALYSIS
   — ICP-filtered segment definition
   — Sizing methodology
   — Geographic and product scope constraints applied
   — SAM as % of TAM with rationale

5. SOM ANALYSIS
   — 3-year and 5-year SOM projection
   — Built from sales capacity, CAC, deal size
   — SOM as % of SAM with rationale

6. MARKET ATTRACTIVENESS ASSESSMENT
   — Growth rate and trajectory
   — Competitive density
   — Whitespace / underserved segments
   — Tailwinds (technology, regulation, behavior)
   — Risks and headwinds

7. MARKET MAP (described or visual)
   — Where incumbents play
   — Where whitespace exists
   — Where you are positioned

8. EXPANSION PATH
   — How you move from beachhead SOM to SAM to TAM over 5–10 years

9. SOURCES & ASSUMPTIONS LOG
   — All data sources cited with year
   — All key assumptions explicitly listed
```

```
- TAM using both top-down (with industry sources cited) and bottom-up (unit economics) approaches

```
