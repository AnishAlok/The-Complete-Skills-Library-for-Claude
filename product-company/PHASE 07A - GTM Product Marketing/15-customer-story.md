# customer-story.md

> **Skill:** Produces a customer story in narrative format that brings a customer's journey to life through character, context, and transformation for use in keynotes, thought leadership, and brand content.

---

## TLDR

A customer story is a narrative-driven form of customer evidence that goes deeper than a case study. Where a case study is structured and results-focused, a customer story is written as a compelling narrative with a protagonist, a conflict, a turning point, and a resolution. Used in keynotes, video scripts, thought leadership articles, and brand content where emotional engagement matters alongside proof.

---

## Topic Explanation

### Case Study vs. Customer Story

A case study answers: "Did it work?" A customer story answers: "What was it like?"

Case studies serve the evaluation stage when buyers need proof. Customer stories work earlier in the funnel, at events, and in brand content where the goal is emotional connection and aspiration rather than technical evidence.

The same customer can appear in both formats. The case study documents the measurable outcomes. The story documents the human experience of getting there.

### The Protagonist Must Be a Person

The most common customer story failure: the customer is a company, not a person. Companies do not have experiences. People do.

"Acme Corp reduced its processing time" is data. "Sarah, the only operations manager at a 200-person company trying to run a process designed for a team twice her size, finally got her Friday afternoons back" is a story.

Every customer story needs a named human protagonist with a specific job, a specific challenge, and a specific transformation.

## Output Format

```
CUSTOMER STORY

Headline: [Character-driven or transformation-driven.
Evokes the journey, not just the outcome.]

[Opening paragraph: establish the protagonist and their world before the product.
Be specific: not "a marketing manager" but "the sole marketing hire at a 40-person
logistics startup responsible for everything from paid ads to quarterly reports,
all while supporting a sales team of six who needed materials yesterday."]

[The problem paragraph: what specifically was not working.
What did the protagonist try before? Why did it not solve the problem?
Include the emotional dimension: how did it make them feel?]

[The turning point: how they found or decided to try the product.
What were they skeptical about? What made them try it anyway?]

[The experience: what happened when they started using it.
What surprised them? What changed first? What took longer than expected?
Make this feel real and specific, not like a brochure.]

[The result: what is different now. Include specific outcomes where available,
but told as story ("the report that used to take her Friday afternoon
now takes 20 minutes on Thursday morning") rather than as bullets.]

[The customer's voice: their words, as they would actually say them.
Not polished corporate language. Specific and human.]
"[Quote in the customer's natural voice]"
[Full Name], [Title], [Company]

[Closing: what is possible now that was not before.
Where the protagonist goes from here. End on aspiration.]
```

---

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a product marketer writing a customer story in narrative format.

Customer protagonist: [name, title, company, what their job involves day to day]
Their world before: [their specific situation, team size, challenges]
The specific problem: [what was not working and why it mattered to them personally]
How they found the product: [what prompted the decision]
The experience: [what it was like to use it, what surprised them]
The outcomes: [what changed, specific results where available]
Their voice: [a direct quote or the essence of what they would say]

Write a narrative customer story between 600 and 900 words.
The protagonist must be a specific named person, not a company.
Lead with their world and their problem before introducing the product.
The tone must feel personal and real, not like marketing copy.
```
