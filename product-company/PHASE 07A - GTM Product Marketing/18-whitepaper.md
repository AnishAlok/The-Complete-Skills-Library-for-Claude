# whitepaper.md

> **Skill:** Produces a long-form thought leadership whitepaper that argues a specific point of view on a market problem, presents evidence, and positions the company as an authoritative voice in the category.

---

## TLDR

A whitepaper is a 2,500 to 5,000-word authoritative document arguing for a specific point of view on a market problem: the problem is real and larger than people realize, current approaches are failing, and a new approach exists. Used for demand generation, analyst relations, and establishing category leadership. The key distinction from a brochure: a whitepaper must be worth reading even if the reader never buys the product.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Separates a Whitepaper from a Brochure

A brochure describes a product. A whitepaper argues a position about a market problem.

The best whitepapers advance the reader's understanding of a topic and would be worth reading independent of any commercial relationship. This counterintuitive principle is what makes them effective: genuine intellectual value generates trust, earns attention, gets shared, and eventually drives pipeline.

A whitepaper that is thinly veiled product marketing gets ignored. A whitepaper that teaches something new gets read, cited, and shared.

### The Whitepaper Argument Structure

1. The problem exists and is larger than people realize. (Data and evidence.)
2. Current approaches are failing, and here is specifically why. (Rigorous analysis.)
3. There is a better way: the insight or framework. (The intellectual contribution.)
4. Here is how to implement it. (Actionable guidance.)
5. Organizations doing this are getting ahead. (Social proof and evidence.)
6. Here is how to get started. (The product, introduced late and lightly.)

The company's product appears in steps 4 through 6 as an example of the better way, not as the subject of the paper.

## Output Format

```
WHITEPAPER

Title: [Problem-led or insight-led. Promises intellectual value, not product features.]
Subtitle: [One sentence expanding on the promise]

ABSTRACT (100 words)
[The argument in miniature: the problem, why current approaches fail, the key insight,
and what the reader will learn. Written to convince the reader the paper is worth their time.]

SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM (500 to 800 words)
[Establish that the problem is real, significant, and costly.
Use data from reputable sources. Define the problem precisely.
Quantify the cost or consequence. This section should be alarming but not hyperbolic.]

SECTION 2: WHY CURRENT APPROACHES FAIL (600 to 900 words)
[Analyze why the current state of practice is insufficient.
Be specific about the failure modes, not just generically critical.
This is the intellectual heart of the paper. Rigor here builds credibility.]

SECTION 3: A NEW FRAMEWORK OR APPROACH (700 to 900 words)
[Introduce the insight or framework that resolves the problem.
Name the framework. Make it memorable and teachable.
The reader should feel like they are learning something genuinely new and applicable.]

SECTION 4: HOW LEADING ORGANIZATIONS ARE DOING THIS (500 to 700 words)
[Concrete evidence of the approach working in practice.
Customer examples, research findings, or case data.
The company's product is introduced here as one example of how to implement the framework.]

SECTION 5: HOW TO GET STARTED (400 to 600 words)
[Practical, actionable guidance for readers who want to act.
Specific steps or a decision framework.
CTA for readers who want help implementing: the product as a solution.]

CONCLUSION (200 to 300 words)
[Restate the argument arc. Strong call to action for interested readers.]

ABOUT THE RESEARCH (if primary research was conducted)
[Methodology, sample size, and dates]

ABOUT [COMPANY]
[2 to 3 sentence boilerplate. Keep commercial. The paper earns credibility; the boilerplate converts it.]
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a product marketer writing a thought leadership whitepaper.

Topic: [the market problem or category trend]
Central argument: [the specific, arguable claim you want to make]
Target reader: [role and industry]
Evidence available: [data, research, customer examples to cite]
The framework or insight: [the new way of thinking you are proposing]
Current approach failures: [specifically why the status quo is inadequate]
Company's role: [how the product enables the proposed approach]

Write a complete whitepaper outline with the abstract written in full.
The paper must make a specific, arguable claim, not just describe a trend.
The company's product must appear late and lightly, as evidence not as subject.
Every factual claim requires a cited source.
```
