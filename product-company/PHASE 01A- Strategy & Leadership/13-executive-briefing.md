# executive-briefing.md

> **Skill:** Produces concise, high-signal executive briefing documents and board-ready summaries that communicate complex situations, decisions, or analyses to senior leaders in a format optimized for fast comprehension and clear action.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"How do I communicate this complex situation or analysis to an executive or board in a way that respects their time and drives a clear decision?"* It produces a structured, one-to-two-page executive briefing document using the Pyramid Principle: conclusion first, supporting logic second, detail on request. Used for board presentations, CEO briefings, investor updates, cross-functional decision memos, and escalation documents.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is an Executive Briefing?

An executive briefing is a written document designed to give a senior leader or governing body (board, investors, executive committee) the essential information needed to understand a situation, make a decision, or approve a recommendation, in the shortest possible time with the highest possible signal-to-noise ratio.

It is not a full report. It is not a deck of slides. It is the written equivalent of a 5-minute executive conversation where you walk in, state the situation, give your recommendation, explain why, and ask for what you need. Everything else is appendix.

### Why Most Executive Briefings Fail

Most briefings fail for one of four reasons:

**Burying the lead.** The conclusion comes on page 4 after three pages of context. Executives are time-constrained and context-switch constantly. If the point is not in the first paragraph, many will never reach it.

**Confusing activity with impact.** Briefings that describe what the team did rather than what changed or what it means for the business. Executives care about outcomes and decisions, not activity reports.

**Providing data without interpretation.** Long tables of metrics with no narrative interpretation force the executive to do the analytical work themselves. The writer's job is to tell them what the data means.

**Asking for no specific action.** A briefing that informs but does not specify what the executive needs to do, decide, or approve has failed its primary purpose.

### The Pyramid Principle

The Pyramid Principle, developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1970s, is the foundational structure for executive communication. It states that all written business communication should start with the top of the pyramid: the single governing thought or conclusion. Supporting arguments sit below it. Evidence and detail sit below those.

In practical terms this means:
- Paragraph 1: The situation, the problem or opportunity, and your recommendation in 3 to 5 sentences
- Body: The 3 to 5 supporting arguments, each with its evidence
- Appendix: The full detail for anyone who wants to go deeper

This structure works because it matches how executives actually read: they form their own conclusion quickly from the opening and then scan for the supporting evidence that confirms or challenges it.

### The SCQA Framework

An alternative structure for executive briefings when the situation is complex or contentious:

**S - Situation:** The stable, agreed-upon context. What is the current state that everyone accepts as true? This anchors the reader.

**C - Complication:** The development that creates tension or urgency. What has changed, emerged, or been discovered that makes the situation require attention? This is the "but..."

**Q - Question:** The question that the complication raises. What must now be decided or resolved?

**A - Answer:** Your recommended answer to that question. This is the conclusion the briefing is built to support.

SCQA is particularly useful when the audience does not yet know why they need to read this document.

### What Belongs in an Executive Briefing vs. an Appendix

**In the briefing (1 to 2 pages):**
- Situation summary
- The specific problem, opportunity, or decision required
- Recommendation or key findings
- 3 to 5 supporting points, each in 1 to 3 sentences
- The ask: what do you need the executive to do, decide, or approve?
- Key risks or trade-offs in 2 to 3 bullet points

**In the appendix (available on request):**
- Full data tables and analysis
- Methodology documentation
- Detailed financial models
- Meeting notes and research sources
- Alternative options considered and rejected

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Preparing a board update on a strategic initiative, risk, or decision
- Briefing a CEO or exec committee on a situation that requires their decision
- Communicating a significant finding from a research project or data analysis
- Summarizing a complex strategic analysis (SWOT, market analysis, competitive landscape)
  into an executive-ready format
- Escalating an issue that requires senior leadership intervention
- Sending a pre-read document before a leadership meeting
- Creating an investor update memo
- Writing a decision memo for a cross-functional initiative requiring sign-off

Do NOT use when:
- You need a full strategic plan document (use `strategic-planning-doc.md`)
- You need a detailed project status report (use `project-status-report.md`)
- You need a full investor pitch deck (use `pitch-deck.md`)
- The audience needs the full detail to make the decision (in which case the briefing
  should point to the detailed document, not replace it)

## Dos

- **Do lead with the conclusion.** The first paragraph must contain the situation, the implication, and your recommendation. If a reader stops after paragraph one, they should know what you are asking for and why.
- **Do write the ask explicitly.** Every executive briefing must end with a clear statement of what you need: a decision, an approval, awareness, or a specific action by a specific date.
- **Do use the active voice throughout.** "Revenue declined 12% in Q3" is cleaner and more credible than "It was observed that there was a 12% decline in revenue during the Q3 period."
- **Do calibrate length to the audience and urgency.** A CEO briefing for a routine decision can be one page. A board briefing on a material risk may need two. The discipline is to never go longer than necessary.
- **Do number your supporting points.** Three numbered supporting arguments are faster to scan than three paragraphs of continuous prose.
- **Do include the so what for every data point.** Do not write "NPS dropped from 45 to 31." Write "NPS dropped from 45 to 31, driven primarily by onboarding friction that our customer interviews indicate is causing 30% of new accounts to underuse the product in month one."
- **Do state trade-offs honestly.** If the recommended action has a cost or risk, name it. Executives who discover unstated trade-offs after approving something lose trust in the briefer.
- **Do reference where the full detail lives.** "Full analysis is in Appendix A" gives the executive permission to go deeper if needed without requiring it.
- **Do write it to be readable without verbal context.** Executive briefings are often forwarded, shared with boards, or read weeks later. Every document must stand alone.
- **Do use a consistent header structure.** Situation / Recommendation / Supporting rationale / Ask / Risks. Executives who read briefings frequently scan for these headers automatically.

---

## Donts

- **Dont start with background.** Three paragraphs of context before reaching the point is the most common executive briefing failure mode. Context goes in a single sentence in the opening paragraph or in an appendix.
- **Dont present data without interpretation.** Raw metrics are not insight. Every number needs a "this means..." statement.
- **Dont use passive voice or hedge language.** "It may be considered that perhaps..." signals lack of confidence and wastes words. Write declaratively.
- **Dont include options if you have a recommendation.** Presenting three equally weighted options and asking the executive to choose is not briefing them. It is delegating the analysis back to them. Do the analysis, make a recommendation, and present alternatives only in the appendix.
- **Dont write for the lowest common denominator.** Executive briefings are written for a specific audience. Know who will read it and calibrate the assumed knowledge level accordingly.
- **Dont pad with unnecessary context to demonstrate effort.** Length signals effort only to junior audiences. Brevity signals confidence and analytical rigor to senior ones.
- **Dont bury risks or bad news.** Executives who discover bad news that was softened or buried in the briefing lose confidence in the writer permanently. State uncomfortable facts directly.
- **Dont use jargon without defining it.** Even in technical organizations, not every executive has the same domain knowledge. Define acronyms on first use. Avoid industry jargon when plain language works equally well.
- **Dont forget to date and version the document.** Executive briefings are often distributed, forwarded, and referenced later. Always include date, version, and author.
- **Dont write the conclusion last.** Write the recommendation first, then write the briefing that supports it. Documents written bottom-up often bury the conclusion. Documents written top-down lead with it.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE SITUATION
   What is the current state that the reader needs to understand?
   What has happened or what has been discovered?

2. THE PROBLEM OR OPPORTUNITY
   What does the situation create: a problem to be solved, a decision
   to be made, a risk to be managed, or an opportunity to be captured?

3. YOUR RECOMMENDATION
   What do you recommend the executive or board do?
   This is the conclusion of the briefing.

4. THE SUPPORTING RATIONALE
   The 3 to 5 most important reasons your recommendation is correct.
   Each reason with its supporting evidence.

5. THE ASK
   Specifically: what do you need the reader to do, decide, or approve?
   By when?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. KEY RISKS OR TRADE-OFFS
   What are the 2 to 3 most important risks or trade-offs in
   your recommendation that the executive needs to be aware of?

7. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED
   What other options were evaluated and why they were not recommended?
   This goes in the appendix but should be referenced in the briefing.

8. AUDIENCE
   Who specifically is reading this? CEO, full board, exec committee,
   specific investor? This shapes assumed knowledge and tone.

9. CONTEXT
   Is this routine (quarterly update) or urgent (escalation)?
   Is this the first time the audience is hearing about this situation
   or do they already have background?

10. SUPPORTING DOCUMENTS
    What full analyses, reports, or data exist that this briefing summarizes?
    These become the appendix.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Situation: Our enterprise tier launch in Q3 missed its target by 60%.
We signed 3 enterprise deals vs. a target of 8. Analysis reveals
two root causes: sales team is not equipped to navigate enterprise procurement,
and our security documentation is insufficient for enterprise IT review.
Problem: At this miss rate, we will not hit the ARR milestone required
for our Series B in Q2 next year.
Recommendation: Pause enterprise new business for 8 weeks, invest in
security certification (SOC 2 Type II) and sales enablement, relaunch in Q1.
Supporting rationale:
- All 5 lost deals cited the same two blockers (security docs, procurement navigation)
- SOC 2 Type II can be completed in 6 weeks with current infrastructure
- 3 of the 5 lost prospects have confirmed they will re-engage once SOC 2 is in place
- The cost is $45K and 2 weeks of engineering time vs. $1.8M ARR risk to Series B
Risks: 8-week pause delays pipeline, competitive risk if Drata closes these prospects
Ask: Board approval to redirect $45K from marketing budget to SOC 2 certification
and to pause enterprise new business outreach for 8 weeks
Audience: Board of directors, pre-read for Q3 board meeting
```

## Output Format

```
1. DOCUMENT HEADER
   Title, date, author, version, audience, classification (confidential/internal)

2. SITUATION AND RECOMMENDATION (opening paragraph, 3 to 5 sentences)
   Situation: what is happening
   Implication: why it matters
   Recommendation: what you are proposing
   This paragraph must stand alone as a complete summary.

3. SUPPORTING RATIONALE (3 to 5 numbered points)
   Each point: 2 to 4 sentences maximum
   Each point includes the finding and its implication
   Data cited inline, not in separate tables

4. THE ASK (1 to 3 sentences)
   What specifically do you need: decision, approval, awareness, action?
   By when is this needed?
   What is the consequence of delay?

5. KEY RISKS AND TRADE-OFFS (2 to 4 bullet points)
   The most important risks or downsides of the recommendation
   Each with a one-sentence mitigation or acknowledgment

6. APPENDIX REFERENCE (1 sentence)
   What full analyses, data, or documents are available for those who want more detail

7. APPENDIX (attached separately or labeled clearly)
   Full supporting analysis, data tables, methodology, alternatives considered
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are an executive communications specialist helping me write a concise,
high-signal executive briefing document using the Pyramid Principle.

Situation: [what is happening or has happened]
Problem or opportunity: [what the situation creates: decision needed, risk, opportunity]
Recommendation: [what you are proposing the executive or board do]
Supporting rationale: [the 3 to 5 most important reasons your recommendation is right,
with evidence for each]
Key risks or trade-offs: [the 2 to 3 most important downsides to acknowledge]
The ask: [specifically what you need the reader to do, decide, or approve, and by when]
Audience: [who will read this: CEO, board, exec committee, investor]
Context: [routine update / urgent escalation / first time they are hearing this]
Supporting documents available: [what full analyses exist that this briefing summarizes]

Please produce:
1. A complete executive briefing document of 1 to 2 pages maximum
2. An opening paragraph that contains the situation, implication, and recommendation
   in 3 to 5 sentences
3. Three to five numbered supporting points, each 2 to 4 sentences with evidence
4. A clear, specific ask statement
5. Two to four risk/trade-off bullet points
6. A reference to appendix materials

Apply the Pyramid Principle: conclusion first, then supporting logic, then evidence.
Use active voice throughout. No jargon without definition.
If I have buried the lead or included unnecessary context, restructure it.
If the ask is vague, ask me to clarify what decision or action I actually need.
```
