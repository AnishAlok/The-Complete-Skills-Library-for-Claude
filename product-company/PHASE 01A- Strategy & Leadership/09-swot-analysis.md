# swot-analysis.md

> **Skill:** Generates a rigorous, insight-rich SWOT analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) with strategic implications drawn from each quadrant and a clear action framework based on the findings.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"Where do we stand right now and what does that mean for our strategy?"* It produces a complete SWOT analysis that goes beyond a four-box list of bullet points. It identifies meaningful strengths, honest weaknesses, genuine opportunities, and credible threats, and then synthesizes them into strategic implications using cross-quadrant analysis (SO, ST, WO, WT strategies). Used for strategic planning, board presentations, investor prep, and competitive reviews.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is a SWOT Analysis?

SWOT analysis is a strategic planning framework that evaluates a company (or product, initiative, or decision) across four dimensions:

- **Strengths:** Internal attributes and resources that give you an advantage
- **Weaknesses:** Internal attributes and gaps that put you at a disadvantage
- **Opportunities:** External conditions that you could exploit to your advantage
- **Threats:** External conditions that could harm your position or performance

The two-by-two structure is simple. The hard part is doing it rigorously. Most SWOT analyses fail because they produce obvious, generic bullet points ("our strength is our team," "our threat is competition") that generate no insight and lead to no action.

A useful SWOT analysis is specific, honest, evidence-based, and synthesis-driven.

### Internal vs. External

The most important structural distinction in SWOT is internal vs. external:

**Internal (within your control):**
- Strengths: what you have or do well
- Weaknesses: what you lack or do poorly

**External (outside your control):**
- Opportunities: conditions in the market you could take advantage of
- Threats: conditions in the market that could harm you

A common mistake is placing external factors in the internal quadrants and vice versa. "Our pricing is higher than competitors" is a weakness (internal). "Customers are becoming more price-sensitive" is a threat (external). They are related but they belong in different quadrants.

### The TOWS Matrix: Turning SWOT into Strategy

Raw SWOT analysis produces observations. The TOWS Matrix turns those observations into strategic options by crossing the quadrants:

**SO Strategies (Strengths + Opportunities)**
How do we use our strengths to capture the opportunities available?
These are your most aggressive growth strategies.

**ST Strategies (Strengths + Threats)**
How do we use our strengths to defend against or neutralize threats?
These are your competitive defense strategies.

**WO Strategies (Weaknesses + Opportunities)**
How do we address our weaknesses so we can take advantage of opportunities?
These are your investment and capability-building strategies.

**WT Strategies (Weaknesses + Threats)**
How do we minimize weaknesses and avoid threats?
These are your risk mitigation and defensive strategies.

The TOWS Matrix is what separates a useful SWOT from a pretty four-box diagram. It is the analytical step most teams skip.

### What Makes a SWOT Analysis Rigorous

**Specificity:** "Strong customer relationships" is generic. "94% net revenue retention with an average of 4.2 product seats per account expanding to 7.8 by month 12" is specific.

**Evidence:** Every item should be backed by data, customer feedback, competitive intelligence, or documented experience. Opinion-based SWOT generates opinion-based strategy.

**Honesty:** Weaknesses and threats are where most SWOT analyses fail. Teams downplay weaknesses and dismiss threats. A SWOT that does not make leadership uncomfortable is probably not honest enough.

**Prioritization:** Not all strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats are equal. The top 3 to 5 in each quadrant are more useful than an exhaustive list of 20.

**Strategic implication:** Every quadrant should end with "and therefore..." statements that connect observations to decisions.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Beginning an annual strategic planning cycle and needing a current state assessment
- Evaluating whether to enter a new market or launch a new product
- Preparing for a board discussion on competitive position and strategic options
- Onboarding a new executive who needs a rapid orientation to the company's position
- Conducting a post-mortem on a lost deal, missed target, or failed initiative
- Evaluating an acquisition target or partnership candidate
- Preparing an investor update that includes discussion of strategic position

Do NOT use when:
- You need a full strategic plan (use `strategic-planning-doc.md` - SWOT is an input)
- You need a detailed competitor analysis (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)
- You need market sizing data (use `market-opportunity-analysis.md`)
- You need a risk register for a specific project (use `risk-register.md`)

## Dos

- **Do be specific in every quadrant.** Replace vague observations with data, customer quotes, and named examples. Specificity is what turns SWOT from decoration into strategy input.
- **Do be brutally honest about weaknesses.** The weaknesses quadrant is where most SWOT analyses sanitize themselves into uselessness. Name the real gaps: technology debt, team capability gaps, customer dissatisfaction data, retention problems.
- **Do use external evidence for opportunities and threats.** Opportunities and threats should be grounded in market data, trend signals, competitor moves, and regulatory developments, not gut feelings.
- **Do prioritize.** Rank items within each quadrant by strategic importance. The top 3 in each box are what the strategy needs to address.
- **Do complete the TOWS Matrix.** Cross-quadrant analysis is what generates actionable strategy. Do not stop at the four-box list.
- **Do involve multiple perspectives.** Sales sees threats customers mention. Engineering sees internal weaknesses. Leadership sees opportunities. A SWOT built from one perspective misses too much.
- **Do date the analysis.** SWOT is a point-in-time snapshot. Label it clearly so future readers know when it was conducted.
- **Do connect SWOT outputs to specific strategic decisions.** Each major SO, ST, WO, or WT strategy should map to a specific priority, investment, or decision in the strategic plan.
- **Do distinguish between genuine strengths and assumed strengths.** A strength only counts if it translates into competitive advantage with customers. Internal capabilities customers do not care about are not strategic strengths.

---

## Donts

- **Dont list generic items that could apply to any company.** "Experienced team," "strong culture," and "competitive product" are meaningless unless backed by specific evidence of why they translate into advantage.
- **Dont confuse weaknesses with threats.** Weaknesses are internal. Threats are external. A rising competitor is a threat. Your slow product development cycle is a weakness. They are related but must be analyzed separately.
- **Dont make the weaknesses quadrant a token gesture.** If your weaknesses section is three times shorter than your strengths section, you are not being honest. Real SWOT analysis is uncomfortable.
- **Dont skip the TOWS Matrix.** A SWOT without strategic synthesis is an observation exercise, not a strategy tool.
- **Dont treat SWOT as a standalone deliverable.** SWOT is an input to strategic planning. The output of a SWOT is strategic options and priorities, not the SWOT itself.
- **Dont include items in opportunities that are just wishful thinking.** Opportunities must be real, external conditions you can realistically act on given your resources and position.
- **Dont list 20 items in each quadrant.** More is not more rigorous. Three to five prioritized, specific items per quadrant is more useful than an exhaustive list.
- **Dont present SWOT as a democratic exercise where everyone's input is equal.** Synthesize and prioritize based on evidence, not political comfort.
- **Dont update the SWOT only annually.** Markets move fast. Competitive threats emerge quickly. Review and update the SWOT at least quarterly in high-velocity environments.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. COMPANY OR SUBJECT OF ANALYSIS
   Are you analyzing the whole company, a specific product,
   a market entry decision, or a specific initiative?

2. COMPANY CONTEXT
   Stage, revenue, team size, market category, key products.

3. KNOWN STRENGTHS
   What does the company genuinely do well compared to alternatives?
   Back up with data or evidence where possible.

4. KNOWN WEAKNESSES
   Where does the company fall short, struggle, or underperform?
   Be honest. Include things you might not want to say in public.

5. MARKET AND COMPETITIVE CONTEXT
   What is happening externally: competitor moves, market trends,
   regulatory changes, technology shifts, customer behavior changes?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. RECENT PERFORMANCE DATA
   Win/loss rates, churn data, NPS scores, growth rates,
   customer feedback themes. Evidence-based SWOT beats opinion-based SWOT.

7. COMPETITOR INTELLIGENCE
   Specific competitor strengths, recent moves, funding, and growth signals.

8. CUSTOMER FEEDBACK
   Themes from customer interviews, support tickets, NPS surveys,
   or churn conversations. Customers often name weaknesses you miss internally.

9. TEAM PERSPECTIVES
   What do sales, product, engineering, and CS see as the biggest gaps
   and opportunities? Cross-functional input strengthens the analysis.

10. PURPOSE AND AUDIENCE
    Is this for a board presentation, internal strategy session,
    investor prep, or a specific decision? Shapes depth and framing.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Subject: Full company SWOT for annual planning
Company: SaaS payroll platform for restaurant operators, $3.1M ARR, 34 employees, Series A
Strengths: 97% gross retention, purpose-built for restaurant-specific payroll compliance
(tip pooling, tipped minimum wage, spread-of-hours), strong NPS of 52, 40% of new business
from referrals
Weaknesses: No mobile app (competitors have one), engineering team of 4 is under-resourced,
brand awareness is near zero outside existing customer network, only integrates with Toast POS
Opportunities: 50% of target market still using manual payroll, new tip credit regulations
creating compliance urgency, ADP and Paychex both pulling back from restaurant segment,
restaurant tech consolidation creating integration partnership opportunities
Threats: Gusto building restaurant-specific features, Square Payroll improving hospitality
compliance, potential recession cutting restaurant margins and new software spend
Performance data: Churned 3 customers in Q3 due to missing integrations, won 8 competitive
deals in Q3 all displacing manual/spreadsheet processes, 0 deals won against Gusto
Purpose: Annual planning input for board and leadership team
```

## Output Format

```
1. CONTEXT STATEMENT
   One paragraph framing what is being analyzed, when,
   and what strategic decisions this analysis is meant to inform.

2. SWOT MATRIX

   STRENGTHS (internal advantages)
   For each item (3 to 5 prioritized):
   - The strength
   - The evidence or data behind it
   - Why it translates into competitive advantage

   WEAKNESSES (internal gaps)
   For each item (3 to 5 prioritized):
   - The weakness
   - The evidence or data behind it
   - The strategic cost of this weakness if unaddressed

   OPPORTUNITIES (external conditions to exploit)
   For each item (3 to 5 prioritized):
   - The opportunity
   - The evidence or market signal behind it
   - What would need to be true to capture it

   THREATS (external conditions to defend against)
   For each item (3 to 5 prioritized):
   - The threat
   - The evidence or competitive signal behind it
   - The likely impact if unaddressed

3. TOWS MATRIX (cross-quadrant strategic synthesis)

   SO Strategies: Use strengths to capture opportunities
   ST Strategies: Use strengths to defend against threats
   WO Strategies: Address weaknesses to capture opportunities
   WT Strategies: Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats

4. STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS
   The 3 to 5 most important decisions or priorities that this
   SWOT analysis points toward. Written as "Because of X, we should Y."

5. CRITICAL UNCERTAINTIES
   The 2 to 3 assumptions in the analysis that, if wrong,
   would change the strategic implications significantly.

6. RECOMMENDED ACTIONS
   Immediate actions (this quarter): things to act on now
   Short-term investments (this year): capabilities to build
   Risks to monitor: threats to watch with defined trigger conditions
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a strategic analyst helping me build a rigorous, evidence-based
SWOT analysis that will drive real strategic decisions.

Subject of analysis: [company / product / market entry / initiative]
Company context: [stage, revenue, team size, category]
Known strengths: [what we do well, with data or evidence]
Known weaknesses: [where we fall short, be honest]
Market and competitive context: [external trends, competitor moves, market signals]
Recent performance data: [win rates, churn, NPS, growth, customer feedback]
Competitor intelligence: [specific competitor moves or strengths]
Customer feedback themes: [what customers say about us and competitors]
Purpose and audience: [board / leadership team / investor prep / specific decision]

Please produce:
1. A rigorous SWOT matrix with 3 to 5 prioritized, specific, evidence-based items
   per quadrant
2. Evidence or data cited for every item, not just opinions
3. For weaknesses: the strategic cost if unaddressed, not just the observation
4. For threats: the likely impact and timeline if unaddressed
5. A complete TOWS Matrix with SO, ST, WO, and WT strategies
6. The 3 to 5 most important strategic implications in "because X, we should Y" format
7. Critical uncertainties that could change the analysis
8. Recommended actions split into immediate, short-term, and monitor categories

Be direct. If my stated strengths are not actually strategic advantages,
say so. If my weaknesses are more serious than I have acknowledged, name that.
If a threat is existential and I have not treated it that way, flag it clearly.
```
