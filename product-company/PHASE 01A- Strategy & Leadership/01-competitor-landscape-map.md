# competitor-landscape-map.md

> **Skill:** Builds a structured competitive landscape map using competitive matrix formats and positioning analysis to understand how competitors are positioned, where whitespace exists, and how to differentiate effectively.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"Who are we competing with, how are they positioned, and where is our best opportunity to stand out?"* It produces a structured competitive landscape document that maps all relevant players across meaningful dimensions, identifies gaps and whitespace, and gives you a clear picture of where your product fits and how to sharpen your differentiation. Output is useful for product strategy, sales enablement, investor decks, and GTM planning.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is a Competitive Landscape Map?

A competitive landscape map is a structured analysis of all companies competing for the same customer, budget, or problem-solution space as you. It goes beyond a simple list of competitors. It organizes players by positioning dimensions, identifies patterns in how the market has clustered, and surfaces strategic implications for your own product and go-to-market decisions.

Done well, a competitive landscape map is one of the most strategically useful documents a product or marketing team can maintain. Done poorly, it becomes a features spreadsheet that generates no insight.

### Types of Competitors to Include

Most teams only track direct competitors. A complete landscape map covers four types:

**Direct Competitors**
Same customer, same problem, similar solution. These are the players your prospects compare you against in deals.
Example: If you build project management software for construction, other construction PM tools are direct competitors.

**Indirect Competitors**
Same customer, same problem, different solution category. Customers choose between you and these alternatives even if they look nothing like you.
Example: Excel spreadsheets, email threads, or paper-based processes are indirect competitors to most SaaS tools.

**Substitute Competitors**
Different problem framing, but competing for the same budget or time.
Example: A "hire a consultant" option vs. buying your software.

**Emerging or Potential Competitors**
Companies not currently in your space but positioned to enter. Large adjacent players, well-funded startups, or platform companies who could add your feature.

### The Competitive Matrix

The competitive matrix is the core artifact of this skill. It plots competitors across two strategically meaningful axes, revealing how the market has clustered and where whitespace exists.

Choosing the right axes is the most important decision in building the matrix. Bad axes produce a map that looks like a feature checklist. Good axes reveal strategic positioning.

**Good axis examples:**
- Breadth of solution vs. depth of solution
- Enterprise focus vs. SMB focus
- Human-assisted vs. fully automated
- Workflow-specific vs. general purpose
- High-touch/services-heavy vs. self-serve/product-led
- Price point (low vs. high)
- Ease of use vs. configurability

**Bad axis examples:**
- Has Feature X vs. does not have Feature X (this is a feature comparison, not a positioning map)
- Founded before 2015 vs. after 2015 (not strategically meaningful)
- Big company vs. small company (too generic)

### Positioning Analysis

Beyond the matrix, positioning analysis examines how each competitor presents itself to the market:
- What customer segment do they claim to serve?
- What is their stated value proposition?
- What proof points and case studies do they lean on?
- What pricing model and tier structure do they use?
- What channels do they use to acquire customers?
- What are their reviewers saying on G2, Capterra, or Trustpilot?

Positioning analysis tells you where competitors are vulnerable and what messaging territory is unclaimed.

### Whitespace Identification

Whitespace is the combination of customer needs, market segments, or positioning dimensions that no competitor is serving well. Identifying whitespace is the strategic payoff of the entire exercise.

Whitespace appears in three forms:
1. **Segment whitespace:** A customer type no one is targeting well (e.g., mid-market companies in a market dominated by enterprise tools)
2. **Feature or capability whitespace:** A workflow or use case everyone ignores
3. **Positioning whitespace:** A value message no one is claiming (e.g., "easiest to implement" in a market of complex tools)

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Entering a new market and needing to understand the landscape before building
- Preparing a fundraising pitch and needing to show investors how you are differentiated
- Developing or refreshing your positioning and messaging
- Arming your sales team with competitive intelligence for deals
- Planning a product roadmap and needing to know what to build vs. avoid
- Evaluating whether a new feature is already commoditized by competitors
- Onboarding a new CMO, CPO, or sales leader who needs to get up to speed fast
- Responding to a board question about competitive threats

Do NOT use when:
- You need a feature-by-feature technical comparison for a specific deal (use `competitive-battlecard.md`)
- You need to size the total market (use `market-opportunity-analysis.md`)
- You need customer-level insight on why you win or lose (use `win-loss-analysis.md`)

## Dos

- **Do choose axes that reveal strategic positioning, not just features.** The matrix is only as useful as the dimensions you choose to map against.
- **Do include indirect and emerging competitors.** Limiting your map to direct competitors gives you a false sense of the competitive environment.
- **Do pull data from multiple sources.** Combine company websites, G2/Capterra reviews, LinkedIn, job postings, pricing pages, and press coverage.
- **Do read actual customer reviews of competitors.** G2 and Capterra reviews are a goldmine. They reveal real pain points that competitor positioning does not admit.
- **Do include your own product on the map.** Place yourself honestly. An overgenerous self-assessment destroys the credibility of the whole document.
- **Do note funding and growth signals.** A competitor with a recent $50M Series B is more threatening than one that raised $2M three years ago and has not grown.
- **Do date your research.** Competitive landscapes shift. Every data point should include when it was sourced.
- **Do look at job postings as a signal.** What a competitor is hiring for reveals their strategic priorities before any press release does.
- **Do segment competitors by tier.** Tier 1 (primary threats), Tier 2 (secondary players), Tier 3 (niche or declining) helps prioritize attention.
- **Do identify each competitor's apparent strategic wedge.** What is the first use case they win, then expand from?

---

## Donts

- **Dont copy competitor feature lists from their marketing pages.** Marketing pages show what companies want you to believe, not what customers actually experience. Cross-reference with reviews.
- **Dont build a matrix with more than 10 competitors.** A 20-row matrix is unreadable and strategically unfocused. Tier your competitors and focus your matrix on the top 6 to 8.
- **Dont use generic axes like "good vs. bad" or "cheap vs. expensive."** Axes must reflect real strategic trade-offs that customers and buyers care about.
- **Dont treat this as a one-time exercise.** The competitive landscape changes constantly. Build a habit of quarterly updates, especially tracking funding news and product launches.
- **Dont only track current-state competitors.** The company that kills you is usually one you did not take seriously early enough.
- **Dont let the sales team build this in isolation.** Sales sees who they lose to in deals. Product sees capability gaps. Marketing sees positioning. You need all three lenses.
- **Dont confuse competitor positioning with competitor reality.** What a company says about itself is not the same as what customers experience. Use both.
- **Dont skip the "so what."** Every competitive landscape map must end with strategic implications. What should you do differently because of what you found?
- **Dont omit pricing if it is publicly available.** Pricing structure reveals the customer segment a competitor is targeting more clearly than their website copy does.
- **Dont make it prettier than it is insightful.** A beautiful matrix with no strategic clarity is a waste of time. Insight comes first.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. YOUR PRODUCT DESCRIPTION
   What does your product do, who is it for, and what problem does it solve?

2. YOUR TARGET CUSTOMER (ICP)
   Industry, company size, geography, role of buyer/user.

3. KNOWN COMPETITORS
   List every competitor you are already aware of, even if partial.
   Include direct, indirect, and any emerging threats you suspect.

4. THE DIMENSIONS THAT MATTER TO YOUR BUYER
   What do your customers care most about when choosing a solution?
   (e.g., ease of implementation, price, integrations, support quality, specific features)

5. PURPOSE OF THE ANALYSIS
   Fundraising deck? Sales battlecards? Internal product strategy? GTM positioning?
   This affects depth, format, and framing.
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. RECENT WIN/LOSS DATA
   Who do you most commonly lose deals to, and why?
   Who do you most commonly displace, and why?

7. CUSTOMER VERBATIMS
   Quotes from customers or prospects about competitors they considered.

8. PRICING INTELLIGENCE
   Any pricing data you already have on competitors.

9. FUNDING AND GROWTH DATA
   Recent funding rounds, headcount signals, revenue estimates if available.

10. AXES PREFERENCE
    If you have a hypothesis about the two dimensions most useful to map against,
    include them. Otherwise ask Claude to recommend axes based on your market.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Product: B2B SaaS tool for automating compliance reporting for fintech startups
ICP: Head of Compliance or CFO at US-based fintech companies, 20 to 200 employees
Known competitors: Drata, Vanta, Tugboat Logic, Secureframe, manual Excel processes
What buyers care about: speed to first compliance report, SOC 2 vs multi-framework support,
price, integration with existing tools, audit support quality
Purpose: Series A fundraising deck and internal product strategy
Win/loss: We lose to Vanta on brand and integrations. We win against manual processes on speed.
I want axis recommendations plus a full competitive matrix and positioning analysis.
```

## Output Format

```
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
   3 to 5 sentences: who are the key players, how is the market clustered,
   and what is your key differentiation opportunity.

2. COMPETITOR UNIVERSE
   Full list of all competitors identified, segmented by:
   - Tier 1: Primary direct competitors (deals you win and lose against)
   - Tier 2: Secondary players (present in market, less frequent competition)
   - Tier 3: Indirect and emerging threats

3. COMPETITIVE MATRIX
   Two-axis map placing all Tier 1 and Tier 2 competitors.
   Axes chosen based on what your buyers care about most.
   Your product placed honestly on the map.
   Whitespace zones identified and labeled.

4. COMPETITOR PROFILES (one per Tier 1 competitor)
   For each:
   - Founded / funding / headcount estimate
   - Target customer claim
   - Core value proposition
   - Pricing model and price point
   - Key strengths (based on reviews and evidence)
   - Key weaknesses (based on reviews and evidence)
   - Strategic wedge (how they enter accounts)
   - Threat level to you: High / Medium / Low with rationale

5. POSITIONING ANALYSIS
   - What messaging territories are claimed by competitors
   - What messaging territory is unclaimed (whitespace)
   - How customers describe competitors in their own words (from reviews)
   - Where competitors are most vulnerable based on negative review themes

6. WHITESPACE ANALYSIS
   - Segment whitespace: customer types no one serves well
   - Capability whitespace: workflows or use cases underserved
   - Positioning whitespace: value messages no one is claiming

7. STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS
   - What this means for your product roadmap
   - What this means for your positioning and messaging
   - What this means for your GTM motion
   - Which competitors to prioritize tracking vs. deprioritize

8. SOURCES AND DATE STAMP
   All sources cited with access date.
   Note: to be refreshed quarterly.
```

## Starter Prompt

Copy and paste this into Claude, filling in the brackets:

```
You are a competitive intelligence analyst helping me build a comprehensive competitive landscape map.

My product: [describe what your product does and who it is for]
My ICP: [firmographics, geography, buyer role]
Known competitors: [list every competitor you know, including indirect ones]
What my buyers care about most: [the key decision criteria when choosing a solution]
Purpose: [fundraising / product strategy / sales enablement / GTM positioning]
Win/loss context: [who you win and lose against and why, if known]
Pricing intelligence: [any competitor pricing data you have]

Please produce:
1. A tiered competitor universe (Tier 1, 2, 3)
2. Axis recommendations for the competitive matrix, with rationale
3. A competitive matrix placing all Tier 1 and 2 players and my product
4. A one-page profile for each Tier 1 competitor
5. A positioning analysis identifying claimed and unclaimed messaging territory
6. A whitespace analysis with three types: segment, capability, and positioning
7. Strategic implications for product, positioning, and GTM

Be direct. If a competitor is significantly stronger than us in a dimension, say so clearly.
If our current positioning overlaps too heavily with a competitor, flag it.
Show sources and note the date for each data point.
```
