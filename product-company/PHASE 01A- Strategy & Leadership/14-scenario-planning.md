# scenario-planning.md

> **Skill:** Builds structured future scenarios to stress-test strategy, prepare for uncertainty, and develop contingency plans across multiple plausible futures for a business, market, or decision.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"What are the different ways the future could unfold, and are we prepared for each of them?"* It produces a set of plausible, distinct, and internally coherent future scenarios built around critical uncertainties, each with strategic implications and contingency responses. Used for long-range strategic planning, board risk discussions, investment thesis stress-testing, and leadership team preparedness exercises.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is Scenario Planning?

Scenario planning is a structured method for thinking about and preparing for an uncertain future. Rather than attempting to predict a single future outcome (which is rarely accurate), scenario planning constructs multiple plausible futures based on critical uncertainties and asks: "What would we do in each of these worlds?"

The method was developed formally at Royal Dutch Shell in the 1970s, where strategist Pierre Wack and his team used it to anticipate the 1973 oil crisis and position Shell to respond better than competitors who had not considered that possibility. Since then it has been adopted widely in corporate strategy, government planning, and investment management.

Scenario planning is particularly valuable when:
- The future is genuinely uncertain (multiple plausible outcomes, not just one)
- The decisions being made are long-horizon and hard to reverse
- The stakes are high enough to justify the investment in preparation
- The organization needs to build shared mental models of the future across leadership

### Scenarios vs. Forecasts

A forecast says: "We believe X will happen, with Y% probability."
A scenario says: "X could happen. Here is what that world looks like and what we would do."

Forecasts are useful when uncertainty is low and the range of outcomes is predictable. Scenarios are useful when uncertainty is high, when the future is discontinuous (not just a continuation of current trends), or when a single forecast would give false confidence.

Scenarios are not best-case/worst-case/base-case. That approach anchors on the present and extrapolates linearly. Genuine scenario planning builds qualitatively different futures based on the intersection of critical uncertainties.

### The Scenario Building Process

**Step 1: Define the focal question**
What is the decision or strategy that these scenarios are meant to inform? Scenarios without a focal question produce interesting fiction, not strategic insight.

**Step 2: Identify driving forces**
What external forces will shape the future of your market and business? Typically from the STEEP categories: social, technological, economic, environmental, political/regulatory.

**Step 3: Identify critical uncertainties**
Of all the driving forces identified, which are both highly uncertain (we genuinely do not know how they will resolve) AND highly impactful (the resolution will materially affect the focal question)?

**Step 4: Select two axes**
Choose the two most critical uncertainties to form the axes of a 2x2 scenario matrix. The axes must be genuinely independent (not correlated), and each must have at least two plausible extreme outcomes.

**Step 5: Build four scenarios**
Each quadrant of the 2x2 represents a distinct scenario. Give each scenario a name (not "optimistic/pessimistic" but evocative names that capture the world's character) and build out the narrative.

**Step 6: Test each scenario for internal coherence**
Each scenario must be internally consistent. Events and conditions within the scenario must logically coexist. Remove elements that contradict the scenario's core logic.

**Step 7: Derive strategic implications**
For each scenario, ask: What does this world mean for our strategy? What would we need to do differently? What early warning signals would tell us this scenario is materializing?

**Step 8: Identify robust strategies and hedges**
A robust strategy performs acceptably across all scenarios, not perfectly in one. Identify which strategic moves are robust vs. which are bets on a specific scenario.

### The 2x2 Scenario Matrix

The 2x2 matrix is the most common scenario planning structure. Two critical uncertainties form the axes, producing four distinct scenario worlds.

Example axes for a B2B SaaS company:
- Axis 1: AI capability pace (slow and incremental vs. rapid and transformative)
- Axis 2: Regulatory environment (permissive and growth-friendly vs. restrictive and compliance-heavy)

This produces four scenarios:
- Rapid AI + permissive regulation: land-grab, move fast, build AI-native product
- Rapid AI + restrictive regulation: AI capability constrained by compliance requirements
- Slow AI + permissive regulation: incremental improvement, execution efficiency wins
- Slow AI + restrictive regulation: stable market, compliance differentiators matter most

### Early Warning Signals

Each scenario should have 3 to 5 early warning signals: observable events or data points that would indicate the scenario is beginning to materialize. These signals are the bridge between scenario planning and real-time strategic monitoring.

Without early warning signals, scenarios remain intellectual exercises. With them, the leadership team can monitor the environment and adapt strategy as clarity about which scenario is emerging improves.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Running annual strategic planning in a highly uncertain market environment
- Evaluating a major long-horizon investment or strategic bet
- Stress-testing an investment thesis before committing capital
- Preparing a board or leadership team for a decision with significant uncertainty
- A major external force (regulatory change, technology shift, economic disruption)
  is creating genuine uncertainty about the future of your market
- Building a multi-year roadmap that needs to account for different possible futures
- Conducting an executive team alignment session on strategic risk

Do NOT use when:
- The future is relatively certain and a single forecast is sufficient
- The decision horizon is short enough that scenarios would not change the decision
- The team does not have the time or facilitation capacity to run the process properly
- The primary need is a competitive analysis of current-state players (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)

## Dos

- **Do start with a clear focal question.** Scenarios that are not anchored to a specific decision or strategy are interesting but not useful. Define what you are trying to decide before building the scenarios.
- **Do choose axes that are genuinely independent.** If the two axes are correlated (one almost always predicts the other), you will end up with only two real scenarios, not four. Independence is essential.
- **Do name scenarios evocatively, not evaluatively.** "The Acceleration" and "The Regulation Wave" are better names than "Good Scenario" and "Bad Scenario." Evaluative names create bias toward planning only for the preferred future.
- **Do make each scenario internally coherent.** Every element of a scenario must logically coexist with the others. If a scenario requires contradictory conditions to both be true, it is not coherent and will not be useful.
- **Do include early warning signals for each scenario.** Without observable signals, scenarios have no operational value. Each scenario needs 3 to 5 measurable or observable events that would indicate it is materializing.
- **Do identify robust strategies.** A strategy that only works in one scenario is a bet, not a plan. Identify the actions that are worth taking regardless of which scenario materializes.
- **Do differentiate between robust moves and scenario-specific hedges.** Robust: build excellent core product (good in all scenarios). Scenario-specific: invest heavily in AI-native architecture (only optimal in rapid AI scenarios).
- **Do revisit scenarios when early warning signals change.** Scenario planning is not a one-time exercise. Update scenarios when the environment shifts significantly.
- **Do use scenarios as a shared language, not just a planning tool.** When leadership agrees on scenario names and characteristics, discussions about strategic decisions become much faster: "That decision assumes Scenario B. Are we confident enough in B to bet on it?"

---

## Donts

- **Dont build scenarios as best-case/base-case/worst-case.** This is the most common scenario planning mistake. Best/base/worst are not independent scenarios. They are confidence intervals around a single forecast and produce linear extrapolation, not genuine alternatives.
- **Dont build scenarios to confirm the strategy you already want to execute.** Scenario planning that validates a predetermined conclusion is not useful. Build scenarios that challenge your assumptions and make you uncomfortable.
- **Dont choose axes that are highly correlated.** If one axis predicts the other (e.g., GDP growth and consumer spending), you do not have four independent scenarios.
- **Dont try to assign probabilities to scenarios.** The purpose of scenario planning is to prepare for multiple futures, not to predict the most likely one. Assigning probabilities recreates a forecast and defeats the purpose.
- **Dont build more than four to six scenarios.** Too many scenarios become unmanageable and dilute strategic clarity. Four is typically optimal. Six is the maximum for most planning purposes.
- **Dont skip the strategic implications step.** Scenario narratives without "and therefore our strategy should..." are interesting stories, not planning tools. Every scenario must end with specific strategic implications.
- **Dont include only external forces in your scenarios.** Some critical uncertainties are partly internal: Will we be able to hire the team we need? Will our key technology bet work? Include significant internal uncertainties where relevant.
- **Dont let the process become too academic.** Scenario planning can become an intellectual exercise that never connects to actual decisions. Keep the focal question visible throughout and constantly ask: "Does this change what we would do?"

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE FOCAL QUESTION
   What specific strategic decision, investment, or plan are these
   scenarios meant to inform? Be as specific as possible.

2. THE PLANNING HORIZON
   How far out are you planning? 2 years, 5 years, 10 years?
   This determines the scope and uncertainty level appropriate for scenarios.

3. YOUR CURRENT STRATEGY OR PLAN
   What are you currently planning to do?
   Scenarios should stress-test this, so knowing the current plan is essential.

4. THE KEY DRIVING FORCES
   What external forces do you believe will most shape your market
   and business over the planning horizon?
   (Technology, regulation, customer behavior, economic conditions, competition)

5. COMPANY AND MARKET CONTEXT
   Stage, revenue, market category, current competitive position.
   This grounds the scenarios in your specific reality.
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. KNOWN CRITICAL UNCERTAINTIES
   Any specific uncertainties you already believe are most important
   to the future of your business.

7. EXISTING TREND OR SIGNAL ANALYSIS
   Any trend or signal analysis that has already identified the key
   driving forces. (See trend-signal-analysis.md as an input skill.)

8. LEADERSHIP TEAM ASSUMPTIONS
   What does the leadership team currently assume about the future?
   Scenarios that challenge these assumptions are most valuable.

9. PRIOR SCENARIO WORK
   Has the organization done scenario planning before?
   What scenarios were developed and how have they aged?

10. STAKEHOLDER AUDIENCE
    Is this for an internal leadership team, a board, or investors?
    The level of detail and the format of presentation varies significantly.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Focal question: Should we invest $3M in building an AI-native version of our
compliance automation product over the next 18 months, or should we continue
improving the current rule-based product?
Planning horizon: 3 to 5 years
Current plan: Continue improving rule-based product, add AI features incrementally
Key driving forces we see: AI capability pace (GPT-5 and successors),
regulatory response to AI in compliance decisions, enterprise buyer adoption
of AI tools, competitive intensity from AI-native startups entering our space
Company context: $4.2M ARR, Series A, 32 employees, compliance automation SaaS for
US fintech startups
Known critical uncertainties: How fast will enterprise buyers accept AI-generated
compliance decisions? Will regulation require human review of AI compliance outputs?
Leadership assumptions: AI will be important but 2 to 3 years away from enterprise
acceptance. We have time to take an incremental approach.
Audience: CEO and board for a major capital allocation decision
```

## Output Format

```
1. FOCAL QUESTION AND PLANNING CONTEXT
   The specific decision or strategy being stress-tested.
   The planning horizon.
   The current plan that scenarios will challenge.

2. DRIVING FORCES ANALYSIS
   The key external forces identified across STEEP categories.
   Ranked by impact and uncertainty.

3. CRITICAL UNCERTAINTIES SELECTION
   The two critical uncertainties selected as scenario axes.
   Rationale for why these two were chosen over others.
   The two poles of each axis described clearly.

4. SCENARIO MATRIX
   The 2x2 matrix with scenario names in each quadrant.
   Visual or table representation of the four worlds.

5. SCENARIO NARRATIVES (one per scenario)
   For each of the four scenarios:

   SCENARIO NAME: [evocative, non-evaluative name]
   Core logic: What combination of conditions defines this world?
   World description: What does this world look like in [horizon] years?
   Impact on your market: How does this scenario change your market structure?
   Impact on your company: What does this mean specifically for your business,
   customers, competitive position, and revenue model?
   Strategic implication: What would you need to do differently in this world?
   Early warning signals: 3 to 5 observable events that indicate this scenario
   is materializing (with rough timing: 6 months, 12 months, 24 months)

6. CROSS-SCENARIO STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

   Robust moves: Actions that are worth taking in all four scenarios
   Scenario-specific bets: Moves that only make sense in certain scenarios
   High-regret actions: Moves that would be very costly if the wrong scenario materializes

7. IMPLICATIONS FOR THE FOCAL QUESTION
   Given the scenario analysis, what does this mean for the specific
   decision or strategy being evaluated?
   What is the recommended approach given the uncertainty across scenarios?

8. MONITORING AND TRIGGER PLAN
   Which early warning signals to monitor and how frequently.
   The trigger conditions that would cause a strategy pivot.
   Who owns monitoring each signal.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a strategic foresight analyst helping me build a rigorous
scenario planning framework to stress-test my strategy and prepare for uncertainty.

Focal question: [the specific decision or strategy these scenarios are meant to inform]
Planning horizon: [2 years / 5 years / 10 years]
Current plan: [what we are currently planning to do]
Key driving forces: [the external forces you believe will most shape your future]
Company and market context: [stage, revenue, market, competitive position]
Known critical uncertainties: [specific uncertainties you already believe are most important]
Leadership team assumptions: [what leadership currently believes about the future]
Audience: [internal leadership / board / investors]

Please produce:
1. A driving forces analysis ranked by impact and uncertainty
2. Selection of two critical uncertainties as scenario axes with rationale
3. A 2x2 scenario matrix with four evocative, non-evaluative scenario names
4. Full narrative for each of the four scenarios including: world description,
   market impact, company impact, strategic implication, and 3 to 5 early warning signals
5. A cross-scenario analysis identifying robust moves, scenario-specific bets,
   and high-regret actions
6. Direct implications for the focal question: given this uncertainty, what is recommended?
7. A monitoring and trigger plan for the early warning signals

Do not build best-case/base-case/worst-case scenarios. Build genuinely distinct
alternative worlds based on the intersection of the two critical uncertainties.
Name scenarios evocatively, not evaluatively.
Challenge our current leadership assumptions in at least one scenario.
Be direct if the current plan is fragile in more than two of the four scenarios.
```
