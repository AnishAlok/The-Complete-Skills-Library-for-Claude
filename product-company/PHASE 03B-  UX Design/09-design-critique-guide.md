# design-critique-guide.md

> **Skill:** Produces a design critique facilitation guide with session structure, feedback frameworks, and documentation format to make design reviews productive and psychologically safe.

---

## TLDR

Design critique is the structured practice of evaluating design work against its goals, the user's needs, and design principles to improve it. When run well, critique makes designs significantly better through diverse perspectives. When run poorly, it produces defensive designers, unfocused feedback, and directionless revisions. This guide produces the facilitation structure, feedback frameworks, and documentation format for productive critique sessions.

---

## Topic Explanation

### Critique vs. Feedback vs. Opinion

These three are frequently conflated in design reviews and the conflation damages session quality:

**Opinion:** "I don't like the blue." Personal preference. Not critique.
**Feedback:** "The blue doesn't work here." Evaluative but unexplained. Barely critique.
**Critique:** "The blue creates insufficient contrast for text at this size against this background, failing WCAG AA. Consider [alternative]." Grounded in a standard, specific, actionable. This is critique.

Good critique is always:
- Grounded in a standard, principle, or user need (not personal taste)
- Specific about what the issue is and where it appears
- Constructive (suggests a direction or alternative, not just the problem)

### The Facilitator's Role

The facilitator is not the smartest person in the room. The facilitator's job is to create conditions where the designer gets the most useful feedback possible:
- Protect against unfocused or opinion-based feedback
- Ensure all voices are heard, not just the loudest
- Keep the session focused on the stated design goals
- Prevent the designer from becoming defensive
- Capture feedback in a usable format

The designer and the facilitator can be the same person, but having a separate facilitator generally produces better sessions.

## Output Format

```
1. SESSION SETUP
   Who should attend (designer, PM, engineering, other designers, stakeholders).
   Optimal session length: 30 to 60 minutes per design area.
   Materials required: design access, context doc, critique framework.

2. DESIGNER CONTEXT BRIEF (presented at session start, 5 minutes)
   Template for the designer to fill out before the session:
   - Problem being solved
   - User type and goals
   - Design goals for this work
   - Specific questions for feedback (focus areas)
   - What is out of scope for this critique

3. OBSERVATION PHASE (silent, 3 to 5 minutes)
   Participants review the design silently.
   Record observations independently before discussion.
   Observation prompt: "What do you notice? What questions arise?"

4. CLARIFYING QUESTIONS PHASE (5 minutes)
   Questions to understand intent, not to critique.
   "Why did you..." and "What happens when..." are clarifying.
   Opinions are not allowed in this phase.

5. FEEDBACK PHASE FRAMEWORK
   The standard for valid critique: grounded in user need, design principle, or goal.
   Feedback format: "I notice [observation]. Because [reasoning], I wonder if [question or suggestion]."
   Facilitator role: redirect opinions to principles.
   Round-robin structure to ensure all voices heard.

6. PRIORITIZATION (5 minutes)
   Which feedback is most important to act on?
   Dot voting or group consensus on top 3 items.

7. SESSION DOCUMENTATION TEMPLATE
   Design reviewed, attendees, date.
   Top 3 prioritized feedback items.
   All feedback by category: user needs / design principles / goals / visual / other.
   Designer's intended next steps.
   Follow-up critique scheduled (if needed).
```
