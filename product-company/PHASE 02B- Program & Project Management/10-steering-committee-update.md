# steering-committee-update.md

> **Skill:** Produces an executive-level steering committee update that surfaces status, key decisions needed, escalated risks, and strategic implications in a format designed for senior stakeholder decision-making.

---

## TLDR

A steering committee update is the executive version of a project status report. It is exception-based (only surfaces what requires attention), decision-focused (explicitly requests the decisions executives need to make), and framed in business terms (outcomes and impacts, not task completion). Used monthly or at key project milestones to keep executive sponsors informed and engaged.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Executives Need from a Project Update

Executives reviewing multiple projects simultaneously need to quickly answer:
1. Is this project still a good investment?
2. Is it on track to deliver the expected value?
3. Is there anything that requires my decision or intervention?
4. What am I responsible for knowing and acting on?

A steering committee update answers all four questions efficiently. It does not provide operational detail. It provides the signal: continue / course-correct / escalate.

### The Exception Principle

Executives are overloaded with information. A steering committee update should operate on the exception principle: only surface what has changed, what is at risk, or what requires a decision. Items that are on track and require no action should be acknowledged briefly and moved past quickly.

"All three workstreams are on track. No changes to the approved plan. No decisions required this period" is a complete and valuable update for a project that is going well.

### Pre-Wiring Decisions

No executive should encounter a major decision for the first time at a steering committee meeting. Major decisions should be pre-wired: discussed informally with key stakeholders before the meeting so that the steering committee session is a confirmation, not a discovery.

The PM's job is to ensure that by the time a decision appears in the steering committee update, all required background information has been shared and informal alignment has been built.

## Output Format

```
1. UPDATE HEADER
   Project name, meeting date, presenter, attendees.
   Time allotted for this update.

2. SNAPSHOT (1 slide or section)
   Overall status: RAG.
   One sentence on each of: schedule / budget / scope / strategic value.
   Period covered.

3. SINCE LAST MEETING
   Key milestones achieved.
   Key decisions made.
   Significant changes.

4. DECISIONS REQUIRED (the most important section)
   For each decision needed from the steering committee:
   - Decision description: what is being asked
   - Background: why this decision is needed now
   - Options: the choices available
   - Recommendation: what the PM recommends and why
   - Consequence of delay: what happens if this is not decided today

5. ESCALATED RISKS AND ISSUES
   Only risks/issues that:
   - Require executive authority to resolve
   - Have budget or strategic implications beyond PM authority
   - Represent a material change to projected outcomes
   For each: description / impact / proposed resolution / ask

6. OUTLOOK
   Project on track for [planned completion date and delivery].
   Any change to expected business value.
   Next steering committee date and what will be brought.

7. APPENDIX (for those who want detail)
   Full schedule status.
   Full budget status.
   Full risk register summary.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a PM preparing a steering committee update.

Project: [name and strategic context]
Meeting date and attendees: [who is in the room]
Period covered: [since last steering committee]
Overall status: [RAG]
Since last meeting: [milestones and decisions]
Decisions needed: [what the committee must decide]
Escalated risks: [issues beyond PM authority]
Forecast: [projected completion and value delivery]

Produce a steering committee update optimized for executive consumption.
Apply the exception principle: only surface what requires attention or decision.
For every decision requested, include options, recommendation, and consequence of delay.
Keep operational detail in an appendix.
```
