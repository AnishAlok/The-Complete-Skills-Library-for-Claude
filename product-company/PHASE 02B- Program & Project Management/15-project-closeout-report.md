# project-closeout-report.md

> **Skill:** Produces a project closeout report that formally closes a project, documents outcomes against objectives, captures lessons learned, and transfers knowledge to ongoing operations.

---

## TLDR

A project closeout report formally ends a project by documenting what was delivered against what was planned, what was learned, and what must be transitioned to ongoing operations. It is the institutional memory document that future projects will reference. Used when a project reaches its planned end, when it is cancelled, or when it is handed off to an operational team.

---

## Topic Explanation

### Why Project Closeout Is Systematically Neglected

Project closeout is the phase most commonly abbreviated or skipped. By the time a project delivers, the team is already being pulled toward the next project. Stakeholders have mentally moved on. The PM is tired.

The organizational cost of skipping closeout:
- Lessons learned from expensive mistakes are not captured and are repeated
- Knowledge held by project team members leaves with them when they move on
- Systems and processes handed to operations are poorly documented
- Vendor contracts are not properly closed, creating ongoing financial exposure

A good closeout report is an investment in the organization's future project performance, not just a bureaucratic requirement.

### Lessons Learned vs. Post-Mortem

**Lessons learned:** A structured reflection on what went well, what could have gone better, and what should be done differently. Collaborative. Forward-looking. Focused on process improvement.

**Post-mortem:** Often specifically focused on what went wrong and why. More investigative. Sometimes triggered by a specific failure.

Both have value. The project closeout report should always include lessons learned. A post-mortem may be warranted if specific significant failures occurred.

### Knowledge Transfer Requirements

Any ongoing operations, support responsibilities, or maintained systems that the project created must be formally transferred to an operational owner. The closeout report documents what was transferred, to whom, and what documentation supports the transition.

## Output Format

```
1. CLOSEOUT REPORT HEADER
   Project name, closure date, PM, sponsor.
   Closure type: successful completion / cancelled / transferred.

2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
   What was delivered.
   Key outcomes vs. objectives.
   Overall assessment: successful / partially successful / unsuccessful.
   Key recommendation for future similar projects.

3. PROJECT OBJECTIVES vs. OUTCOMES
   For each charter objective:
   - Original objective
   - Achieved / partially achieved / not achieved
   - Evidence of outcome
   - Variance explanation if applicable

4. SCOPE PERFORMANCE
   Delivered scope vs. planned scope.
   Scope changes approved during the project.
   Items descoped and why.

5. SCHEDULE PERFORMANCE
   Planned completion date vs. actual.
   Milestones: planned vs. actual dates.
   Variance explanation.

6. BUDGET PERFORMANCE
   Approved budget vs. actual spend.
   Variance explanation.
   Outstanding financial commitments to close.

7. QUALITY PERFORMANCE
   Quality metrics achieved vs. targets.
   Known defects or issues carried forward.
   Post-launch issue summary.

8. LESSONS LEARNED
   What went well (3 to 5 items with recommendations to repeat)
   What could have gone better (3 to 5 items with recommendations to change)
   Process improvements recommended for future projects.

9. KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER
   Systems or processes handed to operations.
   Documentation produced and where it lives.
   Named operational owner for each transferred item.
   Any training delivered.

10. OPEN ITEMS
    Issues or risks that remain open after project closure.
    Owner and resolution timeline for each.

11. PROJECT ARCHIVE
    Where project documentation is stored.
    Key documents list with locations.

12. FORMAL CLOSURE SIGN-OFF
    Sponsor acknowledgment of project closure.
    PM final statement.
    Date.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a PM writing a project closeout report.

Project: [name and what it delivered]
Original objectives: [the charter objectives]
Outcomes achieved: [what was actually delivered]
Schedule: [planned vs. actual completion]
Budget: [planned vs. actual spend]
Scope changes: [changes approved during the project]
Key lessons learned: [what went well and what could have been better]
Knowledge transfer: [what was handed to operations and to whom]
Open items: [anything remaining after closure]

Produce a complete project closeout report including performance against all
baseline dimensions, lessons learned with specific recommendations, and
formal knowledge transfer documentation.
```
