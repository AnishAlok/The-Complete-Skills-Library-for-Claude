# brand-voice-guide.md

> **Skill:** Produces a brand voice and tone guide that defines how the company communicates: its personality, principles, tone variations by context, and practical writing examples.

---

## TLDR

A brand voice guide defines how the company sounds across all written communication: its personality attributes, its writing principles, how tone adjusts by context (a tweet vs. a legal disclaimer vs. a sales email), and concrete examples of on-brand vs. off-brand writing. It is the reference document that enables consistent voice across a distributed writing team without requiring every piece to go through a single editor.

---

## Topic Explanation

### Voice vs. Tone

Voice is the consistent personality of the brand. It does not change. A brand that is witty and direct is always witty and direct, whether writing a job posting or an error message.

Tone is how that voice is expressed in a specific context. A witty and direct brand is still witty and direct when writing a refund policy, but the tone will be more formal and precise than a product launch tweet. Voice is constant; tone adapts.

The most useful brand voice guides document both: the stable personality plus explicit guidance on how tone shifts across common contexts.

### The Practical Test for Voice Guidance

The test of a brand voice guide is whether it can answer "would we say it this way?" for a piece of copy written by someone who was not in the room when the brand was defined.

Too vague: "We are authentic and approachable." Every brand claims this. It gives writers no guidance.

Useful: "We use contractions. We write in second person. We never use passive voice when active voice is available. We do not use jargon unless we have defined it. We are confident without being arrogant: we state claims without hedging, but we do not mock alternatives."

The best voice guides include side-by-side examples: off-brand on the left, on-brand on the right.

## Output Format

```
BRAND VOICE GUIDE

Company: [name]
Version: [X.X] | Date: [date] | Owner: [marketing lead]
Applies to: [all external communications / specific channels]

---

1. BRAND PERSONALITY
   [3 to 5 adjective pairs that define the brand personality.
   Format: "We are [X] but not [Y]." This prevents misinterpretation.]

   We are [direct] but not [blunt].
   We are [confident] but not [arrogant].
   We are [knowledgeable] but not [academic].
   We are [human] but not [casual to the point of unprofessional].
   We are [clear] but not [oversimplified].

2. VOICE PRINCIPLES
   For each principle (4 to 6):
   Principle name: [short memorable label]
   What it means: [specific explanation]
   In practice: [1 to 2 concrete behaviors]
   Off-brand example: [what we do not say]
   On-brand example: [what we do say]

3. TONE BY CONTEXT
   For each common communication context:
   Context: [e.g., Product launch announcement]
   Tone: [e.g., Energetic, confident, forward-looking]
   Adjust: [What to dial up or dial down from the base voice]
   Example line: [a sentence in that tonal register]

   Standard contexts to cover:
   - Product announcements
   - Error messages and system copy
   - Customer support communications
   - Sales and prospecting copy
   - Social media (by platform)
   - Legal and compliance copy
   - Internal communications

4. GRAMMAR AND STYLE RULES
   Specific rules the company has chosen. Examples:
   - Oxford comma: [use / do not use]
   - Contractions: [always / in conversational contexts / never]
   - Sentence length: [target range]
   - Jargon policy: [define before use / avoid / acceptable in technical docs]
   - Numbers: [spell out / use numerals / when to use each]
   - Person: [first person plural (we) / second person (you) / mix]
   - Passive voice: [avoid / acceptable when actor is unknown]
   - Exclamation marks: [sparingly / never / freely]
   - Em dashes: [use freely / use sparingly / avoid]

5. WORDS WE USE AND AVOID
   We say: [preferred term] | Not: [avoided term] | Why: [reason]
   [e.g., "customers" not "users" because it signals a valued relationship]
   [e.g., "simple" sparingly because it can make customers feel condescended to]

6. WRITING EXAMPLES
   For each example (provide at least 5):
   Context: [what this piece of copy is for]
   Off-brand: [version that violates voice principles]
   On-brand: [version that embodies them]
   What changed: [explicit note on what made the on-brand version work]
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a brand strategist writing a brand voice guide.

Company: [name and what it does]
Company personality: [how the founding team or leadership describes the brand]
Target audience: [who the brand communicates with]
Competitors' voices: [how the main competitors sound, to differentiate from]
Brand positioning: [what the brand stands for]
Examples of writing the team considers on-brand: [specific pieces if available]
Examples of writing the team considers off-brand: [what to avoid]
Special requirements: [any specific style rules already established]

Produce a complete brand voice guide.
Every voice principle must include an off-brand and on-brand example.
The tone by context section must cover at least 5 different communication contexts.
Grammar and style rules must be specific enough to resolve a real writing decision.
```
