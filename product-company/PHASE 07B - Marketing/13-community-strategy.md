# community-strategy.md

> **Skill:** Produces a community building strategy document defining community purpose, platform, member journey, content programming, moderation, and success metrics.

---

## TLDR

A community strategy defines how a company will build and sustain a community of customers, prospects, or practitioners around a shared purpose. It covers why the community exists (for the members, not the brand), where it lives, how members progress from new to deeply engaged, what content and programming keeps it alive, how it is moderated, and how success is measured. Communities built around brand promotion fail; communities built around genuine member value can become a company's most durable competitive advantage.

---

## Topic Explanation

### The Community Purpose Problem

Most community strategies fail because the stated purpose is brand-serving, not member-serving. "A community where customers can engage with our brand" is not a purpose that attracts or retains members. People do not join communities to engage with brands; they join to connect with peers, learn from practitioners, and solve shared problems.

The community purpose must answer: what do members get from each other that they cannot get anywhere else? The company's role is facilitator, curator, and host, not broadcaster.

### Community Types

**Customer community:** existing customers supporting each other, sharing best practices, providing feedback. Primary value: reduces support burden, increases retention, surfaces product insights.

**Practitioner community:** people with a shared professional role or discipline (e.g., UX designers, RevOps leaders). Not necessarily product customers. Primary value: builds brand authority, creates a distribution channel, attracts talent.

**Product community:** developers or power users building with or on the product. Primary value: expands the product ecosystem, drives advocacy, surfaces integration needs.

### The Member Journey

A healthy community needs a structured member journey:

**Discovery:** how potential members find the community.
**Onboarding:** the first 7 days that determine whether a new member stays.
**First contribution:** the moment a member shifts from lurker to participant.
**Regular engagement:** the habits and programming that keep members returning.
**Leadership:** the members who become moderators, advocates, or formal community leaders.

Most community strategies address programming but neglect the onboarding and first-contribution stages, which is why communities have high join rates and low engagement rates.

## Output Format

```
COMMUNITY STRATEGY

Community name: [name]
Owner: [community manager] | Date: [date]

---

1. COMMUNITY PURPOSE AND POSITIONING
   The one-sentence reason this community exists (written for members, not the brand):
   "[Members] come here to [do/learn/connect] with other [member description]."
   What members get from this community that they cannot get anywhere else.
   What this community is not (explicit boundaries).

2. TARGET MEMBER
   Primary member profile: [role, industry, stage, what they care about]
   Secondary member profile: [if applicable]
   Why this member would join: [the specific value proposition]
   Where this member currently connects: [competing communities or platforms]

3. PLATFORM STRATEGY
   Platform choice: [Slack / Discord / Circle / LinkedIn Group / Discourse / other]
   Rationale for platform: [why this platform for this member type]
   Platform structure: [channels, spaces, or categories and their purpose]
   Integration with product (if applicable): [how community connects to the product]

4. MEMBER JOURNEY
   Discovery: [how potential members find the community]
   Onboarding: [first 7 days experience: welcome message, first actions, orientation]
   First contribution trigger: [the specific prompt or moment designed to move lurkers to contributors]
   Engagement loops: [the recurring patterns that bring members back]
   Leadership path: [how engaged members become moderators or community champions]

5. CONTENT AND PROGRAMMING
   Recurring content formats: [weekly threads, AMAs, office hours, spotlights]
   Event programming: [monthly or quarterly community events]
   Content calendar structure: [who produces what on what cadence]
   Member-generated content: [how to encourage and surface member contributions]
   Company content boundaries: [what company content is appropriate and what feels like advertising]

6. MODERATION AND COMMUNITY HEALTH
   Community guidelines: [the specific rules members agree to]
   Moderation process: [who moderates, how violations are handled, escalation path]
   Toxicity prevention: [how the culture is shaped before problems emerge]
   Member recognition: [how engaged members are recognized]

7. BUSINESS INTEGRATION
   How community connects to marketing: [pipeline, content, case studies]
   How community connects to product: [feedback loops, beta programs]
   How community connects to customer success: [support deflection, expansion signals]
   What the company gives vs. what it gets: [the value exchange must favor members]

8. SUCCESS METRICS
   Primary community health metric: [active members / posts per member / member NPS]
   Growth metrics: [new members per month, retention rate]
   Engagement metrics: [DAU/MAU, posts per member, response rate]
   Business impact metrics: [support ticket deflection, retention correlation, pipeline influenced]
   Reporting cadence and owner.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a community strategist writing a community building strategy.

Community purpose: [who it is for and why it exists, from the member's perspective]
Target member: [specific role, industry, what they care about]
Platform preference: [Slack / Discord / Circle / open to recommendation]
Company's business goals for the community: [retention / advocacy / pipeline / support deflection]
Current state: [does a community exist, or is this greenfield?]
Resources: [dedicated community manager, part-time, none yet]
Timeline: [launch target or growth milestones]

Produce a complete community strategy.
The purpose statement must be written for members, not the brand.
The member journey must address onboarding and first-contribution specifically.
Success metrics must include both community health and business impact indicators.
```
