# thought-leadership-article.md

> **Skill:** Produces an executive or expert thought leadership article suitable for LinkedIn, company blog, or external publication that advances a specific point of view.

---

## TLDR

A thought leadership article is a bylined piece arguing a specific, defensible point of view from an individual perspective: the executive's or expert's genuine take on a trend, problem, or industry question. Distinct from a content marketing blog post (which targets SEO keywords and serves informational intent) and from a whitepaper (which documents extensive research). A thought leadership article is personal, opinionated, and grounded in real experience.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Thought Leadership Actually Requires

"Thought leadership" is one of the most abused terms in marketing. Content labeled thought leadership is often:
- Generic observation of trends anyone could have written
- Thinly veiled product promotion framed as industry insight
- Polished but unchallenging content that risks nothing and says nothing new

Real thought leadership has three characteristics:

**A specific, arguable claim.** Not "AI is changing how we work" but "The companies betting on AI for efficiency will lose to the companies betting on AI for judgment." An arguable claim has a counterargument worth engaging.

**Experience-grounded credibility.** The author has been in rooms, seen data, or made decisions that give them genuine insight others do not have. The article draws on that.

**Intellectual courage.** The author is willing to challenge a common assumption, name a mistake the industry is making, or take a position that not everyone will agree with.

### Voice in Ghost-Written Thought Leadership

Most executive thought leadership articles are ghost-written by marketing. This is normal and ethical. What makes it work: the ghost-writer must capture the executive's genuine perspective, specific examples, and actual vocabulary, not impose a generic "thought leadership" voice that sounds like every other article.

The process: 30-minute interview with the executive to extract their real view, specific examples from their experience, and the specific claim they want to make. Then write from that material.

## Output Format

```
THOUGHT LEADERSHIP ARTICLE

Author: [name and title]
Publication target: [LinkedIn article / company blog / Forbes / HBR / industry publication]
Topic: [the specific question or debate this addresses]
The claim: [the specific, arguable position this article takes]
Target length: [800 / 1200 / 1500 words]

---

HEADLINE
[Should state or imply the claim. Specific, not generic.
Not: "How AI Is Changing Business"
Yes: "We Have Been Solving the Wrong Problem With AI"]

SUBHEAD (optional)
[Expands on the claim or frames the question the article answers]

OPENING (150 to 200 words)
[Open with a specific observation, a surprising data point, or a vivid scene from the
author's experience. Do not open with "In today's rapidly changing landscape..."]
[State the central claim clearly in the opening. Do not save it for the middle.]

SECTION 1: THE COMMON ASSUMPTION (200 to 300 words)
[State the conventional wisdom or dominant industry view that this article challenges.
Be fair to it: steelman the position before taking it on.]

SECTION 2: WHY THE ASSUMPTION IS WRONG (OR INCOMPLETE) (300 to 400 words)
[The author's argument. Specific. Grounded in real experience or data.
One example from the author's direct observation is worth more than three abstract claims.]

SECTION 3: THE BETTER FRAME OR APPROACH (300 to 400 words)
[What the reader should think or do differently.
Practical but not prescriptive. The author is proposing, not instructing.]

CLOSING (100 to 150 words)
[Synthesize the argument. The closing should feel like a conclusion, not a summary.
End with the implication: what does this mean for the reader going forward?
Do not close with "In conclusion..." - close with the idea itself.]

AUTHOR BIO
[2 to 3 sentences. Current role and relevant credibility. One humanizing detail.
Link to LinkedIn or company website.]
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a ghost-writer producing a thought leadership article for an executive.

Author: [name, title, company]
The central claim: [the specific, arguable position they want to take]
The conventional wisdom being challenged: [what most people believe that the author disagrees with]
Evidence or examples from the author's experience: [specific stories, data, or observations]
Audience: [who should read this and what publication it is for]
Tone: [how this executive communicates: direct / analytical / conversational / formal]
Target length: [800 / 1200 / 1500 words]

Write a complete thought leadership article.
The claim must be stated clearly in the opening paragraph.
Evidence must be specific and grounded in experience, not abstract.
Avoid all generic thought leadership opening lines.
```
