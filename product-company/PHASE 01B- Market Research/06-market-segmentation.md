# market-segmentation.md

> **Skill:** Builds a structured market segmentation model that divides a broad market into distinct, actionable customer groups with shared characteristics, enabling targeted product, marketing, and go-to-market strategies.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"Who exactly are the different types of customers in our market, how are they distinct from each other, and which segment should we focus on first?"* It produces a market segmentation model with defined segment criteria, profiles, sizing, attractiveness scores, and strategic recommendations on which segment to prioritize and why. Used for product positioning, go-to-market strategy, pricing model design, and ICP definition.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is Market Segmentation?

Market segmentation is the process of dividing a heterogeneous market into homogeneous subgroups whose members share characteristics that make them respond similarly to a product, message, or offer. The goal is to identify groups that are internally similar (within each segment, customers look alike) and externally different (between segments, customers differ meaningfully).

Effective segmentation is the foundation of focused strategy. A company that tries to serve all segments equally well typically serves none of them as well as a focused competitor. Segmentation is what makes focus possible.

### The Four Dimensions of Segmentation

**Firmographic / Demographic segmentation**
Divides the market by observable structural characteristics:
For B2B: company size (revenue, headcount), industry/vertical, geography, technology stack, business model, ownership structure
For B2C: age, gender, income, education, family situation, geography

Strengths: Easy to measure, data widely available, simple to operationalize in targeting
Weaknesses: Does not directly capture behavior or motivation. Two companies with identical firmographics may behave very differently.

**Behavioral segmentation**
Divides the market by how customers act: purchase frequency, product usage patterns, feature adoption, switching behavior, loyalty, decision-making process.

Strengths: More predictive of future behavior than demographics alone. Captures what customers actually do rather than who they structurally are.
Weaknesses: Requires behavioral data that may not be available for non-customers.

**Psychographic / Attitudinal segmentation**
Divides the market by values, beliefs, attitudes, motivations, and risk tolerance.

Strengths: Captures the "why" behind behavior. Explains why two identical companies make different purchase decisions.
Weaknesses: Harder to measure, harder to operationalize for targeting, requires qualitative research to develop.

**Needs-based / Jobs-to-be-Done segmentation**
Divides the market by the outcome customers are trying to achieve or the problem they are trying to solve, regardless of demographic or firmographic characteristics.

Strengths: Most strategically actionable. Two companies with different firmographics may have the same core need and respond to the same solution. Often surfaces non-obvious market opportunities.
Weaknesses: Requires primary research to develop. Less immediately operational for list-building.

### Segmentation Criteria: What Makes a Good Segment?

A useful market segment must meet five criteria:

**Measurable:** The segment can be described and sized using available data.

**Accessible:** The segment can be reached through available marketing and sales channels.

**Substantial:** The segment is large enough to be worth pursuing economically.

**Differentiable:** The segment responds differently to marketing, product, or pricing than other segments. If two "segments" would respond identically to the same offer, they are not truly separate segments.

**Actionable:** The company can develop distinct product, marketing, and sales strategies for the segment. If a segment cannot be served differently from others, it has no strategic value.

### Segmentation vs. ICP

Segmentation and Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) are related but distinct:

**Segmentation** identifies and describes all the meaningful groups in the total market, including segments the company does not currently target.

**ICP** defines the single segment (or small number of segments) the company has chosen to prioritize. The ICP is the output of a strategic choice. Segmentation is the map that makes that choice informed.

You build a segmentation model first. Then you use attractiveness scoring and fit analysis to identify which segment to designate as your ICP.

### The Segmentation Process

1. Define the full market scope (the universe being segmented)
2. Identify segmentation dimensions most relevant to this market
3. Generate candidate segments using the dimensions
4. Evaluate segments against the five criteria
5. Profile each segment with rich characteristics
6. Size each segment
7. Score each segment on attractiveness and fit
8. Recommend ICP and prioritization sequence

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Building a go-to-market strategy and needing to identify which customers to focus on
- Refreshing product positioning when the market has evolved or the company has changed
- Evaluating whether to expand into new customer segments
- Designing a pricing model and needing to understand who will pay what
- Aligning a sales and marketing team around a common definition of the target customer
- Onboarding a new GTM or product leader who needs to understand the customer landscape
- Responding to investor questions about market focus and ICP definition

Do NOT use when:
- You already have a well-defined, validated ICP and do not need to revisit it
- You need a single persona for a specific design or copy task (use `persona-doc.md`)
- You need customer journey maps for a known segment (use `user-journey-map.md`)
- You need win/loss analysis within a known segment (use `win-loss-analysis.md`)

## Dos

- **Do use multiple segmentation dimensions simultaneously.** Firmographics alone produce segments that are easy to find but not meaningfully different in behavior. Combining firmographics with behavioral or needs-based dimensions produces more actionable segments.
- **Do validate segment boundaries with data.** Hypothesized segments must be tested against real customer and market data. Customers who do not naturally cluster the way you hypothesized require you to revise the segmentation.
- **Do size each segment.** A segment that cannot be sized is not operationally useful. Even a rough estimate is better than no size estimate.
- **Do score segments on attractiveness, not just size.** The largest segment is not always the best target. Attractiveness scoring should include: segment size, growth rate, willingness to pay, competitive density, fit with your product, and cost to serve.
- **Do name segments descriptively, not generically.** "Enterprise segment" is not a useful name. "Risk-averse mid-market CFOs in regulated industries" is. Names that capture the defining characteristic make segments more memorable and useful in strategy discussions.
- **Do verify that segments are genuinely differentiable.** If your hypothesized Segment A and Segment B would respond to the same product, message, and pricing, they are not different segments.
- **Do include a "not now" segment analysis.** Identifying which segments exist but are not worth pursuing yet, and why, is as strategically important as identifying the priority segment.
- **Do connect segment analysis directly to ICP definition.** The segmentation model should produce a clear recommendation on which segment to designate as the ICP and in what sequence to pursue others.

---

## Donts

- **Dont create segments based on how your internal data is organized.** Data systems often group customers by account size or acquisition channel for operational reasons. These operational groupings are not the same as strategically meaningful segments.
- **Dont confuse segments with verticals.** Industry verticals (healthcare, finance, manufacturing) are one segmentation dimension, not a full segmentation. Two manufacturing companies can be in completely different segments if their needs, behaviors, and buying processes differ.
- **Dont create too many segments.** A segmentation model with 15 segments is not actionable. Three to five segments is the optimal range for most markets. More than seven makes strategic choice impossible.
- **Dont build segments you cannot reach.** A segment with no accessible acquisition channel has no commercial value. Accessibility is one of the five required segmentation criteria for a reason.
- **Dont segment on vanity criteria.** Segments defined by criteria that feel interesting but do not affect product fit, willingness to pay, or response to messaging are not useful.
- **Dont skip the attractiveness scoring.** Size alone is not enough to prioritize. A smaller, faster-growing segment with lower competitive density may be strategically superior to a large, crowded one.
- **Dont present segments without connecting them to strategic decisions.** Segmentation is a tool, not an end. Every segmentation model should conclude with a recommendation: which segment, in what order, and why.
- **Dont assume the segmentation is permanent.** Markets evolve, customer needs shift, and your product changes. Segmentation models should be revisited annually or when a significant market shift occurs.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE MARKET BEING SEGMENTED
   What is the total market or universe you are dividing?
   Define the category and geographic scope.

2. YOUR CURRENT CUSTOMER BASE
   Who are your existing customers?
   What patterns do you see in the customers who succeed vs. struggle with your product?
   What customers churn and why?

3. THE SEGMENTATION PURPOSE
   What decision is this segmentation model meant to inform?
   ICP definition? Pricing? GTM strategy? Product roadmap?

4. KNOWN DIFFERENCES BETWEEN CUSTOMER TYPES
   What differences in behavior, needs, or outcomes do you already observe
   across different types of customers?
   These are the raw material for segment boundaries.

5. DATA AVAILABLE
   What data sources do you have access to?
   CRM data, product analytics, customer interviews, market data?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. CURRENT ICP DEFINITION (IF EXISTS)
   How is the ICP currently defined?
   Where is that definition working well and where is it breaking down?

7. SEGMENT SIZE DATA
   Any market sizing data that could help estimate segment sizes.

8. WIN/LOSS DATA
   Which types of customers do you win most reliably?
   Which types are you losing to competitors?

9. CUSTOMER INTERVIEW THEMES
   What patterns from customer interviews suggest different needs or behaviors
   across customer types?

10. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE BY SEGMENT
    Are different competitors strong in different segments?
    This is a signal that genuine segment differences exist.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Market: US businesses that need payroll processing and compliance management
Current customer base: 85 customers, majority independent restaurants with 1 to 3 locations,
some food trucks, a few small retail businesses (5 customers) who found us organically
Segmentation purpose: Redefine ICP for Series A investor narrative and sales focus
Known customer differences: Restaurant customers with compliance complexity (tip credit,
spread of hours) have 97% retention. Retail customers with simpler payroll have 74% retention.
Food trucks have high churn due to seasonality. Some restaurant customers run 1 location,
others run 3 to 5 and want multi-location management.
Data available: CRM with customer records, product analytics on feature usage,
12 customer interviews completed, Crunchbase data for market sizing
Win/loss: We win against manual/spreadsheet, lose to Gusto on brand in non-restaurant segments
Competitive landscape: Gusto dominates general SMB, Square Payroll strong in restaurant
but weak on compliance depth, no strong player for multi-location restaurant compliance
```

## Output Format

```
1. SEGMENTATION FRAMEWORK
   The segmentation dimensions selected and rationale for choosing them.
   How dimensions are combined to create segment boundaries.

2. SEGMENT MAP
   Visual or table representation of all segments identified.
   Showing how segments differ on the key dimensions.

3. SEGMENT PROFILES (one per segment)
   For each segment:
   - Segment name (descriptive, not generic)
   - Defining characteristics: firmographic, behavioral, attitudinal, needs-based
   - Who they are: representative description in plain language
   - How they buy: decision process, stakeholders, timeline, triggers
   - What they value: top 3 to 5 selection criteria
   - What they struggle with: key pain points
   - Current solutions they use
   - Estimated size: number of potential customers and total market value
   - Growth rate if estimable

4. SEGMENT ATTRACTIVENESS SCORING
   Scored matrix across all segments on:
   - Segment size
   - Growth rate
   - Willingness to pay
   - Competitive density (lower = more attractive)
   - Product fit with your current capabilities
   - Cost to acquire and serve
   - Strategic fit with your company's strengths
   Total score and ranking.

5. ICP RECOMMENDATION
   The recommended primary ICP segment with full rationale.
   Why this segment over the alternatives.

6. SEGMENT PRIORITIZATION SEQUENCE
   The recommended order for pursuing segments over time.
   The trigger conditions for expanding to Segment 2 and Segment 3.

7. "NOT NOW" SEGMENTS
   Segments identified but deliberately deprioritized.
   Rationale for deferral and conditions under which they should be reconsidered.

8. IMPLICATIONS FOR PRODUCT, GTM, AND PRICING
   What this segmentation means for:
   - Product: which features matter most for the ICP
   - Messaging and positioning: how to speak to the ICP
   - Sales motion: how the ICP buys and what that requires
   - Pricing: willingness to pay differences across segments
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a market strategy analyst helping me build a structured market
segmentation model and ICP recommendation.

Market being segmented: [the total market universe and geographic scope]
Current customer base: [who you already serve and patterns you observe]
Segmentation purpose: [what decision this will inform]
Known differences between customer types: [behavioral or needs differences you observe]
Data available: [CRM, analytics, interview data, market data]
Current ICP definition: [how ICP is currently defined, if at all]
Win/loss patterns: [who you win and lose against and which customer types]
Competitive landscape by segment: [are competitors strong in different segments?]

Please produce:
1. A segmentation framework selecting the most strategic dimensions for this market
2. A segment map showing how segments differ on key dimensions
3. A full profile for each segment including characteristics, buying process,
   values, pain points, sizing, and growth rate
4. An attractiveness scoring matrix ranking all segments on six to seven criteria
5. An ICP recommendation with rationale for why this segment over alternatives
6. A segment prioritization sequence with trigger conditions for expansion
7. A "not now" analysis for deprioritized segments
8. Implications for product, messaging, sales motion, and pricing

Flag if two of my hypothesized segments are not actually differentiable.
Recommend whether needs-based or firmographic dimensions are more strategic
for this specific market.
If the data I have provided is insufficient to size a segment, say so and
recommend how to get that data.
```
