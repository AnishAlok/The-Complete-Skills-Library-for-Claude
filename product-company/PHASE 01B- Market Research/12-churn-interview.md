# churn-interview.md

> **Skill:** Produces a complete exit interview script, facilitation guide, and synthesis framework for understanding why customers cancel, designed to surface the real reasons behind churn and generate actionable product, onboarding, and retention insights.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"Why did this customer leave, what could we have done differently, and what pattern is this part of?"* It produces a structured exit interview guide, a synthesis template per interview, and a cross-interview analysis framework for identifying systematic churn drivers. Used by customer success, product, and leadership teams to reduce preventable churn, prioritize product fixes, and understand where the product is failing to deliver on its promise.

---

## Topic Explanation

### Why Churn Interviews Are Uniquely Difficult

Churn interviews are among the most valuable research a company can conduct and among the most consistently avoided. Several factors make them hard:

**Emotional weight:** Cancellations feel like rejection. CS and sales teams often do not want to revisit the loss, and leadership sometimes avoids the data because it is uncomfortable.

**Customer reluctance:** Churned customers often do not want to spend 30 minutes explaining why they left. They have already moved on. Response rates for churn interviews are typically 10 to 25%, compared to 30 to 50% for win interviews.

**Rationalization:** Even when customers agree to talk, they often give the polite reason for leaving ("budget cuts," "too expensive") rather than the real one ("we never figured out how to use it," "your support was terrible").

**Timing:** By the time a cancellation is processed, the customer made their mental decision weeks earlier. Interviewing too late misses the critical inflection point.

These challenges are real but solvable with the right design, timing, and interview technique.

### The Three Categories of Churn

Not all churn has the same cause or the same fix. A churn analysis program must distinguish:

**Voluntary, product-related churn**
The customer actively decided to leave because the product was not solving their problem, had gaps they needed, or was too difficult to use. This is the most actionable category for product and CS teams.

**Voluntary, circumstantial churn**
The customer is leaving due to external factors: budget cuts, company shutdown, acquisition, role change, or pivot away from the use case. The product may have been fine. These churns require less product response but may reveal ICP fit issues.

**Involuntary churn**
Payment failure, expired cards, billing issues. These are operational fixes, not product fixes. Often conflated with voluntary churn in CRM data, inflating the apparent product churn rate.

A rigorous churn interview program tags each churn to one of these categories before analysis. Mixing involuntary and voluntary churn in win/loss calculations produces misleading metrics.

### The Real Reasons Behind the Stated Reasons

Churned customers frequently state one reason and mean another. The most common stated-vs-real gaps:

| Stated reason | Real reason often beneath it |
|---|---|
| "Too expensive" | Did not experience enough value to justify the cost |
| "We went in a different direction" | Did not understand how to use the product for their actual need |
| "Budget cuts" | Was the first thing cut because it was not deeply embedded in workflow |
| "Missing features" | Discovered the core use case did not match their actual job to be done |
| "Switching to a competitor" | A competitor solved a specific gap that was blocking real usage |

The job of the churn interview is to get past the stated reason to the real one. This requires neutral framing, skilled probing, and patience.

### Timing and Who Should Conduct the Interview

**Timing:** Conduct churn interviews within 5 to 14 days of cancellation. Earlier is better. After 3 weeks, memory and motivation both deteriorate. For high-value churned accounts, a same-day or next-day outreach is worth attempting.

**Who conducts it:** This is the most commonly debated question in churn interview design. Options:

The CSM who managed the account: Most context, lowest response rate, lowest candor.
A neutral CS team member not involved in the account: Good balance of context and neutrality.
A researcher or product team member: Highest candor, lowest context.
Third-party research firm: Highest candor and independence, highest cost.

For accounts over a threshold ACV, a neutral third party or researcher is worth the investment. For standard churn interviews, a neutral CS team member is usually the right balance.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- You need to understand the root causes driving churn in your product
- Churn rate is above benchmark and the team lacks a clear explanation
- A specific cohort or segment is churning at a higher rate than others
- A product change or pricing change was followed by elevated churn
- You are preparing to build a churn reduction program and need baseline insight
- Customer health scores are not predicting churn accurately and you need to understand why
- A high-value customer has just churned and you want a full debrief

Do NOT use when:
- You need to understand competitive deal losses before a purchase (use `win-loss-analysis.md`)
- You need to understand why customers are not adopting features (use `usability-test-script.md` or a discovery interview)
- Churn volume is too low for meaningful pattern analysis (fewer than 10 churns per month makes systematic analysis difficult)

## Dos

- **Do reach out within 5 to 14 days of cancellation.** This is the most important timing factor. Speed matters more than almost any other design choice.
- **Do acknowledge the departure without defensiveness.** The opening framing is critical. "We would love a chance to change your mind" signals desperation and reduces candor. "We are trying to learn and improve" signals genuine curiosity.
- **Do ask about their experience throughout the lifecycle, not just the end.** Churn decisions are made over time. The final trigger is usually just the last in a series of disappointments. Understanding the full arc is more valuable than understanding only the end.
- **Do probe beneath the first answer.** "Too expensive" is not an answer. It is a starting point. "Help me understand what you mean by that" and "What would you have needed to see to feel it was worth the cost?" get to the real story.
- **Do ask about the moment things started going wrong.** There is usually a specific moment when a customer's perception shifted. Finding that moment is often the most strategically valuable part of the interview.
- **Do ask what they are doing now to solve the problem.** What are they doing instead? Who did they switch to? What was the deciding factor? This is competitive intelligence as much as it is churn intelligence.
- **Do separate emotional acknowledgment from data collection.** Some churned customers are frustrated and want to vent. Let them. Then redirect to specific questions once the emotional content has been expressed.
- **Do tag every interview to a churn category and primary cause.** Qualitative interviews are only analytically useful when they are systematically tagged for cross-interview pattern analysis.

---

## Donts

- **Dont make the churn interview a win-back attempt.** If the goal of the interview is to get the customer back, it is not an interview. It is a sales call. These are incompatible goals. Decide upfront which one you are doing.
- **Dont be defensive.** Any defensiveness, justification, or correction in response to the customer's criticism will shut down honest feedback immediately.
- **Dont accept the first answer.** Churned customers often give the polite or simplified answer first. Budget, direction change, and "we found a better fit" are almost never the complete story. Probe every stated reason.
- **Dont send the outreach from a generic company email.** Personal outreach from a named person gets dramatically higher response rates than a form or automated email.
- **Dont combine churn interview outreach with marketing or re-engagement emails.** If the customer suspects the interview is a cover for a sales attempt, they will not participate or will not be candid.
- **Dont wait for the customer to cancel before starting the program.** At-risk customer interviews (conducted before cancellation, when health signals decline) are more valuable and easier to conduct. A churn interview program should include both exit interviews and at-risk interviews.
- **Dont skip the positive portion of the interview.** Asking what did work and what they valued is as important as asking what did not. It reveals what is worth protecting and amplifying.
- **Dont treat all churns as equally important for interview investment.** Prioritize higher-ACV churns, churns from your ICP segment, and churns that appear to be part of a pattern. Not every churned $49/month account warrants a 30-minute interview.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE CHURNED CUSTOMER CONTEXT
   Who churned: company name, segment, ACV, tenure, product plan.
   When they cancelled.
   What the CS team knows about why they left (even if incomplete or suspected).

2. THE CUSTOMER'S USAGE HISTORY
   How actively did they use the product?
   Which features did they use and which did they not?
   Was their usage trending up or down in the months before churn?
   Did they ever engage with customer success?

3. YOUR HYPOTHESES
   What does the team suspect is the real reason?
   Was there a specific incident, failed support interaction, or missed onboarding moment?

4. THE PRIMARY QUESTION TO ANSWER
   Is this a one-off deep dive on a specific high-value account?
   Or is this a template for a systematic churn interview program?

5. WHO WILL CONDUCT THE INTERVIEW
   CSM who managed the account, neutral researcher, third party?
   This affects the interview framing and likely candor level.
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. SUPPORT TICKET HISTORY
   Any support tickets, feature requests, or complaints the customer filed.
   These are often early churn signals that can be referenced in the interview.

7. NPS OR CSAT SCORES
   If the customer completed any satisfaction surveys, what did they say?

8. CHURN COHORT CONTEXT
   Is this customer part of a broader churn pattern?
   Are other customers from the same segment or cohort also churning?

9. COMPETITIVE CONTEXT
   Did the customer mention a competitor during the sales cycle or in any communications?
   Any intelligence on who they switched to?

10. PRIOR CHURN INTERVIEW FINDINGS
    If you have run churn interviews before, what patterns emerged?
    This interview should build on prior findings.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Churned customer: Orchard Restaurant Group, 4 locations, $14,400 ACV,
12 months of tenure, cancelled 8 days ago.
CS team intelligence: Limited engagement with the product after month 3.
Support team helped with initial setup but customer stopped responding
to quarterly check-in emails. No support tickets in last 6 months.
Usage history: Used payroll processing feature every week, never used
the compliance reporting feature (our key differentiator).
Hypotheses: We think they may have switched to a cheaper general payroll tool
and did not see the value in the compliance features.
Who conducts: Neutral researcher from the product team, not the CSM.
Churn context: This is the 4th restaurant customer in this size range to
churn in the past 2 months, all with similar low compliance feature usage.
Competitive context: Customer mentioned Gusto during sales cycle.
Prior churn interviews: 2 previous interviews both cited "we did not fully
use all the features" as the stated reason.
```

## Output Format

```
1. OUTREACH SCRIPT
   Email or phone outreach template for requesting the interview.
   Honest framing: learning not selling.
   Multiple outreach attempts with timing (day 3, day 7, day 12).

2. INTERVIEW PREPARATION CHECKLIST
   What to review before the session:
   account history, usage data, support tickets, CS notes.
   What to have ready: timeline of their lifecycle, key usage milestones.

3. INTERVIEW GUIDE

   OPENING AND FRAMING (2 to 3 minutes)
   Welcome script that establishes neutral, learning-focused tone.
   Explicit statement: this is not a win-back attempt.
   Recording consent.

   POSITIVE FOUNDATION (5 minutes)
   What did work? What value did they get?
   Purpose: establishes credibility, surfaces what to protect,
   and warms up candor before harder questions.

   LIFECYCLE TIMELINE (5 to 10 minutes)
   Walk through their journey from decision to sign to cancel.
   When did expectations vs. reality begin to diverge?
   Was there a specific moment when their perception shifted?

   CORE CHURN EXPLORATION (10 to 15 minutes)
   What was the primary factor in the decision to cancel?
   Probe beneath stated reason.
   What would have needed to be different for them to stay?
   What did they try before deciding to leave?

   COMPETITIVE CONTEXT (5 minutes)
   What are they doing now to solve the problem?
   If they switched to another vendor, what made that option more compelling?
   What does that vendor do that we did not?

   PRODUCT AND EXPERIENCE FEEDBACK (5 minutes)
   Specific product gaps or quality issues.
   Onboarding and support experience.
   What the first 90 days felt like.

   CLOSE (2 to 3 minutes)
   Anything important not covered?
   Permission to follow up if needed.
   Thank you.

4. POST-INTERVIEW SYNTHESIS TEMPLATE
   Churn category: voluntary-product / voluntary-circumstantial / involuntary.
   Primary driver (tagged from taxonomy).
   The real reason beneath the stated reason.
   The moment the decision shifted.
   What they are doing instead.
   Product gaps mentioned.
   Onboarding or CS failure points.
   Competitive intelligence surfaced.
   What would have retained them.
   Verbatim quotes to preserve.

5. CHURN DRIVER TAXONOMY
   Standard tags for: primary reason, product failure type, lifecycle stage
   when the failure occurred, competitive switch (yes/no/which competitor),
   ICP fit assessment (right fit / wrong fit / unclear).

6. CROSS-INTERVIEW PATTERN ANALYSIS TEMPLATE
   Churn reason frequency table.
   Patterns by segment, cohort, ACV, tenure.
   Most common lifecycle failure point.
   Product gaps with frequency count.
   Onboarding failure patterns.
   CS engagement correlation with churn.
   Competitive loss patterns.
   Hypothesis scorecard.
   Top 5 recommended actions with estimated retention impact.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a customer success researcher helping me design a churn exit interview
script and analysis program.

Churned customer context: [company, segment, ACV, tenure, cancellation date]
CS team intelligence: [what your team suspects and what they observed]
Usage history: [features used, engagement level, usage trend before cancellation]
Your hypotheses: [what you think the real reason is]
Who will conduct the interview: [CSM / neutral researcher / third party]
Churn cohort context: [is this part of a broader pattern?]
Competitive context: [any known competitor involvement]
Prior churn interview findings: [what previous interviews have revealed]
Primary question to answer: [what you most need to understand from this churn]

Please produce:
1. A multi-touch outreach sequence for requesting the interview
2. An interview preparation checklist using account history and usage data
3. A complete interview guide: opening, positive foundation, lifecycle timeline,
   core churn exploration, competitive context, product feedback, and close
4. Probe questions for getting beneath the stated reason to the real one
5. A post-interview synthesis template with churn category tagging
6. A churn driver taxonomy for consistent cross-interview tagging
7. A cross-interview pattern analysis template

Write the opening script with genuinely neutral, learning-focused language.
Include a note on how to handle customers who are angry or emotional.
Flag the questions most likely to uncover the real reason rather than the stated reason.
```

## Churn Category Reference

| Category | Definition | Primary Responder | Fix Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| Voluntary - product gap | Left because product did not solve the job | Product team | Roadmap investment |
| Voluntary - value not realized | Used product but did not experience ROI | CS and onboarding | Activation and CS motion |
| Voluntary - wrong fit | Customer should not have been sold to begin with | Sales and marketing | ICP tightening |
| Voluntary - circumstantial | External factors (budget cut, acquisition, pivot) | None directly | ICP fit validation |
| Voluntary - competitive | Specific competitor feature or pricing drove switch | Product and sales | Competitive response |
| Involuntary - payment failure | Card expired or payment failed | Billing and CS | Dunning flow fix |
