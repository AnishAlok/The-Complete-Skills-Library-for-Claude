# desk-research-synthesis.md

> **Skill:** Structures the collection, evaluation, and synthesis of secondary research sources into a coherent, insight-rich document that can inform strategy, validate assumptions, and stand as a credible reference for decision-making.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"What does the existing body of knowledge tell us about this market, customer, or problem, and what gaps remain that require primary research?"* It produces a structured secondary research synthesis that evaluates source quality, extracts key findings, identifies convergent themes, surfaces contradictions, and explicitly maps remaining knowledge gaps. Used before primary research, as a standalone market intelligence document, or as the evidence base for strategic recommendations.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is Desk Research (Secondary Research)?

Desk research, also called secondary research, is the practice of gathering and synthesizing information that already exists: published reports, academic papers, industry data, news coverage, public company filings, competitor websites, analyst reports, government data, and expert commentary. It is called "desk research" because it can be conducted from a desk without going into the field.

Secondary research is almost always the right starting point before primary research because:
- It is fast and relatively inexpensive
- It prevents you from researching what is already well-documented
- It surfaces hypotheses to test in primary research
- It identifies where genuine knowledge gaps exist that only primary research can fill

The discipline of secondary research is not just finding information. It is critically evaluating sources, triangulating across multiple sources, identifying where sources agree and disagree, and synthesizing findings into an integrated picture.

### Source Quality Evaluation

Not all secondary sources are equal. A rigorous synthesis evaluates each source on four dimensions:

**Credibility:** Who produced this? Is the source authoritative in this domain? A Gartner report is more credible for enterprise IT market sizing than a blog post. A peer-reviewed journal article is more credible for behavioral research than a consultant's whitepaper.

**Methodology transparency:** For quantitative data, how was it collected? What was the sample size? What were the survey methods? Data from a 50-person opt-in survey is much weaker than data from a census-representative 2,000-person study.

**Recency:** When was this published? In fast-moving markets, data more than 2 to 3 years old may be substantially wrong. In stable markets, older research may still be valid.

**Independence:** Who funded or commissioned this research? Vendor-commissioned research is systematically biased toward findings that favor the vendor's product or market position. Note sponsorship when it exists.

### Source Types and Their Strengths

**Analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, Frost and Sullivan, etc.)**
Strength: Market sizing, technology adoption, enterprise buyer behavior, vendor landscape
Weakness: Expensive, often paywalled, methodology not always transparent, can lag the market

**Academic and peer-reviewed research**
Strength: Rigorous methodology, peer-validated, unbiased
Weakness: Often theoretical, may not be directly applicable, lags industry by 2 to 4 years

**Government and regulatory data**
Strength: Large samples, authoritative for demographic and economic data
Weakness: Slow to update, may not cover the specific market segment you need

**Public company filings (10-K, annual reports, investor presentations)**
Strength: Authoritative on company-specific data, strategy, and market sizing language
Weakness: Companies present their markets optimistically

**Trade publications and industry press**
Strength: Current, specific to the industry, often surfaces practitioner insights
Weakness: Varies widely in quality and rigor, often advertiser-influenced

**Competitor websites, pricing pages, and content**
Strength: Current, direct evidence of positioning, messaging, and product strategy
Weakness: Represents intended positioning, not customer reality

**Review platforms (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)**
Strength: Actual customer voices, highlights real pain points and strengths
Weakness: Self-selected reviewers, often skewed toward strong opinions

**Expert interviews and conference presentations**
Strength: Current practitioner insight, surfaces unpublished knowledge
Weakness: Individual perspective, may not be representative

### Synthesis vs. Summary

A summary reports what individual sources say. A synthesis integrates findings across sources to produce insight that no single source contains.

**Summary:** "Gartner reports the market is $4.2B. IDC reports it is $5.1B. Forrester reports $3.8B."

**Synthesis:** "Three independent analyst sources estimate the market between $3.8B and $5.1B, a range that reflects different category definitions. The convergence point across all three is the enterprise segment, where all sources agree on 12 to 15% CAGR. The variance is in SMB inclusion criteria."

Synthesis is the harder skill and the more valuable output. It requires holding multiple sources simultaneously and identifying what they collectively imply.

### Knowledge Gap Mapping

The output of a desk research synthesis should explicitly map what is now known (covered by existing sources) and what remains unknown (genuine gaps requiring primary research). This knowledge gap map is often as valuable as the findings themselves because it directly informs the design of primary research.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Beginning a market research initiative and needing to establish the knowledge baseline
- Evaluating a new market opportunity and needing to understand what data already exists
- Building an investment thesis and needing a credible evidence base
- Writing a strategic planning document and needing the market context section
- Designing primary research and needing to scope what questions still need answering
- Briefing a leadership team on a topic where published knowledge is abundant
- Responding to a board or investor question with a well-evidenced answer

Do NOT use when:
- The question cannot be answered by existing sources and requires primary research
- You need to validate assumptions about your specific customers (use primary research)
- The market is so new that meaningful secondary research does not yet exist
- You need current behavioral data for your specific user base (use analytics)

## Dos

- **Do evaluate source quality before including any source.** Not all sources are worth including. Apply the credibility, methodology, recency, and independence criteria to each source before using it.
- **Do triangulate: use at least 3 independent sources for any significant claim.** A single source for an important finding is fragile. Three independent sources pointing to the same conclusion are much more credible.
- **Do separate direct quotes from paraphrased findings.** When you use the precise language of a source, mark it clearly. When you paraphrase, ensure the meaning is not distorted.
- **Do date every piece of data.** Research from 2020 may be misleading in a 2025 analysis. Every statistic and finding should carry its source date.
- **Do explicitly note where sources disagree.** Contradictions between credible sources are important data. Do not resolve them by picking the one you prefer. Acknowledge the contradiction and explain what it likely means.
- **Do map knowledge gaps explicitly.** The synthesis should conclude with a clear list of questions that existing sources cannot answer. This is the input for primary research design.
- **Do organize findings by theme, not by source.** A synthesis organized by "what Gartner says, what Forrester says, what McKinsey says" is a bibliography. A synthesis organized by "market size findings, buyer behavior findings, competitive dynamics findings" is useful.
- **Do note source limitations alongside findings.** Every finding is only as strong as its methodology. Including limitations builds credibility and prevents overconfidence.

---

## Donts

- **Dont use secondary research to validate a predetermined conclusion.** Selectively using sources that support your hypothesis while ignoring contradictory ones is confirmation bias, not research.
- **Dont cite secondary sources without checking the original.** Much secondary research misquotes or decontextualizes original sources. Always try to access the primary source.
- **Dont treat analyst estimates as facts.** Market sizing from analyst firms carries significant uncertainty. Treat ranges as estimates with stated assumptions, not as authoritative facts.
- **Dont include sources without evaluating their quality.** A page of search results is not a bibliography. Every source included should have been evaluated for credibility and methodology.
- **Dont produce a summary when you need a synthesis.** Walking through sources one by one is not synthesis. The value is in the integrated view across sources.
- **Dont ignore the recency problem.** Secondary research ages quickly in technology markets. Explicitly flag any source older than 18 to 24 months and note whether it is still likely to be valid.
- **Dont skip the knowledge gap section.** A synthesis that claims to cover everything it needs to cover is probably overconfident. Explicitly naming what remains unknown is a mark of rigor, not weakness.
- **Dont use vendor-commissioned research without noting the sponsorship.** Research commissioned by a company with a financial interest in the findings must be disclosed and treated with additional skepticism.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE RESEARCH QUESTION OR TOPIC
   What does this synthesis need to answer?
   What decision or document will it feed?

2. THE SCOPE
   What market, industry, geography, customer segment, or topic area
   is being researched?
   What is explicitly out of scope?

3. SOURCES ALREADY IDENTIFIED OR AVAILABLE
   What sources do you already have access to?
   What sources are known to be relevant but not yet accessed?

4. TIME AND QUALITY CONSTRAINTS
   How much time is available for the synthesis?
   What level of depth is required (quick scan vs. comprehensive literature review)?

5. THE OUTPUT FORMAT NEEDED
   Who is the audience?
   How will findings be used and presented?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. EXISTING HYPOTHESES
   What does the team currently believe about this topic?
   What should the research confirm or challenge?

7. KNOWN DATA GAPS
   Are there specific questions you already know secondary research
   probably cannot answer, and which will require primary research?

8. SOURCE ACCESS CONSTRAINTS
   Are analyst reports or paywalled sources available?
   Are there particular high-priority sources to prioritize?

9. PREVIOUS DESK RESEARCH
   Has this topic been researched before internally?
   What prior synthesis exists that this builds on?

10. INTEGRATION WITH PRIMARY RESEARCH
    Is this synthesis being designed to directly feed into
    a primary research design? If so, what are the primary research questions?
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Research question: What does existing published research tell us about
the market for restaurant-specific payroll and compliance software?
Specifically: market size, growth rate, buyer behavior, competitive landscape,
key pain points in restaurant payroll, and technology adoption patterns.
Scope: US market, independent and small-chain restaurants (1 to 20 locations),
payroll and compliance software specifically
Sources available: Access to Gartner and Forrester via library,
open access to restaurant industry trade publications (Restaurant Business,
Nation's Restaurant News), public company filings for Toast and Lightspeed,
G2 and Capterra review data for competitor products
Time available: 3 days for synthesis
Output: A 5-page market intelligence brief for the product and leadership team,
feeding into our Series A investor narrative
Known gaps: Specific willingness-to-pay data for our segment is unlikely
to exist in secondary research. Specific workflow patterns are unknown.
These will be covered in primary research.
```

## Output Format

```
1. SYNTHESIS BRIEF HEADER
   Topic, date, author, scope definition, source count, time period covered.

2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (half page)
   The 3 to 5 most important findings from the synthesis.
   What the body of research collectively tells us about this topic.
   The most significant knowledge gap identified.

3. METHODOLOGY AND SOURCE INVENTORY
   How sources were identified and selected.
   Source quality evaluation criteria applied.
   Full bibliography with: source name, publisher, date, methodology notes,
   quality rating (High / Medium / Low), and any bias flags.

4. FINDINGS BY THEME (main body)
   Organized by research theme, not by source.
   For each theme:
   - Headline finding (synthesized view across sources)
   - Supporting evidence (what each relevant source says)
   - Contradictions or tensions (where sources disagree and why)
   - Confidence level: High (multiple strong sources agree) /
     Medium (partial or mixed evidence) / Low (single source or weak methodology)
   - Source date range covered

5. CONVERGENCE AND CONTRADICTION MAP
   A summary table showing where sources agree and where they contradict.
   Explanation of significant contradictions.

6. KNOWLEDGE GAP MAP
   Explicit list of questions this synthesis cannot answer.
   For each gap: why secondary research does not cover it, and
   what research method would be needed to fill it.

7. IMPLICATIONS FOR DECISION OR STRATEGY
   What do the synthesized findings mean for the specific decision
   or document this synthesis is feeding?
   Written as "Because X, we can conclude Y."

8. FULL SOURCE BIBLIOGRAPHY
   All sources with full citation information, access date,
   and quality rating.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a research analyst helping me conduct and synthesize secondary
(desk) research on a market or topic.

Research question or topic: [what this synthesis needs to answer]
Scope: [market, geography, customer segment, topic boundaries]
Sources available: [what you have access to]
Sources to find: [what additional sources should be sought]
Time and depth constraints: [how much time and what level of depth]
Output format and audience: [who will use this and how]
Existing hypotheses: [what the team currently believes]
Known gaps: [questions you already know secondary research will not answer]
Integration with primary research: [is this feeding a primary research design?]

Please produce:
1. A source evaluation framework for assessing quality before including any source
2. A synthesis organized by theme with convergence and contradiction noted
3. Confidence levels for each major finding
4. A contradictions map where credible sources disagree
5. A knowledge gap map explicitly listing what secondary research cannot answer
6. Strategic implications: what the findings mean for the specific decision
7. A full source bibliography with quality ratings

When synthesizing, do not summarize sources one by one.
Integrate across sources to produce insight that no single source contains.
Flag any vendor-commissioned research and treat it with appropriate skepticism.
Note the date of every statistic and flag any data older than 24 months.
```
