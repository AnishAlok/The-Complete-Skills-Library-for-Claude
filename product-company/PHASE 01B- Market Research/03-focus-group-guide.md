# focus-group-guide.md

> **Skill:** Produces a complete focus group moderator guide including a participant screener, session structure, discussion flow, probing questions, and facilitator notes for market research and product validation.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"How do we design and facilitate a focus group that produces rich, honest qualitative insight rather than groupthink and social performance?"* It produces a moderator guide with a screener, warm-up, structured discussion flow, probe questions, and closing protocol. Used for market research, concept testing, brand perception research, and exploring shared experiences among a defined customer group.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is a Focus Group?

A focus group is a moderated group discussion, typically 6 to 10 participants, designed to explore attitudes, perceptions, motivations, and reactions to a topic, concept, product, or message. The defining characteristic is interaction: participants discuss with each other, not just respond to the moderator, producing richer, more socially situated data than individual interviews.

Focus groups are best suited to questions where the social dimension of belief or behavior matters, or where hearing how people articulate and defend views in response to others is valuable.

### When Focus Groups Are the Right Method (and When They Are Not)

**Focus groups work well when:**
- You want to understand shared cultural attitudes or social norms around a topic
- Concept or message testing benefits from social reaction and discussion
- You want to observe how people articulate and justify their views to others
- You are exploring language and vocabulary (how do people talk about this problem?)
- You need to generate a large number of ideas or reactions quickly
- You want to understand the range of perspectives in a defined group

**Focus groups do not work well when:**
- The topic is sensitive or private (individuals will not be honest in groups)
- You need individual behavioral data (group dynamics distort individual reports)
- You want to understand individual decision-making journeys (use in-depth interviews)
- The sample is too small to fill groups and an individual interview series would be more efficient
- Dominant participants are likely to suppress minority views on important topics
- The topic requires expert respondents who are unlikely to interact naturally

### Groupthink and How to Mitigate It

Groupthink is the most serious methodological risk in focus group research. It occurs when the social dynamics of the group suppress individual dissent and produce artificial consensus. Strong personalities dominate, quieter participants suppress unusual views, and the moderator ends up with data that reflects social performance rather than genuine attitudes.

Mitigation strategies:
- **Pre-task:** Ask participants to write down their views before the group discussion begins. This anchors individual positions before social influence takes hold.
- **Anonymous polling:** Use live polling tools to capture individual ratings before group discussion.
- **Round-robin protocol:** Explicitly invite every participant to respond before open discussion begins.
- **Devil's advocate prompts:** Actively introduce the minority view or counterargument to test whether it has real support.
- **Segmented groups:** If dominant vs. subordinate dynamics are predictable (e.g., managers and direct reports, senior executives and junior staff), run separate groups.

### The Moderator's Role

The moderator's job is to create a safe environment for honest sharing, ensure all voices are heard, follow the discussion guide without being enslaved to it, probe beneath surface answers, and prevent dominant personalities from hijacking the session.

The moderator should not:
- Express opinions about the topic under discussion
- Signal approval or disapproval of participant views
- Suggest answers to questions participants are struggling with
- Allow the discussion to be dominated by one or two participants
- Rush through the guide at the expense of depth

The moderator should:
- Stay genuinely curious and neutral
- Use probes consistently and not selectively
- Notice and invite quiet participants
- Challenge gently when responses seem socially mediated rather than genuine
- Keep the group on track without feeling scripted

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Testing brand perception, messaging, or positioning with a defined audience
- Exploring reactions to a new product concept or prototype
- Understanding the shared language and mental models of a customer segment
- Generating hypotheses to test in later quantitative research
- Exploring sensitive social topics where group discussion surfaces shared norms
- Understanding how a customer segment makes decisions together (e.g., buying committee)

Do NOT use when:
- The topic is highly personal or sensitive (use individual interviews)
- You need precise individual behavioral data (use surveys or interviews)
- Participant anonymity is required for honest responses
- You cannot fill a group of 6 to 8 qualified participants on the same topic
- Your research question requires sequential individual decision-making maps
  (use `customer-discovery-interview.md`)

## Dos

- **Do use a screener to recruit participants who are genuinely right for the research.** A group filled by whoever said yes will produce low-quality data. Screen rigorously.
- **Do run a pre-task before the session begins.** Asking participants to write, sketch, or reflect before the session anchors individual views and mitigates groupthink.
- **Do practice active listening and strategic silence.** Long pauses after initial responses often produce the most honest follow-up. Resist the urge to fill silence.
- **Do use projective techniques for sensitive or abstract topics.** "Imagine a person who uses this product. Describe them." surfaces attitudes that direct questioning suppresses.
- **Do have a note-taker separate from the moderator.** The moderator should be entirely focused on the group. All recording and note-taking should be handled by a separate person or captured via recording with consent.
- **Do capture verbatim language.** The exact words participants use to describe problems, motivations, and reactions are data. Paraphrasing in notes loses this.
- **Do plan for a debrief immediately after the session.** The moderator and note-taker should spend 15 minutes capturing key observations and initial interpretations while memory is fresh.
- **Do time-box each section.** Allocate time to each section of the guide. Sessions that spend too long on warm-up leave no time for the substantive discussion.

---

## Donts

- **Dont put the most important questions at the end.** Sessions frequently run long. If the most critical questions are in the final section, they risk being cut short.
- **Dont lead with the concept or stimulus material.** Start by exploring current behaviors and attitudes before showing any concept. Exposure to the concept primes all subsequent responses.
- **Dont allow the discussion to be dominated by one or two participants.** Monitor airtime. Use explicit invitations to quieter participants. If necessary, use round-robin protocols.
- **Dont ask "do you like this?"** Approval questions produce socially mediated answers. Ask instead about specific reactions, what they would do, what it reminds them of, and what is missing.
- **Dont use industry jargon in questions.** Use the language participants use, not the language your team uses.
- **Dont mix participant types who will create power dynamics.** Bosses and direct reports, experts and novices, highly vocal and highly reserved participant types should be in separate groups when possible.
- **Dont record without consent.** Always obtain explicit, documented consent for recording before the session begins. Clarify how recordings will be used and stored.
- **Dont skip the screener.** A focus group with the wrong participants produces data that looks credible but is worthless. The screener is not optional.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. RESEARCH OBJECTIVES
   What does this focus group need to answer?
   Stated as open research questions.

2. PARTICIPANT PROFILE
   Who should be in this group?
   What are the key characteristics: industry, role, behavior, demographics,
   product usage, attitudes?

3. TOPIC OR STIMULUS MATERIAL
   What is the topic being discussed?
   Will any concept, product, prototype, message, or ad be shown?
   If so, what and in what format?

4. SESSION LENGTH
   How long is the session? Typical focus groups run 90 to 120 minutes.

5. FORMAT
   In-person or online? This affects logistics, interaction dynamics,
   and exercise possibilities.
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. NUMBER OF GROUPS
   How many focus groups are planned?
   Are there different participant segments who will be in separate groups?

7. KNOWN HYPOTHESES OR TENSIONS
   What does the team currently believe about the topic?
   What views need to be probed or challenged?

8. PRIOR RESEARCH
   Has there been prior qualitative or quantitative research on this topic?
   What is already known that this group should build on?

9. PROJECTIVE TECHNIQUES PREFERENCE
   Are there specific exercises (collage, sentence completion, metaphor elicitation)
   that should be included?

10. REPORTING FORMAT
    Who will receive the findings and in what format?
    This shapes how detailed the note-taking guide needs to be.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Research objectives:
1. How do independent restaurant operators currently think about and describe their
   payroll and compliance burden?
2. What language do they use to describe the pain?
3. How do they react to the concept of automated tip pooling and compliance reporting?
4. What would need to be true for them to consider switching from their current approach?
Participant profile: Independent restaurant owners or operators with 1 to 3 locations,
currently managing payroll themselves or with a bookkeeper, not currently using
dedicated restaurant payroll software
Topic: First exploring current state, then showing a 2-minute product concept video
Session length: 90 minutes
Format: Online via Zoom
Number of groups: 2 groups of 6 to 8 participants each
Known hypotheses: We believe the pain is real but operators may not associate it
with software as the solution. They may be skeptical of complexity.
Prior research: 8 customer interviews completed. Common themes: time burden,
compliance anxiety, distrust of generic payroll tools not built for restaurants.
```

## Output Format

```
1. PARTICIPANT SCREENER
   Introduction and purpose statement (for recruiter use)
   Qualification questions with routing:
   - Include criteria with "continue" routing
   - Exclude criteria with "thank and release" routing
   Demographic collection questions
   Scheduling and logistics information

2. MODERATOR GUIDE HEADER
   Study title, date, moderator name, note-taker name
   Research objectives (brief, for moderator reference)
   Session logistics: platform, recording setup, consent process
   Time allocation per section

3. WELCOME AND CONSENT (5 to 10 minutes)
   Welcome script
   Ground rules for the group
   Recording consent language
   Confidentiality and incentive confirmation

4. WARM-UP (10 to 15 minutes)
   Icebreaker questions (non-threatening, topic-adjacent)
   Brief participant introductions
   Purpose: establish comfort, begin topic orientation

5. PRE-TASK PROTOCOL (if applicable)
   Instructions for individual reflection before group discussion
   Worksheet or prompt

6. CURRENT STATE EXPLORATION (20 to 30 minutes)
   Open questions about current behaviors and attitudes
   Probes for depth
   No exposure to concept or stimulus yet

7. CONCEPT OR STIMULUS INTRODUCTION (if applicable)
   Precise instructions for presenting the concept
   What to say before showing it
   How to show it (order, time, format)

8. CONCEPT REACTION DISCUSSION (25 to 35 minutes)
   Initial unguided reaction questions
   Structured exploration questions
   Probes for positives, negatives, confusions, missing elements
   Projective or indirect questions if needed

9. PRIORITIZATION OR DECISION EXERCISES (10 to 15 minutes)
   Structured exercises: ranking, voting, card sorting
   Analysis of group agreements and disagreements

10. CLOSING AND DEBRIEF (10 minutes)
    Summary and member check: "Does this reflect what you said?"
    Final open question: "What have we not asked that matters?"
    Thank you and incentive confirmation

11. POST-SESSION DEBRIEF TEMPLATE
    Key themes from this session
    Surprises or unexpected findings
    Quotes to capture verbatim
    Tensions or disagreements observed
    Follow-up questions for next session
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a qualitative research specialist helping me design a complete focus group
moderator guide and participant screener.

Research objectives: [the 3 to 5 questions this focus group must answer]
Participant profile: [who should be in this group, specific criteria]
Topic or stimulus material: [what is being discussed and what will be shown, if anything]
Session length: [90 minutes / 120 minutes]
Format: [in-person / online]
Number of groups: [how many sessions and any segment differences between them]
Known hypotheses or tensions: [what the team believes and what needs to be tested]
Prior research context: [what is already known from previous research]
Projective techniques: [any specific exercises to include]

Please produce:
1. A complete participant screener with qualification criteria, routing logic,
   and demographic collection
2. A moderator guide with all sections: welcome, warm-up, current state exploration,
   concept introduction (if applicable), concept reaction, exercises, and close
3. Time allocations for each section
4. Probe questions for each main discussion section
5. At least one projective or indirect technique appropriate to the topic
6. A round-robin protocol for ensuring all participant voices are heard
7. A post-session debrief template

Write all moderator-facing language in second person: "Ask the group..."
Write all participant-facing questions in the language participants will hear.
Flag any sections where groupthink is particularly risky and note the mitigation.
```
