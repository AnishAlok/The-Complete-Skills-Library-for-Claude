# industry-report-summary.md

> **Skill:** Extracts, structures, and synthesizes the most strategically relevant findings from analyst industry reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, McKinsey, and others) into an actionable summary that decision-makers can use without reading the full report.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"What does this analyst report actually tell us that matters for our business, stripped of the filler?"* It produces a structured summary that extracts key findings, market data, and strategic implications from analyst and industry reports into a format decision-makers can consume in 5 minutes. Used for board prep, investor narrative building, strategic planning inputs, and keeping leadership current on market intelligence.

---

## Topic Explanation

### Why Analyst Reports Need Summarization

Industry analyst reports from firms like Gartner, Forrester, IDC, McKinsey, Bain, and others are among the most credible sources of market data available. They are also frequently:

- 40 to 150 pages long, with the most important data buried on page 67
- Written in dense, jargon-heavy language optimized for analyst credibility, not reader clarity
- Padded with methodology sections, background context, and qualification discussions
- Structured to serve all readers rather than the specific strategic question you have
- Full of caveats that make it hard to extract clean, usable numbers

The skill of extracting value from an analyst report is not reading comprehension. It is strategic filtering: identifying which findings matter for your specific context, extracting the numbers and claims that are credible and relevant, and building a synthesis that connects the report's findings to your specific decisions.

### Types of Analyst Reports and What They Contain

**Market sizing and forecast reports (IDC, Gartner, Forrester)**
Contain: TAM estimates, growth rate projections (CAGR), segment breakdowns, regional splits, technology adoption curves.
Best for: Market sizing inputs for investor narratives, strategic planning context, pricing benchmarks.
Watch for: Different category definitions between firms that make the same market look very different in size.

**Magic Quadrant and Wave reports (Gartner, Forrester)**
Contain: Vendor evaluations on two dimensions (completeness of vision vs. ability to execute for Gartner; current offering vs. strategy for Forrester). Customer feedback-informed ratings.
Best for: Understanding how analysts perceive the competitive landscape, which vendors are seen as leaders vs. challengers.
Watch for: MQ and Wave methodology has paid participation elements. Vendors who pay for the process are included; those who do not may be excluded.

**Buyer behavior and adoption research (Forrester, Gartner, McKinsey)**
Contain: Survey data on how buyers make decisions, what they prioritize, what percentage have adopted various technologies, what barriers to adoption exist.
Best for: Sales positioning, messaging development, objection handling, understanding the buying process.
Watch for: Sample bias. Research on "enterprise buyers" may not apply to your SMB or mid-market segment.

**Technology and trend reports (McKinsey, Deloitte, PwC, a16z)**
Contain: Analysis of technology trends, market shifts, sector-specific disruption, emerging competitive dynamics.
Best for: Strategic planning context, trend signal analysis, board-level market narrative.
Watch for: Consultant reports often have a service-selling agenda. McKinsey reports on digital transformation tend to conclude that companies need more digital transformation consulting.

**Sector-specific research (trade association reports, specialized firms)**
Contain: Sector-specific data that generalist firms do not cover. Often includes practitioner surveys and operational benchmarks.
Best for: Deep vertical intelligence for industries your product serves.
Watch for: Methodology quality varies more than major analyst firms. Evaluate rigorously.

### What to Extract vs. What to Skip

**Always extract:**
- Market size and growth rate numbers (with dates and definitions)
- Customer survey findings on priorities, pain points, and adoption
- Technology adoption percentages and forecasts
- Vendor rankings or ratings that affect your competitive narrative
- Direct quotes that are compelling enough to cite in investor or board materials
- Predictions for 3 to 5 year horizon that affect your product bets

**Usually skip:**
- Extensive methodology sections (summarize in one line: "based on X survey of Y respondents")
- Background context chapters that set up the report's thesis
- Regional breakdowns that do not apply to your geographic focus
- Vendor profiles of companies you do not compete with
- Footnotes and qualification language (unless they substantially change the meaning of a finding)

### How to Handle Conflicting Data Across Reports

It is common for two analyst firms to report different market size estimates for the same market. This happens because:
- Different category definitions (one includes adjacent segments, one does not)
- Different survey methodologies
- Different geographic scopes
- Different time periods
- Different inclusion criteria for what counts as "the market"

When reports conflict, the right approach is not to pick the number you prefer. It is to:
1. Document both numbers with their source and methodology
2. Explain why the definitions may differ
3. Use a range rather than a point estimate
4. Note which definition is more relevant to your specific market position

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- A key analyst report has been published and leadership needs to understand its implications quickly
- Building the market context section of an investor pitch and needing to cite authoritative data
- Preparing a board presentation and needing to reference current market intelligence
- Running strategic planning and needing to synthesize 3 to 5 reports on the same market
- A sales team member asks: "Can you give me the most important stats from that Gartner report?"
- A new executive needs to be briefed on the state of the market using published research

Do NOT use when:
- The report does not yet exist and you need primary research (use `primary-research-plan.md`)
- You need a synthesis of many secondary sources, not just analyst reports (use `desk-research-synthesis.md`)
- You need a full competitive analysis (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)

## Dos

- **Do read the executive summary and conclusion first.** Analyst reports bury their thesis in the introduction and repeat it in the conclusion. Read those two sections before diving into the detail.
- **Do extract the specific numbers and their precise definitions.** "The market will grow at 14% CAGR" is only useful if you also know: which market, which geography, what time period, and how the market was defined.
- **Do note the survey methodology for any survey-based findings.** "67% of buyers say X" is very different data depending on whether it came from 50 self-selected respondents or 2,000 stratified surveyed buyers.
- **Do flag findings that appear to conflict with other sources.** If Gartner says the market is $4.2B and IDC says it is $6.8B, document both and explain the likely reason for the difference.
- **Do translate findings into implications for your specific business.** "The market is growing at 18% CAGR" is a finding. "At 18% CAGR, the segment we are targeting will double in size by 2028, supporting our expansion thesis" is an implication.
- **Do note the publication date prominently.** Analyst reports from 2021 on a fast-moving market may be significantly wrong. Always make the date visible.
- **Do pull the best quotable data points for investor and board use.** Some findings are not just analytically useful but rhetorically powerful. Flag these for easy retrieval.

---

## Donts

- **Dont cite a market size figure without knowing how the market was defined.** Two reports can both be technically correct about a "cloud security market" that they define very differently.
- **Dont present analyst estimates as facts.** They are informed estimates with significant uncertainty. Present them as estimates: "According to Gartner's 2024 estimate..."
- **Dont reproduce large portions of the report verbatim.** Analyst reports are copyrighted. Extract findings in your own words, cite the source, and do not reproduce paragraphs or tables directly.
- **Dont strip out the caveats that change the meaning of a finding.** If Gartner says adoption will reach 60% by 2027 "among enterprises with over $1B revenue," that caveat matters enormously if you serve SMBs.
- **Dont use findings from paid-placement reports without noting the sponsorship.** Vendor-commissioned or vendor-sponsored analyst reports should be treated with additional skepticism and disclosed.
- **Dont summarize every section equally.** The sections relevant to your market, customer, and technology deserve more depth. Background sections and irrelevant verticals deserve a single sentence or nothing.
- **Dont assume the analyst's category definition matches your market position.** Gartner may classify your product in a category that does not match how your customers think about it. Note any mismatch.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE REPORT OR REPORTS TO SUMMARIZE
   Name of the report, publisher, publication date.
   Either the report itself or key sections you have access to.

2. YOUR SPECIFIC CONTEXT
   What market are you in? Who are your customers?
   What strategic questions are you trying to answer?
   This determines which findings are relevant to extract.

3. THE PURPOSE OF THE SUMMARY
   Investor pitch? Board presentation? Strategic planning input?
   Internal team briefing? Sales enablement?
   Purpose shapes depth, format, and emphasis.

4. THE AUDIENCE
   Who will read this summary?
   What do they already know and what do they need to understand?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
5. RELATED REPORTS TO COMPARE AGAINST
   Are there other reports on the same market that should be
   compared or reconciled with this one?

6. SPECIFIC SECTIONS OR FINDINGS TO PRIORITIZE
   Are there specific questions this report should answer?
   Are there specific data points you are hoping to find?

7. PRIOR SUMMARIES
   Has this report or a prior edition been summarized before?
   What has changed since the last summary?

8. DOWNSTREAM DOCUMENTS
   What documents will this summary feed?
   An investor deck? A strategy doc? A board update?
   Knowing the downstream use helps prioritize what to extract.
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Report: Forrester's "The State of Cloud Security, 2024" (hypothetical example)
My context: We build a cloud security compliance tool for mid-market companies.
Our customers are primarily IT directors and CISOs at companies with 200 to 2,000 employees.
We are pre-Series A and will use this summary for our investor deck and board materials.
Purpose: Build credible market context section for Series A pitch deck.
Audience: Series A VCs who will scrutinize market size claims.
Specific questions: What is the market size and growth rate? What are the top buyer priorities?
What percentage of mid-market companies have adopted cloud security tooling?
What are the barriers to adoption? Which vendors are seen as leaders?
Downstream use: Market context section of Series A pitch deck.
Related reports: We also have the IDC cloud security market forecast and
Gartner's Magic Quadrant for cloud security posture management.
```

## Output Format

```
1. REPORT METADATA
   Title, publisher, publication date, report type, methodology summary
   (sample size, survey method, geography covered), access date.
   Classification: Public / Paywalled / Commissioned (note if vendor-sponsored).

2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (3 to 5 sentences)
   The single most important finding or thesis of the report.
   The headline market data point.
   The most relevant implication for your specific business.

3. KEY MARKET DATA EXTRACTED
   Market size: figure, definition, geography, time period, source.
   Growth rate: CAGR, period covered, segment scope.
   Adoption data: technology or category adoption percentages.
   Segment breakdowns relevant to your market.
   Each figure with full context: what exactly was measured, how, and when.

4. BUYER BEHAVIOR FINDINGS
   What buyers prioritize in vendor selection.
   Barriers to adoption.
   Decision-making process insights.
   Budget and spending trends.
   Relevant for: sales positioning, messaging, overcoming objections.

5. COMPETITIVE AND VENDOR LANDSCAPE FINDINGS
   How analysts characterize the vendor landscape.
   Any vendor ratings or rankings relevant to your competitive positioning.
   Analyst characterization of market maturity (emerging / growth / mature / declining).

6. TREND AND FORECAST FINDINGS
   3 to 5 year market forecasts most relevant to your planning horizon.
   Technology adoption curves.
   Emerging dynamics the analyst believes will shape the market.

7. CONFLICTS AND LIMITATIONS
   Any findings that conflict with other sources, with explanation.
   Methodology limitations that affect how much weight to place on findings.
   Category definition differences that affect market size comparability.
   Date limitations: findings likely to be outdated given market velocity.

8. QUOTABLE DATA POINTS
   The 5 to 10 most compelling, citable statistics and findings.
   Formatted for easy use in presentations, investor materials, or sales collateral.
   Each with precise citation format: "According to [publisher], [year]..."

9. STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS FOR YOUR BUSINESS
   What these findings mean specifically for your product, market positioning,
   or strategic decisions. Written as "Because X, we should consider Y."

10. RELATED REPORTS AND FURTHER READING
    Other reports referenced or recommended for additional depth.
    Any gaps in this report that other sources should fill.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a market intelligence analyst helping me extract and structure
the most strategically valuable findings from an analyst industry report.

Report to summarize: [report title, publisher, publication date]
Report content: [paste relevant sections or describe what you have access to]
My context: [your market, product, and customer type]
Purpose of summary: [investor pitch / board presentation / strategic planning / team briefing]
Audience: [who will read this and what they need to understand]
Specific questions to answer: [the 3 to 5 questions this report should answer for you]
Related reports to compare: [other reports that should be reconciled with this one]
Downstream use: [what document will this summary feed]

Please produce:
1. Report metadata including methodology summary and quality assessment
2. An executive summary of the report's thesis and most important finding
3. All key market data extracted with full context: definition, geography, period, methodology
4. Buyer behavior and decision-making findings
5. Competitive and vendor landscape findings
6. Trend and forecast findings relevant to my planning horizon
7. Conflicts and limitations section noting any data quality or recency issues
8. The 5 to 10 most quotable, citable data points formatted for presentation use
9. Strategic implications for my specific business

Do not reproduce large sections of the report verbatim.
Cite all findings with precise attribution: publisher and year.
Flag any finding where the report's category definition may not match
my specific market position.
Note prominently if any key finding is from data older than 24 months.
```
