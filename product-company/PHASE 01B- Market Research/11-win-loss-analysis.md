# win-loss-analysis.md

> **Skill:** Designs and executes a win/loss analysis program including interview frameworks, deal tagging systems, synthesis methods, and reporting formats that reveal why you win and lose competitive deals.

---

## TLDR

This skill helps you answer: *"Why do we win when we win, why do we lose when we lose, and what should we change?"* It produces a win/loss interview guide, a deal tagging taxonomy, a synthesis framework for identifying patterns across deals, and a reporting format for sharing findings with product, sales, and leadership teams. Used to improve win rates, sharpen positioning, prioritize product investments, and build better competitive intelligence.

---

## Topic Explanation

### What Is Win/Loss Analysis?

Win/loss analysis is a systematic program for understanding the factors that cause sales deals to succeed or fail. It involves structured interviews with buyers immediately after a deal closes (or is lost), combined with analysis of patterns across many deals to identify systematic drivers of outcome.

Win/loss analysis is one of the highest-value market research investments a company can make because it connects directly to revenue. Unlike user research that identifies feature preferences or market research that estimates opportunity size, win/loss analysis reveals the specific factors in your sales process, product, pricing, and positioning that are costing you or generating revenue right now.

### Why Win/Loss Analysis Is Often Done Poorly

Most companies do some form of win/loss tracking but few get full value from it. Common failure modes:

**Failure mode 1: Relying only on sales team input.** Sales reps record loss reasons in CRM, but these are often self-serving ("lost on price" is much more common in CRM data than it is in actual buyer interviews), incomplete, or biased toward factors the rep could not control.

**Failure mode 2: Not interviewing buyers directly.** The only source of accurate win/loss information is the buyer. Everything else is a proxy.

**Failure mode 3: Not interviewing recently enough.** Buyer memory of decision factors degrades rapidly. Interviews conducted more than 3 weeks after a decision are significantly less reliable than those conducted within 10 days.

**Failure mode 4: Interviewing wins but not losses.** Most companies are more comfortable analyzing wins. Losses are more uncomfortable but more instructive.

**Failure mode 5: Collecting data without synthesizing it.** Deal-by-deal notes without cross-deal pattern analysis produces anecdotes, not strategy.

### Win Interviews vs. Loss Interviews

**Win interviews**
Goal: Understand why you won and what made you better than the alternatives.
Focus: What was the decisive factor? What almost made them choose someone else?
Key insight: Wins reveal your actual differentiation, which may be different from your claimed differentiation.

**Loss interviews**
Goal: Understand why you lost and what would have had to be different for you to win.
Focus: When did the decision shift? What did the chosen competitor do better? Was there anything you could have done?
Key insight: Losses reveal your actual weaknesses, not the weaknesses you think you have.

**No-decision interviews**
Goal: Understand why the buyer decided not to buy from anyone.
Focus: What changed? Was it budget, priority, internal politics, or genuine doubt about the category?
Key insight: No-decisions often reveal whether your market timing is right and whether buyers genuinely experience the problem as urgent.

### The Buyer Interview vs. The Sales Debrief

These are not the same and should not be confused:

**Sales debrief:** A conversation with your own sales rep about what happened in the deal. Useful for process improvement. Not useful for accurate buyer insight because reps have limited access to the buyer's true decision rationale.

**Buyer interview:** A conversation directly with the person who made the decision. More accurate, more uncomfortable, and far more valuable.

The gold standard is to conduct buyer interviews directly. Many companies use a third party for loss interviews specifically because buyers are more candid with a neutral third party than with a vendor who just lost their business.

### Deal Tagging for Systematic Analysis

Individual interviews produce rich qualitative data. But the strategic value comes from pattern analysis across many deals. This requires a consistent tagging system that categorizes deals along dimensions that can be tracked over time.

Standard deal tagging dimensions:
- Win / Loss / No decision
- Primary loss reason (product gap / price / competitor strength / internal politics / sales execution / timing)
- Competitor won against (or displaced in a win)
- Customer segment (firmographic characteristics)
- Deal size
- Sales rep
- Sales cycle length
- Stage lost or won

With consistent tagging, you can answer questions like: "Do we lose more often to Competitor X in enterprise deals than in mid-market deals?" or "Is our win rate improving in Q2 vs. Q1 after the new feature launch?"

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Win rate is flat or declining and the team does not know why
- You are losing to a specific competitor more frequently and need to understand what they are doing
- You have launched a new product or feature and want to know if it is affecting win rates
- The sales team and product team have conflicting hypotheses about why deals are lost
- You are preparing a competitive positioning refresh and need real deal data to validate it
- A board or investor asks about win rates and competitive dynamics
- You want to build an ongoing intelligence capability, not just a one-time analysis

Do NOT use when:
- You need a competitive landscape overview (use `competitor-landscape-map.md`)
- You need to understand why customers are churning after purchase (use `churn-interview.md`)
- You do not yet have enough deals to analyze (less than 20 closed deals makes pattern analysis difficult)

## Dos

- **Do interview buyers directly, not just your sales team.** The buyer's perspective is the only accurate source of why the decision went the way it did.
- **Do interview within 10 days of the decision.** Memory of decision factors is most accurate immediately after the decision. Every week of delay degrades quality.
- **Do conduct loss interviews with a neutral third party when possible.** Buyers are significantly more candid about why they chose a competitor when they are not talking directly to the vendor they rejected.
- **Do ask open questions before closed ones.** "Walk me through your decision process" before "Was price a factor?" Open questions produce the real story. Closed questions produce yes/no confirmation.
- **Do probe beneath the stated reason.** "We lost on price" is almost never the complete story. Price is often a proxy for "we did not believe the value justified the price," which is a positioning problem, not a pricing problem.
- **Do analyze wins as rigorously as losses.** Wins reveal what you are actually good at (which may surprise you) and where you are vulnerable in future deals.
- **Do tag every deal consistently.** A consistent tagging system is what transforms individual interview notes into strategic patterns.
- **Do share findings broadly.** Win/loss insights are most valuable when they reach product, marketing, and leadership, not just sales. Build a regular reporting cadence.

---

## Donts

- **Dont rely on CRM loss reason data as your primary source.** CRM loss reasons are notoriously inaccurate. "Lost on price" is entered because it is quick and does not require the rep to admit to execution failures.
- **Dont only interview losses.** Win interviews are equally important and often more neglected. Understanding why you win is as strategically important as understanding why you lose.
- **Dont interview deals that closed more than 3 to 4 weeks ago.** Memory degrades fast. Prioritize recency.
- **Dont send the account executive to interview a buyer who just rejected them.** The buyer will not be candid and the rep will not get honest feedback. Use a neutral interviewer.
- **Dont conflate "deal lost" with "product lost."** Some deals are lost due to sales execution, relationship factors, or internal politics that have nothing to do with product. Distinguish these clearly in your analysis.
- **Dont present findings as anecdotes without quantification.** "A customer said our onboarding was too complex" is an anecdote. "In 7 of 11 loss interviews, onboarding complexity was cited as a significant factor" is a finding.
- **Dont skip the no-decision deals.** Buyers who decided not to purchase from anyone often reveal whether your market is ready and whether buyers genuinely believe the problem is urgent enough to solve.
- **Dont make win/loss a one-time project.** A single round of win/loss interviews produces a point-in-time snapshot. The value compounds over time with consistent tracking and quarterly synthesis.

## Ideal Inputs

### Required
```
1. THE DEALS TO ANALYZE
   A set of recently closed deals (won, lost, and no-decision if available).
   For each deal: company name, size, segment, outcome, date closed,
   competitor involved (if known), sales rep.

2. YOUR CURRENT HYPOTHESES
   What does the team currently believe about why you win and lose?
   These are the assumptions the analysis should confirm or challenge.

3. THE PRIMARY QUESTION TO ANSWER
   Is this analysis driven by a specific concern (e.g., "why are we losing
   to Competitor X?") or is it a general win/loss program?

4. INTERVIEW ACCESS
   Will you be interviewing buyers directly?
   Who at the buying organization made the decision?
   Is there a neutral third party available to conduct loss interviews?

5. WHAT YOU WILL DO WITH FINDINGS
   Who will receive the output?
   What decisions will it inform?
```

### Optional but Valuable
```
6. CURRENT CRM LOSS REASONS
   What do your sales reps currently enter as loss reasons?
   This reveals where sales perception and buyer reality may diverge.

7. DEAL SIZE AND SEGMENT BREAKDOWN
   Are deals distributed across different segments or sizes?
   This affects whether segment-specific patterns can be identified.

8. PRIOR WIN/LOSS DATA
   Has win/loss analysis been done before?
   What did it find and what has changed since then?

9. COMPETITIVE INTELLIGENCE
   What do you already know about the competitors you lose to?
   This helps design questions to probe specific competitive dimensions.

10. PRODUCT CHANGES
    Are there specific product launches, pricing changes, or positioning
    updates whose impact on win rates you want to measure?
```

### Example of a Strong Input Prompt
```
Deals to analyze: 24 closed deals from Q1 2025:
14 wins, 8 losses, 2 no-decisions.
Primary competitor in loss deals: Drata (5 of 8 losses), Vanta (2 of 8), lost to status quo (1).
Segments: Mix of fintech startups (18 deals) and healthcare startups (6 deals).
Deal size range: $8K to $48K ACV.
Our hypotheses: We think we lose to Drata on integrations and to Vanta on brand.
We think we win on speed to compliance and customer support quality.
Primary question: Are our hypotheses correct, and is there something we are missing?
Interview access: We can reach buyers via email. Loss interviews will be done by
our VP of Customer Success who was not involved in the deals.
CRM loss reasons entered: 4x "lost on price," 2x "competitor features," 2x "timing."
We suspect "lost on price" is often inaccurate.
Prior analysis: None. This is our first structured win/loss program.
```

## Output Format

```
1. WIN/LOSS PROGRAM DESIGN (if setting up a program)
   Interview cadence and timing recommendations.
   Who conducts which types of interviews.
   Deal tagging taxonomy with definitions.
   CRM integration and tracking approach.

2. WIN INTERVIEW GUIDE
   Welcome and framing script.
   Decision process exploration questions.
   Competitive evaluation questions.
   Value perception questions.
   Satisfaction and risk questions.
   Probes for each section.

3. LOSS INTERVIEW GUIDE
   Welcome and framing script (neutral, non-defensive tone).
   Decision process exploration.
   When the decision shifted and why.
   What the winning competitor did better.
   What you could have done differently.
   Whether the door is open in the future.
   Probes for each section.

4. NO-DECISION INTERVIEW GUIDE
   What changed that caused the decision to be deferred.
   Whether the problem was real and urgent.
   What would need to be true for them to reevaluate.

5. DEAL TAGGING TAXONOMY
   Standard tags for: outcome, primary reason, competitor, segment,
   deal size, sales cycle length, stage lost/won.
   Definitions for each tag to ensure consistent entry.

6. POST-INTERVIEW SYNTHESIS TEMPLATE (per deal)
   Key factors in the decision.
   The moment the decision shifted.
   What the competitor did that we did not.
   What we could have done differently.
   Product gaps mentioned.
   Pricing and value perception.
   Sales process factors.
   Tags for this deal.

7. CROSS-DEAL PATTERN ANALYSIS TEMPLATE
   Win rate by segment, competitor, deal size.
   Most common win factors with frequency count.
   Most common loss factors with frequency count.
   Competitor-specific loss patterns.
   Sales process vs. product vs. pricing vs. positioning breakdown.
   Hypothesis scorecard: confirmed / disconfirmed / unclear.

8. FINDINGS REPORT FORMAT
   Executive summary: win rate, top win factors, top loss factors.
   Competitive findings: what each key competitor does better and worse.
   Product implications: gaps mentioned in loss interviews.
   Positioning implications: where our messaging is landing and where it is not.
   Sales process implications: execution factors in wins and losses.
   Recommended actions: the 3 to 5 highest-impact changes to make.
```

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a win/loss analysis specialist helping me design and execute a
program to understand why we win and lose competitive deals.

Deals to analyze: [list of recent closed deals with outcome, competitor, segment, size]
Current hypotheses: [what the team believes about win/loss factors]
Primary question: [the specific question this analysis must answer]
Interview access: [who can be interviewed and who will conduct the interviews]
CRM loss reasons currently recorded: [what reps enter and whether it seems accurate]
Prior analysis: [any previous win/loss work and what it found]
Competitive context: [what you know about the competitors appearing in losses]
What findings will inform: [product roadmap / positioning / sales training / board update]

Please produce:
1. A win interview guide with decision process, competitive evaluation,
   and value perception sections plus probes for each
2. A loss interview guide with a neutral, non-defensive tone and probes
   for understanding the moment the decision shifted
3. A no-decision interview guide
4. A deal tagging taxonomy with definitions for consistent CRM entry
5. A per-deal synthesis template
6. A cross-deal pattern analysis template for identifying systematic factors
7. A findings report format for sharing with product, sales, and leadership

Write interview questions that ask about the decision process, not about
our product's features. Open questions before closed ones throughout.
Include a note on how to handle the emotional dynamics of loss interviews
where buyers may be uncomfortable giving direct feedback.
```
