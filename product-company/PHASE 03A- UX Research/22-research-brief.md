# research-brief.md

> **Skill:** Produces a concise research brief that aligns stakeholders on the purpose, scope, and expected outputs of a research initiative before it begins, preventing scope creep and ensuring findings will be actionable.

---

## TLDR

A research brief is a 1 to 2 page document that communicates the "what, why, and so what" of a research initiative to stakeholders who do not need or want to read the full research plan. It covers: the question being answered, why it matters now, the method and participants, timeline, and the decisions the output will inform. Used for stakeholder alignment before research begins.

---

## Topic Explanation

### Research Brief vs. Research Plan

**Research plan:** The operational document. Detailed methodology, participant criteria, session structure, timeline, roles. Primarily for the research team.

**Research brief:** The alignment document. Distills the plan into what stakeholders need to know to provide input, resources, and buy-in. Primarily for product managers, designers, and leadership.

Both documents should exist. The brief is produced alongside or shortly after the plan and shared with stakeholders for review before execution begins.

### Why Research Briefs Prevent Common Failures

Three common research failures that a well-designed brief prevents:

**Scope creep:** Stakeholders add questions mid-study. The brief establishes agreed scope upfront, making scope additions a deliberate decision rather than drift.

**Unused findings:** Research that stakeholders do not feel ownership over is less likely to be acted on. A brief that stakeholders co-created or reviewed builds ownership.

**Wrong questions answered:** Without a brief, researchers sometimes answer questions the team did not need answered. The brief creates a shared agreement on the actual question.

### The One-Page Discipline

Research briefs should be one to two pages maximum. This constraint forces prioritization. If a research initiative cannot be described in two pages in a way that motivates stakeholder support, either the research is not well-scoped or its strategic importance is not clear.

---

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Starting any research study that involves stakeholders outside the research team
- A product team needs to understand what research is happening and when to expect findings
- Requesting resources, tools, or participant access from outside the research team
- Leadership needs to be briefed on upcoming research before approving it

---

## Output Format

```
1. BRIEF HEADER
   Study name, date, lead researcher, product area, status (draft/approved).

2. THE QUESTION (2 to 3 sentences)
   The specific question this research will answer.
   Why we do not already know the answer.

3. WHY THIS MATTERS NOW (2 to 3 sentences)
   The product or business context making this question urgent.
   The cost of not knowing.

4. WHAT WE WILL DO
   Research method in plain language.
   Number of participants and who they are.
   Approximate session format.

5. WHAT WE WILL NOT DO
   Out-of-scope questions.
   Why these are being excluded.

6. THE DECISION THIS INFORMS
   Specifically: what will change based on what we find.
   Who makes that decision and when.

7. TIMELINE
   3 to 5 key dates: research start, data collection, findings delivery.

8. WHAT WE NEED FROM YOU
   Any stakeholder actions required: participant introductions,
   design materials, prototype access, question input.

9. WHAT YOU WILL RECEIVE
   Output format and distribution plan.
   How to engage with findings.
```

---

## Starter Prompt

```
You are a UX researcher writing a research brief for stakeholder alignment.

Research question: [the specific question being answered]
Business or product context: [why this matters now]
Method and participants: [brief description of the approach]
Out-of-scope questions: [what is explicitly excluded]
Decision being informed: [what will change based on findings]
Timeline: [key dates]
Stakeholder needs: [what you need from them]
Output: [what you will deliver and when]

Produce a concise 1 to 2 page research brief in plain language.
Avoid research jargon. Write for a product manager or designer
who is not a researcher.
```
